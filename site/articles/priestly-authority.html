What Authority Do Priests Actually Have?

The specific, verse-by-verse case

A Protestant objects: “Jesus could calm storms and raise the dead — priests can’t do that. The delegation wasn’t universal.”

⁂

You are right, and we have fixed it. An earlier version of our authority model asserted that Christ had authority over ALL domains and then delegated ALL of it to the Apostles. That is a gigantic jump. Jesus calmed storms, walked on water, and raised the dead. No Catholic has ever claimed priests can do those things. The old model was sloppy — it captured the structure of delegation without capturing its specificity.

The Catholic claim is not “priests can do everything Jesus did.” It is: “Jesus specifically delegated THESE particular authorities, and we can cite chapter and verse for each one.” The delegation is specific, not universal. Let us trace each one.

❅

What Protestants Already Accept

Before we get to the contested delegations, it is worth noting that Protestants already accept two of them. The disagreement is not about WHETHER Jesus delegated authority. It is about WHICH authorities and HOW FAR the chain extends.

1. Teaching — Mt 28:19-20

Ecumenical “Go therefore and make disciples of all nations… teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you.”

Virtually all Christians accept that Jesus commissioned the Apostles to teach. The Great Commission is shared ground. The disagreement is about whether this teaching authority transfers through apostolic succession to bishops and priests, or whether it belongs to every believer.

Succession required? No — even without apostolic succession, the command to teach stands.

2. Baptism — Mt 28:19

Ecumenical “Baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.”

Nearly all Christians practice baptism. The disputes are about mode (immersion vs. sprinkling), subjects (infants vs. believers), and effect (regenerative vs. symbolic) — not about whether Jesus instituted it.

Succession required? No. Catholics accept baptism by anyone, even non-Christians, if done with proper form and intention (CCC §1256).

If you are a Protestant reading this, you already accept two of the eight specific delegations. The question is not whether Jesus delegated authority. It is whether the remaining six delegations are real, and whether they extend beyond the Apostles.
⁂

The Contested Delegations

These are the delegations that Catholics and Orthodox accept but most Protestants contest. Each one has a specific Scripture verse. The question is how to read that verse.

3. Forgiving Sins — Jn 20:21-23

Catholic + Orthodox “Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you retain the sins of any, they are retained.”

Catholic reading: Jesus delegates His authority to forgive sins to the Apostles. The “retaining” clause implies judgment — the Apostles must hear the sin to decide whether to forgive or retain. This is the basis for sacramental confession.

Protestant reading: A general commission to proclaim the gospel of forgiveness. When Christians share the gospel and someone repents, God forgives. The Apostles are messengers, not judges.

Key distinction: Even under the Catholic reading, priests forgive in Christ’s name, not by their own power. The priest is an instrument, not the source. God alone forgives — the question is whether He established a sacramental channel for doing so.

Succession required? Yes — without succession, this authority died with the Apostles.

4. The Eucharist — Lk 22:19

Catholic + Orthodox “Do this in remembrance of me.”

Catholic reading: “Do this” is a command to re-present the sacrifice of Calvary. The bread and wine become Christ’s Body and Blood (transubstantiation). This requires an ordained priest acting in persona Christi.

Protestant reading: “Do this” is a command to share a memorial meal. The bread and wine are symbols (or, for Lutherans, Christ is present “in, with, and under” them). No special ordination is required.

Note: The delegation itself is not disputed. Everyone agrees Jesus said “do this.” The dispute is about the NATURE of what He commanded — sacrifice or memorial.

Succession required? Yes, under the Catholic reading (requires ordained priest). No, under the Protestant reading.

5. Binding and Loosing — Mt 16:19, Mt 18:18

Catholic “Whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven.”

Catholic reading: Judicial authority over sin and discipline. The power to bind and loose includes the authority to absolve sins sacramentally, to excommunicate, and to set conditions for reconciliation.

Protestant reading: Teaching authority — the power to declare what is permitted and forbidden according to Scripture. Church discipline, not sacramental absolution.

Succession required? Yes, under the Catholic reading.

6. Anointing the Sick — Mk 6:13, Jas 5:14

Catholic + Orthodox “Is anyone among you sick? Let him call for the elders of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil in the name of the Lord.” (James 5:14)

This is one of the strongest proof-texts for priestly authority because James explicitly mentions “elders of the church” (presbyterous, from which “priest” derives) performing a specific ritual action.

Protestant reading: Some Protestants practice anointing of the sick as a pious custom but do not consider it a sacrament with guaranteed efficacy.

Succession required? Yes — James specifies “elders,” implying an office.

7. Exorcism — Mk 6:7, Lk 10:17

Catholic + Orthodox “He called the twelve and began to send them out two by two, and gave them authority over the unclean spirits.” (Mk 6:7)

Catholic reading: Exorcism authority passes through apostolic succession to bishops, who delegate it to trained priests with explicit permission (a two-tier access control system).

Some Protestants: Charismatic and Pentecostal traditions practice exorcism but ground it in direct invocation of Jesus’ name, not in apostolic succession. The authority is claimed directly, not through a chain.

Succession required? Yes, under Catholic practice. No, under charismatic practice.

8. Ordination — 2 Tim 1:6, Titus 1:5

Catholic + Orthodox “I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands.” (2 Tim 1:6)

Catholic reading: Ordination is a sacrament that confers an ontological change (an indelible “character” on the soul). The laying on of hands transmits apostolic authority. This is what makes the whole chain work — ordination is the mechanism of succession.

Protestant reading: Ordination (where practiced) is a functional appointment — the community recognizes someone’s gifts for ministry. It does not confer a special ontological status.

Succession required? Yes — ordination IS the mechanism of succession.

What Is NOT Delegated

The specificity of the Catholic claim becomes clearer when you see what it does NOT claim. Christ possessed authorities that were never delegated to anyone:

Authority Christ Has It Delegated?
Calming storms Mt 8:26-27 No. No delegation verse exists.
Walking on water Mt 14:25 No. Peter tried (Mt 14:29) and sank.
Raising the dead Jn 11:43-44 Not institutionally. Peter raised Tabitha (Acts 9:40) as a charismatic gift, not an office. No priest claims this as a standing power.
Forgiving by own authority Mk 2:10 No. Priests forgive in persona Christi — in Christ’s person, not their own. They are instruments, not sources.
Being the source of grace Jn 1:16 No. Priests administer sacraments as channels. Grace originates from God alone.
This is the point: the Catholic claim is carefully scoped. It does not say “priests can do everything Jesus did.” It says “Jesus specifically delegated these eight authorities, and here are the verses.” The delegation is targeted, not universal.
⁂

The Load-Bearing Axiom

Even granting that Jesus made these eight specific delegations, the Protestant has a further question: “Fine, He delegated to the APOSTLES. But the Apostles are dead. How does this reach present-day priests?”

This is exactly the right question. The answer is apostolic succession — the claim that the Apostles passed their authority to bishops through the laying on of hands, and those bishops to their successors, in an unbroken chain to the present. In our formal model, this is the axiom apostolic_succession_general.

Under Catholic axioms
The chain runs: Christ → Apostles → Bishops → Priests. Each link has a scriptural or traditional warrant. The Apostles appointed bishops (Titus 1:5), bishops ordain priests (2 Tim 1:6), and the chain is unbroken (CCC §77, §862).
Under Protestant axioms
The chain stops at the Apostles. Their authority was unique and non-transferable. Present-day ministers derive authority from Scripture and the gathered community, not from a succession chain. The two ecumenical delegations (teaching and baptism) apply to all believers, not to a priestly class.

Notice the structure: both sides accept steps 1 and 2 (Christ has authority; He delegated some of it). The disagreement is about step 3 (does the delegation extend through succession?) and about which delegations go beyond teaching and baptism.

The Formal Model

In the Catlib codebase, the authority model has been rewritten to reflect this specificity. The key changes:

Old Model New Model
christ_delegates_to_apostles: Christ delegates ALL authority to the Apostles (universal claim). Eight separate axioms (christ_delegates_teaching, christ_delegates_baptism, etc.), each with its own Scripture reference.
One theorem: authority_chain_specific that works for any domain universally. authority_chain_specific takes a domain and a PROOF that Christ delegated that specific authority. Each domain theorem (e.g. teaching_authority) supplies its own delegation axiom.
No model of undelegated authorities. Explicit UndelegatedAuthority structure with examples (nature miracles, self-originated forgiveness, source of grace).
No denominational tagging on delegations. Each delegation in the specificDelegations registry carries a DelegationScope tag and a requiresSuccession flag.

The result: a Protestant reader can look at the formal model and see exactly which delegations they accept (teaching, baptism), which they contest (absolution, eucharist, anointing, exorcism, ordination, binding/loosing), and why the disagreement reduces to apostolic succession.

The Charitable Conclusion

The Protestant objection that started this article was exactly right: the universal delegation claim was too strong. Priests cannot calm storms. The Catholic Church has never claimed they can.

What the Catholic Church does claim is more modest and more specific: Jesus made eight identifiable delegations of authority, each grounded in a particular Scripture passage. Two of those delegations (teaching and baptism) are shared across virtually all Christian traditions. The other six are contested.

The contested delegations all share a common dependency: they require apostolic succession to extend beyond the original Apostles. If you accept succession, the six contested authorities flow naturally from their Scripture verses. If you reject succession, those authorities died with the Apostles, and the two ecumenical delegations (teaching and baptism) belong to all believers, not to an ordained class.

Both readings are internally consistent. The disagreement is not about whether Jesus delegated authority (He did, and both sides cite the same Great Commission). The disagreement is about the SCOPE of the delegation and the MECHANISM of its transmission. Seeing this precisely is more honest than caricaturing either side.
