What If the Catechism Is Wrong?

A young Catholic’s attempt to stress-test 2,000 years of reasoning

⁂

I grew up trying to live by the Catechism of the Catholic Church. Not casually — really trying. No contraception. Confession at least once a month. Mass every Sunday, holy days of obligation, no exceptions. Believing that hell is real and that people can go there. Believing that a priest can absolve your sins, that bread becomes God, that a marriage is permanent.

If you have never tried to live this way, let me tell you: it is counter-cultural by definition. Every single one of those commitments puts you at odds with the world around you. Your coworkers think you are strange. Your friends think you are repressed. The internet thinks you are delusional. And every so often, late at night, a quiet voice in your own head asks the question you are not supposed to ask:

What if the Catechism is wrong?

❅

The Question That Started Everything

Not wrong about God. I never doubted that. The question was sharper than that, and more dangerous:

What if the Catechism is wrong about its own reasoning?

The CCC makes thousands of claims. Those claims depend on each other. Grace depends on a theory of the soul. The sacraments depend on a theory of grace. The moral teachings depend on the sacraments. Pull one thread and the whole tapestry could unravel — or hold. But which?

What if somewhere in the chain there is a hidden contradiction? A place where the Catechism says A on one page and not-A on another? Not because the Church Fathers were stupid, but because 2,000 years is a long time, the system is enormous, and human beings make mistakes even when they are trying their absolute hardest not to.

I wanted to know. Not because I wanted to leave. Because I wanted to stay — and staying means knowing what you are standing on.

❅

A Calculator for Reasoning

I found a tool. It is called a proof assistant — a piece of software that checks logical reasoning the way a calculator checks arithmetic. You give it your starting assumptions. You give it your conclusions. And it tells you, with mathematical certainty, whether one actually follows from the other.

It will not let you skip steps. It will not let you wave your hands and say “and therefore obviously…” It will not let you smuggle in an assumption you did not declare. If your reasoning has a gap, it finds it. If your system contains a contradiction, it catches it.

So I started feeding it the Catechism.

Not all of it. You cannot formalize every paragraph of a 900-page document overnight. But the parts that kept me up at night? The questions I actually cared about? Those I could check.

❅

The Questions I Cared About

“Do miracles still happen?”

This one mattered to me because most of the Christians I grew up around seemed embarrassed by miracles. They treated them like something the early Church needed but we have outgrown — training wheels for a faith that has since learned to walk. The formal term for this is cessationism: the idea that miracles stopped after the apostolic age.

The Catechism says no. Miracles continue. Charisms — gifts of the Holy Spirit — are alive in the Church today.

When we formalized this, the proof assistant showed us why cessationism fails. It only works if you accept two premises simultaneously: sola scriptura (Scripture alone is the authority) and the reduction of all miracles to a single purpose (authenticating the apostles). Remove either premise and cessationism collapses.

But the Catechism goes further. Charisms serve not one but three purposes: they build up the Church, they serve the common good, and they serve the needs of the world. Authentication is not even on the list. The entire frame is different.

And here is the structural kicker: the canonization process — how the Church declares someone a saint — requires verified miracles. The Church does not just believe miracles continue in theory. It has built an institution that structurally depends on them continuing in practice. If miracles stopped, the Church could not make saints. The system would break.

“What is hell like?”

Not fire and pitchforks. That image comes from medieval art, not the Catechism.

The CCC says something far stranger and more precise: God sustains everything in existence. Every atom, every moment, every soul. Even the damned. Hell is not a place where God has withdrawn His power. He cannot withdraw His power without annihilating the soul entirely.

So what is hell? Separation from communion with God, not from existence under God. God is still there — sustaining you, holding you in being — but the relationship of love is refused. Think of it as sitting at a feast and refusing to eat. The food is real. Your hunger is real. But you will not open your mouth.

The proof assistant found something important here: this only works under libertarian free will. The choice to refuse God must be a genuine, uncoerced choice. If God determines your will (as some Calvinist theologies hold), then God would be sending people to hell against their will — which contradicts the CCC’s own claim that God desires the salvation of all. The whole doctrine of hell, it turns out, depends on taking human freedom with radical seriousness. You have to genuinely be able to choose it.

“Do the sacraments actually do anything?”

This is the question that separates Catholic life from Protestant life more than any other.

The Catholic answer is yes: the sacraments confer grace. They do not merely represent grace or remind you of grace. They are channels through which grace actually flows. The technical term is ex opere operato — the sacrament works by the work itself, not by the holiness of the minister or the feelings of the recipient.

When we formalized the alternative — the view that sacraments only signify, that they are symbols pointing to something God does separately — the proof assistant showed us what collapses. If sacraments only signify, then the Eucharist reduces to a memorial meal. Baptism reduces to a public declaration. Reconciliation reduces to a conversation with a man in a box. The entire sacramental economy — seven sacraments, each conferring specific graces, structuring the entire rhythm of Catholic life — collapses to a set of memory aids.

Not wrong, necessarily. But a fundamentally different religion. The proof assistant does not tell you which view is true. It tells you what each view costs.

“Is there anything the CCC got wrong on its own terms?”

Yes.

This is the one that scared me. We found two axioms in our formalization that made the system inconsistent. In logic, an inconsistent system is the worst thing that can happen: you can prove anything, including False. Including the claim that God does not exist. Including the claim that 2 + 2 = 5. Once a contradiction enters, the entire system is meaningless.

I panicked. Then I looked closer.

The Catechism was not wrong. Our formalization was wrong. We had modeled an assumption too loosely, drawn a boundary in the wrong place. When we fixed it — tightened the axiom to match what the CCC actually says rather than what we thought it said — the inconsistency vanished.

And here is the beautiful part: fixing it taught us something the Catechism does not say out loud. The repair revealed a structural relationship between doctrines that we had not noticed before. The proof assistant did not just check the reasoning. It deepened our understanding of it.

❅

The Hidden Assumptions

The most surprising finding was not any single answer. It was the assumptions.

The Catechism rests on a set of philosophical commitments that it rarely names. They are not in Scripture. They are not in the Creed. But they are absolutely load-bearing. Remove any one of them and major doctrines stop working.

Aristotelian teleology: things have purposes. An eye is for seeing. Sex is for procreation and unity. A human life is for something. Without teleology, the entire natural law tradition collapses. You cannot say “this act is disordered” unless you first accept that acts have an order.

Hylomorphic anthropology: you are your body and your soul, not a soul trapped in a body. This is why the Church insists on the resurrection of the body, why it cares about what you do with your body, why it will not accept that the “real you” is some disembodied spirit that happens to be wearing flesh. You are not a ghost in a machine. You are the whole thing.

The substance/accident distinction: a thing can change its deepest identity while retaining all of its observable properties. Bread can stop being bread while still looking, tasting, and testing like bread. Without this distinction, transubstantiation is incoherent. With it, the doctrine is logically precise.

None of these are articles of faith. None of them are in the Bible. All of them are doing enormous structural work. And the proof assistant forced us to name every single one.

❅

Unifying the Chain

The answers to the questions I cared about were never missing. They were scattered. Across Scripture, across the Church Fathers, across conciliar documents, across the writings of saints and doctors spanning two millennia. A sentence from Aquinas here, a canon from Trent there, a paragraph from Vatican II somewhere else.

What we wanted was to unify the logical chain. To take the Church’s beliefs and trace them, link by link, from axiom to conclusion. Not because we doubted the chain existed, but because we wanted to see it.

There is a cheeky irony here that we cannot resist pointing out. Christ calls Himself the Logos — the Word. And logos, in Greek, literally means reason, logic, the rational principle underlying all things. A project that uses logic to explore the faith is not departing from Christ. It is, in a certain sense, taking His title seriously.

❅

A Love Letter, Not an Autopsy

Let me be clear about what this project is.

This is a love letter to the Catholic faith. It is an attempt to steelman her beliefs and traditions — to give them the strongest possible logical foundation, to show that they are not a collection of arbitrary rules but a tightly integrated system where each part supports the others.

If you change the beliefs, the prescriptions change. Accept that sacraments only signify and Confession becomes optional. Accept that the will is totally bound and Purgatory becomes unnecessary. Accept sola scriptura and the Magisterium loses its authority. The daily life of a Catholic Christian is downstream of these starting assumptions.

This is why we say: the axiom set is the denomination.

Catholic versus Protestant is not a thousand disagreements. It is not even a hundred. It is about which handful of axioms you accept or reject. We found that it comes down to four. Change those four and you get Protestantism. Keep them and you get Catholicism. Everything else follows.

We proceed as if both sides reason correctly. We assume both are internally consistent. The proof assistant confirms this: given their respective axioms, both Catholics and Protestants reach their conclusions validly. The disagreement is not about logic. It is about premises.

❅

Inverting the Equation

The way I think about this project is like inverting an equation.

Most of the time, theology works forward: start with beliefs, derive practices. God is love, therefore love your neighbor. Grace transforms, therefore receive the sacraments. The body is sacred, therefore treat it with reverence.

We work backward: what beliefs must we hold to justify our behavior? If Catholics go to Confession, what must be true about grace, priesthood, and sin for that practice to make sense? If we genuflect before the tabernacle, what must be true about bread, substance, and the Real Presence?

This backward question drives the entire investigation. It is an iterative process: formalize a belief, check it, find a gap, read more deeply, refine the formalization, check again. Each cycle teaches us something. Each time the proof assistant rejects a step, we learn exactly where our understanding was imprecise.

We are still working on this. We are, frankly, having the time of our lives. Every new formalization opens up new questions, new connections, new moments of “oh, that’s why the Church teaches that.” We are super excited to share it with the world.

❅

What I Found

I started this project afraid that the Catechism might be wrong.

I did not find that it was right — a proof assistant cannot tell you that. It checks reasoning, not revelation. It cannot tell you whether God exists, whether Christ rose, whether the Holy Spirit guides the Church. Those are acts of faith, not acts of logic.

What I found is that the Catechism knows what it assumes. Its reasoning is extraordinarily tight. Its conclusions follow from its premises with a precision that survived being translated into formal logic and checked by a machine. Two thousand years of accumulated thought, and the structure holds.

I also found exactly what it costs. Every hidden assumption, named. Every philosophical commitment, laid bare. Every place where a different starting point leads to a different Christianity. No hand-waving. No skipped steps. No appeals to mystery where logic could do the work.

Now when I go to Mass on Sunday, I know what I am choosing. Not just emotionally, not just culturally, not just because my parents did. I know the axioms. I know the chain. I know exactly which assumptions I am accepting, what they cost, and what they buy me.

And I choose them. Freely, with my eyes open, with the receipts in hand.

That is what the proof assistant gave me. Not certainty — clarity.
