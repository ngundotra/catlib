Reconciliation and the Restoration of Love

The Most Cross-Cutting Formalization in Catlib

Mortal sin does not make you loveless. It destroys exactly one kind of love. Reconciliation restores exactly that kind — and no more.

⁂

Reconciliation.lean is the most cross-cutting file in catlib. It imports ten other formalizations. It connects sin to love, love to grace, grace to authority, authority to absolution, absolution to temporal punishment, temporal punishment to purgatory, and purgatory to the Reformation. No other file touches this many parts of the system.

But the article you are reading is not about all of those connections. It is about one question that the formalization answers with a precision the Catechism itself never achieves: what kind of love does mortal sin destroy, and what kind does reconciliation restore?

The answer, once you see it in the formal model, is surprisingly comforting. A person in mortal sin is not loveless. They have lost one specific kind of love — agape, supernatural charity — while retaining three others. Reconciliation restores exactly that one kind. The formalization makes this visible in a way that centuries of pastoral theology have gestured toward but never stated with this clarity.

❅

The Four Kinds of Love

The full treatment is in The Mathematics of Love. Here is the summary. The Catechism uses “love” in at least four distinct senses, and Love.lean formalizes them as the four constructors of LoveKind.

Kind What It Is After Mortal Sin
Agape (charity) Willing the good of the other for their own sake. The supernatural love of God above all things. CCC §1822. Destroyed
Eros (desire) Attraction toward the good, the beautiful, the true. Involuntary — you do not choose whom you find beautiful. Benedict XVI, Deus Caritas Est §3–8. Survives
Philia (friendship) Mutual benevolence between persons. Requires reciprocity by definition. Aquinas, ST II-II q.23 a.1. Survives
Self-love Legitimate love of oneself. CCC §2264: “Love toward oneself remains a fundamental principle of morality.” Survives

The Catechism says mortal sin “destroys charity” (§1855). It does not say mortal sin destroys love. The formalization makes the distinction load-bearing: mortal_sin_can_destroy_charity reduces agape to degree zero, while mortal_sin_preserves_eros and mortal_sin_preserves_philia prove that eros and philia remain intact.

The Catechism says “destroys charity.” The formalization says: and only charity. This specificity is implied but never stated as a principle. The proof assistant forced us to declare it.

The Asymmetry: Breaking Is Easier Than Mending

The formal model makes something visible that the Catechism never states as a principle. Consider the type signatures:

Destruction

  1. One free act (mortal sin: gravity + knowledge + consent)

A single SinfulAct where isMortal = true suffices to transition the soul from inGrace to notInGrace.

Restoration

  1. Prevenient grace (God’s prior initiative)
  2. Contrition (genuine sorrow + firm purpose)
  3. Confession (disclosure to a priest)
  4. Absolution (priest with valid authority)
  5. Satisfaction (penance performed)

A full ReconciliationAct with four sub-structures, plus prevenient grace, is required.

The theorem destruction_asymmetric_to_restoration proves both directions formally: part (a) shows that one sinful act suffices for destruction, and part (b) shows that restoration requires all four components of the ReconciliationAct.

Destruction is one act. Restoration is four acts plus divine initiative. The Catechism never calls this an asymmetry. The formalization makes it structurally visible: the inverse operation is strictly harder than the original. This is a thermodynamic intuition embedded in sacramental theology.

The Grace Bootstrapping Recurrence

Here is the circle. A person in mortal sin wants to repent. But:

  1. Contrition requires hope — you must believe reconciliation is possible.
  2. Hope is a theological virtue requiring grace.
  3. But you are in mortal sin — charity destroyed, separated from God.
  4. You need grace to get the grace that enables contrition.

This is the same circularity that appears in CCC §2001, formalized in Grace.lean as the grace_bootstrapping_resolved theorem. The resolution is identical: prevenient grace (GraceType.prevenient) is unconditionally available. It precedes any human response. God moves first.

CCC §1489 states it plainly: “To come back to communion with God after having lost it through sin is a process born of the grace of God who is rich in mercy… One must be moved by grace to respond to the merciful love of God who loved us first.”

The theorem grace_bootstrapping_in_reconciliation resolves the circle by calling prevenient_grace_unconditioned — the same axiom from Grace.lean that resolves the §2001 bootstrapping problem. The proof is literally identical: the same function call, the same axiom, the same resolution. This is not a coincidence. It reflects a deep structural principle: God always initiates.

The same formal pattern solves two apparently different theological problems. The §2001 bootstrapping (“how can you come to faith without grace if grace requires faith?”) and the reconciliation bootstrapping (“how can you be contrite without grace if contrition requires grace?”) are the same recurrence with the same base case: prevenient_grace_unconditioned.

What Mortal Sin Does Not Destroy

This is pastorally important. A person hearing “mortal sin destroys love” might conclude that a mortal sinner is incapable of any love at all. The formalization shows this is false. The theorem mortal_sin_destroys_charity_not_eros proves both halves: agape can be destroyed, and eros survives.

Eros Survives Mortal Sin

A person in mortal sin can still feel attraction to beauty, goodness, and truth. Eros is involuntary — you do not choose your attractions — and mortal sin, which is a free act against charity, does not extinguish involuntary responses. The axiom mortal_sin_preserves_eros states this directly. A sinner watching a sunset still finds it beautiful.

Philia Survives Mortal Sin

A person in mortal sin can still have genuine friendships. Philia is mutual benevolence between persons, and the axiom mortal_sin_preserves_philia preserves it. This resolves a real pastoral puzzle: how can mortal sinners still love their families? They love with eros and philia, not with supernatural charity.

Self-Love Survives Mortal Sin

CCC §2264 declares that “love toward oneself remains a fundamental principle of morality.” Self-love grounds the right to legitimate defense. Since mortal sin does not destroy self-love, a person in mortal sin retains the right to self-defense. The theorem self_love_grounds_self_defense connects self-love to the defense right; nothing in the model removes self-love upon mortal sin.

The structural finding

A person in mortal sin is not loveless. They have lost the supernatural dimension of love — agape, the willing of another’s good for God’s sake — while retaining the natural dimensions: attraction, friendship, and care for self. The Catechism implies this by saying “destroys charity” rather than “destroys love.” The formalization makes the implication an explicit, machine-checked claim.

Where They Agree

Before the disagreement, the common ground. It is more than most people on either side expect.

Fully Shared

  • Sin separates from God. S5 is ecumenical. All Christians agree that sin breaks the relationship with God.
  • Repentance requires genuine sorrow. Contrition — real sorrow with the intent to change — is affirmed across all traditions.
  • God initiates the return. The sinner cannot repent from their own resources. God moves first. Even Calvinists and Arminians agree on this, though they describe the mechanism differently.
  • Destruction is easier than restoration. No tradition claims that undoing sin is as simple as committing it. The asymmetry is universally recognized in substance.
  • Mortal sin does not destroy all love. No tradition claims that sinners are utterly incapable of any love. The four-fold distinction (agape, eros, philia, self-love) is broadly ecumenical — C.S. Lewis articulated essentially the same taxonomy in The Four Loves.

The disagreement, precisely stated

The disagreement is not about whether sin separates or whether God initiates return. It is about the mechanism of restoration: whether sacramental confession to a priest with apostolic authority is required (Catholic + Orthodox), or whether confession directly to God suffices (Protestant). And it is about whether temporal punishment survives absolution (Catholic) or whether Christ’s atonement removes all punishment (Protestant).

The Thread to the Reformation

Reconciliation.lean completes a narrative arc that runs through the entire project. The thread is tight enough to follow in a single chain.

The Complete Arc
Temporal punishment survives absolution → Purgatory exists to address it → Indulgences remit temporal punishment → Luther’s 95 Theses (1517) → Sola fide → The axiom swap

Luther’s objection was not primarily about confession itself. He valued private confession and never abolished it. The objection was about what happens after absolution: if guilt is truly removed, why does punishment remain? Under Catholic axioms, the answer is the temporal/eternal distinction — absolution removes eternal punishment (guilt) but temporal punishment (disordered attachments) persists. This is what indulgences claim to address, and what purgatory resolves post-mortem.

Under Protestant axioms, Christ’s atonement covers everything. There is no residual temporal punishment, therefore no purgatory, therefore no coherent role for indulgences. The theorem temporal_punishment_survives_absolution is the hinge: accept it, and the Catholic sacramental system is coherent; reject it, and indulgences become incoherent.

Neither side is being foolish. Both reason correctly from their premises. The disagreement is not about logic — it is about which axioms to accept. A proof assistant cannot tell you which axioms are true. But it can show you what you are accepting and what follows from it.

Where Formalization Stops

The formal model shows that reconciliation restores agape under Catholic axioms. It shows the asymmetry between destruction and restoration. It shows the bootstrapping recurrence. It does not — and cannot — answer the following:

  • Whether Catholic or Protestant axioms are correct. The model proves logical validity given axioms, not the truth of the axioms themselves.
  • Whether a specific penitent’s contrition is genuine. This is an empirical, interior question that no formal system can adjudicate.
  • To what degree agape is restored. The formalization proves degree > 0 — charity returns. Whether it returns to its previous level, or higher, or lower, is not specified. CCC §1468 speaks of “restoring us to God’s grace” without quantifying the degree.
  • Why temporal punishment survives absolution. The model takes this as an axiom (§1472–1473). The “why” belongs to a deeper metaphysics of justice and disorder that the current formalization does not model.

The Charitable Conclusion

A person in mortal sin is not a person without love. They are a person who has lost one specific kind of love — the supernatural charity that orders all lesser loves toward God. They can still find beauty in a sunset (eros). They can still cherish their friends (philia). They can still care about their own life (self-love). What they have lost is the love that connects all of these to their source and summit.

Reconciliation restores exactly that. Not more, not less. The agape that was at zero returns to positive. The beatifying mode of divine relation switches back on. Communion is restored.

But the restoration is harder than the destruction. One free act broke charity. Four acts — contrition, confession, absolution, satisfaction — plus God’s prior initiative through prevenient grace are needed to restore it. The Catechism never calls this an asymmetry. The formalization makes it structurally visible, and it connects by a single thread through temporal punishment, purgatory, and indulgences all the way to the Reformation.

Seeing this precisely is more honest than arguing past each other. Both the Catholic who holds that temporal punishment survives absolution and the Protestant who denies it are reasoning coherently from their respective axiom sets. The disagreement is not about logic. It is about which axioms to accept. Seeing that clearly is better than caricaturing either side.
