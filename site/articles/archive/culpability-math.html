Culpability as a Partially Ordered Space

The mathematics hiding inside the Catechism

The CCC’s reasoning about culpability implies a specific mathematical structure that has never been made explicit. We extract what we can prove and identify what remains genuinely open.

⁂

Culpability is not just a theological concept. It has mathematical structure. When the Catechism of the Catholic Church reasons about mortal and venial sin, about diminished consent and incomplete knowledge, about the effects of fear, habit, and psychological pressure on moral responsibility, it is implicitly constructing a mathematical object — one with partial ordering, multiple dimensions, diminishers, and thresholds.

The Catlib project formalized the Catechism’s moral theology in Lean 4 and found something surprising: the binary structures in the formalization — MoralKnowledge as full | notFull, MoralConsent as complete | incomplete — are a coarsening of what the Catechism actually implies. The text uses binary language for the mortal/venial threshold, but continuous language for pastoral application. §2352 speaks of factors that “lessen, if even to a minimum” culpability. You do not “lessen to a minimum” something that admits of only two values.

This article extracts the full mathematical structure implied by the CCC’s reasoning across multiple passages. Some properties are established. One critical question is genuinely unresolved — not a gap in our formalization, but a gap in the source material itself.

❅

The Three Dimensions

From the Catechism’s treatment of mortal sin (§1857–1862), culpability lives in at least three independent axes. The formalization in Sin.lean models these as binary inductive types:

Knowledge

Formalized as MoralKnowledge with values full and notFull. But §2352 implies a spectrum: factors can diminish knowledge by degrees, suggesting a continuous range from total ignorance to full awareness. The binary model is a coarsening of an underlying interval [0, 1].

Consent

Formalized as MoralConsent with values complete and incomplete. But the Catechism lists multiple factors that diminish consent — fear, habit, psychological pressure, affective immaturity. The word “diminish” is graded language. Complete consent and no consent are the endpoints of a spectrum, not the only two points on it.

Matter

Formalized as MatterGravity with values grave and lesSerious. This axis is different from the other two: it is a property of the act, not of the agent. A lie and a murder differ in the gravity of their matter regardless of who commits them. This asymmetry between axes is itself a structural feature — matter is objective; knowledge and consent are subjective.

The Catechism uses binary language precisely where it draws the mortal/venial threshold (§1857: “full knowledge and deliberate consent”) and continuous language precisely where it discusses pastoral application and diminished responsibility (§2352, §1860). This is not a contradiction. The binary classification is a threshold applied to continuous axes — like the distinction between water and ice, which is a discrete classification imposed on the continuous variable of temperature.

What We Can Establish

The following properties are implied or directly stated by the Catechism’s reasoning across §1857–1862, §2352, §1734, and §1776–1791. Each follows from taking the CCC’s own logic seriously as mathematics.

a) Partially ordered, not totally ordered

Consider two persons. Person A commits a grave act with high knowledge but under extreme duress (diminished consent). Person B commits the same act with low knowledge but full consent. Who is more culpable? The Catechism provides no answer, because it provides no weighting between axes. These two points in culpability space are incomparable — neither is greater than the other. This is the signature of a partial order, not a total order. You cannot always rank two people’s culpability on a single number line.

b) Has a top element

§1857 defines this explicitly: full knowledge, complete consent, grave matter. The point (1, 1, 1) in culpability space is mortal sin — maximum culpability. Every other point is below or incomparable to it.

c) Has an infimum it approaches but may not reach

Zero culpability requires zero freedom, but the Catechism’s model of freedom (§1731–1734) treats it as a property of rational agents. A rock is not culpable because a rock is not free. A human agent always has some freedom, some knowledge, some residual consent. The origin point (0, 0, 0) is a mathematical limit that a free agent approaches asymptotically but may never occupy.

d) Monotone in each axis

More knowledge, holding everything else fixed, means more culpability. More consent, holding everything else fixed, means more culpability. Graver matter, holding everything else fixed, means more culpability. The Catechism never suggests that increasing any axis could decrease culpability. Each axis is monotone.

e) Has a threshold surface

The mortal/venial boundary is not a point — it is a surface in the three-dimensional space. Above it: mortal sin. Below it: venial sin. The Catechism defines this surface implicitly by requiring all three axes to be at their maximum for mortal sin. On binary axes, this is the single point (full, complete, grave). On continuous axes, the surface becomes a genuine boundary — like a phase transition in physics, separating two qualitatively different regimes.

f) The axes are not symmetric

Matter is a property of the act. Knowledge and consent are properties of the agent. This asymmetry connects directly to the TwoLevelEvaluation structure from Conscience.lean — the Catechism consistently distinguishes objective moral evaluation (is the act good?) from subjective culpability assessment (is the agent blameworthy?). Matter belongs to the first level; knowledge and consent belong to the second.

g) Conscience creates a lexicographic override

The culpability asymmetry proved in Conscience.lean reveals a fourth structural feature: acting against conscience is categorically worse than following an erring one. This behaves like a lexicographic ordering — a separate dimension that is checked first, before the three-axis assessment even begins. No amount of diminished knowledge or consent excuses deliberately violating conscience. The conscience dimension overrides.

h) Diminishers exist and are listed

The Catechism explicitly names factors that reduce culpability along specific axes: fear, habit and addiction, ignorance, psychological factors and disorders, affective immaturity, social pressure and duress (§2352, §1860, §1735). Each diminisher operates on a specific axis — ignorance reduces knowledge; fear, habit, and duress reduce consent. The responsibility_proportional_to_freedom axiom in Freedom.lean formalizes the link: less freedom means less responsibility means less culpability.

The Open Question

Everything above is established — directly stated or logically implied by the Catechism’s reasoning. What follows is not. It is a genuine gap in the source material, and identifying it precisely is one of the contributions of formal analysis.

How do diminishers compose?

The Catechism lists multiple diminishers — fear, habit, ignorance, psychological factors, affective immaturity, social pressure — but never specifies how they combine. If a person acts out of fear and habit and under psychological duress, how do these factors interact? The CCC asserts that each factor “lessens” culpability but provides no operator for composing multiple diminishers.

At least four models are conceivable:

a) Multiplicative

effective_consent = consent × (1 − fear) × (1 − habit) × (1 − anxiety)

Problem: This is a modeling choice, not something implied by the CCC. It also means diminishers always compound, which may or may not match the intended theology.

b) Additive

effective_consent = consent − fear − habit − anxiety

Problem: Could go negative, which is incoherent. Requires an artificial floor at zero.

c) Maximum diminisher wins

effective_consent = consent × (1 − max(fear, habit, anxiety))

Problem: Ignores compounding effects entirely. A person under extreme fear AND extreme addiction is treated the same as someone under only one of those pressures.

d) Unspecified

The CCC genuinely does not tell us.

This is not a gap in the formalization. It is a gap in the source material.

The Catechism leaves all of the following genuinely unresolved:

  • Whether diminishers compose additively, multiplicatively, or by some other operator
  • Whether multiple diminishers compound or cap at some maximum reduction
  • Whether there is a minimum floor below which culpability cannot drop for a free agent
  • Whether diminishers on different axes interact — does fear combined with ignorance compound differently than fear alone?

We do not know the composition operator. This is not a failure of our formalization. It is a genuine open question in moral theology. The Catechism asserts that diminishers exist, lists them, and says they reduce culpability. It does not specify the algebra.

This matters pastorally, not just mathematically. A confessor assessing the culpability of someone who acted out of addiction, psychological disturbance, and social pressure simultaneously is implicitly choosing a composition model — without knowing they are doing so, and without the Catechism giving them one.

The Full Structure

Here is the complete mathematical picture, as far as the Catechism’s reasoning determines it. This table is the summary of what formal analysis extracted from the CCC’s text — what is established, what is implied, and what is left open.

Property What the CCC Implies Status
Carrier set [0, 1]³ (knowledge × consent × matter) Implied by continuous language
Order Product order (partial, not total) Proven — incomparable pairs exist
Top element (1, 1, 1) = mortal sin Proven — explicitly stated (§1857)
Bottom Approached but not reached by free agents Implied by freedom model
Threshold Mortal/venial boundary is a surface Implied by binary classification on continuous axes
Diminishers Exist, listed, reduce individual axes Proven — explicitly stated (§2352, §1860)
Composition of diminishers UNKNOWN Open question
Conscience dimension Lexicographic override Implied by culpability asymmetry
Symmetry of axes NOT symmetric — matter is objective, others subjective Implied by two-level evaluation
Monotonicity Yes, in each axis independently Implied

The pattern is striking. Most of the structure is recoverable from careful reading. But the composition question — how multiple diminishers interact — is not merely unrecovered. It is unspecified in the source material. The Catechism leaves it as a free parameter.

Toward a Formalization

What would this look like if we tried to formalize it? The current Lean code in Sin.lean uses binary inductive types. A fuller formalization would require real-valued types from Mathlib. Here is a sketch — theoretical, not machine-checked — of what the continuous model would entail.

Theoretical — not machine-checked
structure Culpability where knowledge : Real -- [0,1] consent : Real -- [0,1] matter : Real -- [0,1] -- Partial order (product order on the cube) instance : LE Culpability where le a b := a.knowledge ≤ b.knowledge ∧ a.consent ≤ b.consent ∧ a.matter ≤ b.matter -- The mortal sin threshold (the coarsened version) def isMortalSin (c : Culpability) : Prop := c.knowledge = 1 ∧ c.consent = 1 ∧ c.matter = 1 -- Diminisher application — BUT WHICH OPERATOR? -- This is the open question. All of the following type-check. -- None of them are derivable from the CCC. -- Option A: multiplicative def applyDiminisher_mult (c : Culpability) (d : Real) : Culpability := { c with consent := c.consent * (1 - d) } -- Option B: additive (with floor) def applyDiminisher_add (c : Culpability) (d : Real) : Culpability := { c with consent := max 0 (c.consent - d) } -- Option C: max-diminisher def applyDiminishers_max (c : Culpability) (ds : List Real) : Culpability := { c with consent := c.consent * (1 - ds.maximum) } -- All three are well-typed. All three are monotone. -- The CCC does not determine which one is correct.

The pseudocode makes the gap visible in a way that prose does not. Three different functions, each consistent with every statement the Catechism makes, each producing different results when multiple diminishers are present. The type system accepts all of them. The theology does not choose among them.

What Formalization Found

This is what happens when you take theological reasoning seriously as mathematics. The Catechism has more structure than most people realize — a partial order, monotone axes, a threshold surface, a lexicographic conscience dimension — and more open questions than most theologians acknowledge.

The binary formalization in Sin.lean is not wrong. It captures the mortal/venial threshold accurately. But it is a coarsening of a richer structure that the Catechism implies without stating. Moving from the binary model to the continuous model would require Mathlib’s real number library, a proper treatment of bounded intervals, and — crucially — a decision about the composition of diminishers that the CCC does not make.

That last point is the one worth sitting with. Formal verification did not just confirm what the Catechism says. It identified a question the Catechism does not answer. The diminisher composition problem is not obscure — it is encountered every time a confessor evaluates the culpability of someone acting under multiple mitigating factors simultaneously. Every such evaluation implicitly assumes a composition model. The Catechism does not provide one.

The proof assistant found both the structure and the silence. Both matter.
