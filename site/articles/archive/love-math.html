The Mathematics of Love

Four kinds, four algebras, four open questions

The Catechism uses “love” in at least four different senses without always distinguishing them. What happens when a proof assistant forces the disambiguation?

⁂

The Catechism uses “love” in at least four different senses without always distinguishing them. What happens when a proof assistant forces the disambiguation?

The existing formalization in Axioms.lean declares three love-related primitives: godIsLove (God’s nature is love), loves (a binary relation between persons), and loveRequiresFreedom (genuine love requires the beloved’s freedom). These are clean and useful. They are also imprecise. The “love” of 1 John 4:8 (“God IS love”), the “love” of Mt 5:44 (“love your enemies”), the “love” that CCC §1827 says increases through virtue, and the “love” that CCC §2264 says grounds legitimate defense are not the same concept. Using a single loves predicate collapses distinctions the Catechism itself makes.

The new formalization in Love.lean introduces a TypedLove structure that specifies which kind of love is at work. Each kind turns out to have different mathematical properties — different symmetry, different freedom requirements, different behavior under mortal sin. The disambiguation is not pedantic. It resolves genuine theological puzzles.

❅

The Four Kinds

Following the classical tradition (Aquinas, C.S. Lewis, Benedict XVI), the formalization distinguishes four kinds of love. The Catechism never uses the Greek terms, but its text operates with exactly these distinctions.

Agape (Charity)

Willing the good of the other for their own sake. CCC §1822: “Charity is the theological virtue by which we love God above all things for his own sake.” This is the supernatural love — the one that CCC §1827 says increases through virtue and CCC §1855 says mortal sin destroys. It is the love of Mt 5:44: you can love your enemies with agape, because it is a free act of the will, not a feeling. Ecumenical

Eros (Desire-Love)

Attraction toward the good, the beautiful, the true. Benedict XVI (Deus Caritas Est §3–8) argues this is not opposed to agape but is its natural starting point. The critical difference: eros is involuntary. You do not choose whom you find beautiful. This makes eros categorically different from agape with respect to freedom — a difference the untyped loves predicate cannot express. Ecumenical

Philia (Friendship)

Mutual benevolence between persons. Aquinas (ST II-II q.23 a.1) defines charity itself as a friendship with God — but friendship, by definition, requires mutuality. You cannot be friends with someone who does not know you exist. This makes philia the only love-kind that is symmetric by definition. Ecumenical

Self-Love

Legitimate love of self. CCC §2264: “Love toward oneself remains a fundamental principle of morality. It is therefore legitimate to insist on respect for one’s own right to life.” This is the moral basis for legitimate defense. It is reflexive by definition: the lover and beloved are the same person. Ecumenical

The Catechism never names these four kinds explicitly as a taxonomy. But its text operates with exactly these distinctions — treating charity as destroyable by mortal sin, attraction as involuntary, friendship as mutual, and self-love as the ground of legitimate defense. The formalization makes the implicit taxonomy explicit.

The table makes visible what the single loves predicate hides. A claim like “love requires freedom” is true for one row and false for another. A claim like “mortal sin destroys love” is true for exactly one kind. Without the disambiguation, these claims appear contradictory. With it, they are precise.

The Ordo Amoris

Aquinas (ST II-II q.26) argues that love has a proper ordering. Not all love-targets are equal. The Great Commandment (Mt 22:37–39) establishes a strict hierarchy:

Love of God > Love of Neighbor > Love of Self

This ordering applies to agape specifically — it is about the proper direction of charity, not of eros or philia. The formalization models this as a strict ordering on LoveTarget values (god, neighbor, self_), with axioms asserting that properly ordered agape directs more intensity toward higher targets.

The ordo amoris explains why self-sacrifice for others is a higher act of love than self-preservation: neighbor ranks above self in the ordering. But it also explains why self-love is legitimate — it is the lowest rank, not an illegitimate rank. CCC §2264 explicitly affirms self-love as a “fundamental principle of morality.” The ordering does not eliminate self-love; it subordinates it.

The ordo amoris is a strict ordering on love-TARGETS, not on love-KINDS. Agape, eros, philia, and self-love are kinds; God, neighbor, and self are targets. These are orthogonal dimensions. The formalization makes this distinction explicit — a conflation the Catechism sometimes invites by discussing both without separating them.

Impact on Existing Theorems

The typed love relation sharpens several results in the existing formalization and exposes imprecision in others.

Sharper: S1 (God is love; love requires freedom)

s1_god_is_love asserts godIsLove ∧ loveRequiresFreedom. The typed version restricts the second conjunct: love_requires_freedom_only_agape proves that agape requires freedom while eros does not. The over-general axiom is now qualified. This matters for the hell doctrine: the damned may still experience eros (desire for goods they lack) but have definitively refused agape toward God.

Sharper: S5 (Sin separates from God)

s5_sin_separates asserts that grave sin breaks communion. mortal_sin_destroys_charity_not_eros specifies what “breaks communion” means at the love level: agape is destroyed, but eros and philia survive. The sinner is separated from God’s friendship (agape-communion) but not from all forms of love.

Sharper: TheologyOfBody (participatesInLove)

PARTICIPATION in TheologyOfBody.lean derives that persons “participate in love” via the Imago Dei. But which kind of love? The typed version reveals: persons participate in agape (as images of the God who IS agape), which is why the personalist norm demands agape as the adequate response. Eros alone would not ground the norm.

Connected: Self-love and legitimate defense

self_love_grounds_self_defense derives from self_love_reflexive that the lover and beloved coincide. CCC §2264 makes this derivation explicitly: because you legitimately love yourself, you may defend the good of your own life. This connects Love.lean to LegitimateDefense.lean.

Why Sinners Can Still Love

CCC §1855: “Mortal sin destroys charity in the heart of man.” This has always presented a pastoral puzzle. If mortal sin destroys love, how can mortal sinners still love their children, maintain friendships, or feel moved by beauty? The answer, made precise by the typed formalization, is that mortal sin destroys charity specifically — supernatural agape — while leaving the natural forms of love intact.

Catholic After mortal sin, a person retains:

  • Eros — the sinner still finds beauty beautiful, still feels attracted to goodness and truth. CCC §1264 confirms that even concupiscence (disordered eros) persists in the baptized.
  • Philia — the sinner still has genuine friendships. Natural mutual benevolence does not require grace.
  • Self-love — the sinner still cares about their own well-being. They suffer, which implies self-love.

What is lost is agape — the supernatural love by which the person wills the good of others for God’s sake and participates in God’s own love. This is why the Catechism can say mortal sin “destroys charity” without implying that mortal sinners become incapable of all love. The typed formalization makes the scope precise. The theorem mortal_sin_destroys_charity_not_eros proves this formally.

This resolves a common misreading. “Mortal sin destroys love” sounds like mortal sinners become loveless monsters. The precise claim is: mortal sin destroys the supernatural love-kind (agape/charity). The natural love-kinds (eros, philia, self-love) survive. The Catechism says exactly this (§1855: “charity” specifically) but the disambiguation is easy to miss without the typed formalization.

The Open Questions

Everything above is established — directly stated or logically implied by the Catechism’s reasoning combined with the classical tradition. What follows is not. These are genuine gaps in the source material, not gaps in the formalization. Identifying them precisely is one of the contributions of formal analysis — parallel to the diminisher composition problem discovered in culpability.

1. How do love-kinds compose?

How do agape and eros combine in conjugal love? Benedict XVI (Deus Caritas Est §3–8) argues that in married love, eros and agape are not simply summed but transformed — eros is “purified” and “elevated” by agape. But we have no operator for this transformation. This is structurally the SAME problem as diminisher composition in culpability: the Catechism asserts that multiple factors interact but does not specify the algebra.

a) Sum

conjugal_love = agape.degree + eros.degree

Problem: Does not capture transformation. Two independent quantities added together is not what Benedict XVI describes.

b) Product

conjugal_love = agape.degree × eros.degree

Problem: If either is zero, total is zero. Is loveless attraction zero conjugal love? Is affectionless charity zero conjugal love? Both seem wrong.

c) Transformation

elevated_agape = transform(eros, agape)

Problem: What is the function? Benedict XVI suggests the direction but not the mechanism.

d) Unspecified

The magisterium genuinely does not tell us.

This is not a gap in the formalization. It is a gap in the source material.

2. Can love reach zero across ALL dimensions?

The damned have definitively refused agape toward God. But do they retain self-love? They suffer — which implies they care about their own state, which implies some form of self-love. Zero total love (all four kinds at degree 0) may be impossible for a conscious being.

If the damned retain self-love, then total love is never zero for any existent conscious being. This would be a surprisingly strong claim: love as a strictly positive quantity, like temperature above absolute zero.

3. “God IS love” as identity

S1 declares godIsLove : Prop — God IS love. But this is modeled as a predicate (God has the property of being love), not an identity (God’s essence = love). The identity claim is much stronger: it says love is not something God HAS but something God IS.

In Lean, we could write axiom god_essence_is_love : God = Love, but this requires God and Love to be the same type — which our type system does not support. The identity claim connects to divine simplicity (all God’s attributes are identical with His essence). Fully capturing this requires metaphysical modeling beyond what the current formalization attempts.

4. Is eros ordered toward agape?

Benedict XVI (Deus Caritas Est §6–7) argues that eros is not simply a different kind of love but is ordered toward agape — eros naturally tends toward transformation into agape when properly directed. If true, this introduces a teleological ordering among love-kinds (not just among love-targets as in the ordo amoris). Disordered eros would be eros that resists this natural trajectory.

But Benedict XVI suggests the direction without specifying the mechanism. We can assert that eros “should” be ordered toward agape, but we cannot formalize the transformation function.

The Proposed Lean Structure

The formalization introduces two core types. The first is an inductive type distinguishing the four love-kinds. The second is a structure that captures a specific love-relation with its kind, participants, and degree.

inductive LoveKind where | agape -- Charity: willing the good of the other | eros -- Desire-love: attraction toward the good | philia -- Friendship: mutual benevolence | selfLove -- Legitimate self-love (CCC §2264) structure TypedLove where kind : LoveKind lover : Person beloved : Person -- for selfLove: lover = beloved degree : Nat -- 0 = absent, higher = more intense

The degree field captures CCC §1827’s teaching that charity can increase (“the practice of all the virtues is animated and inspired by charity”) and CCC §1855’s teaching that mortal sin can reduce it to zero. The kind field is what the untyped loves predicate lacks: it forces you to say which love you mean.

What Formalization Found

This is what happens when you take the Catechism’s use of “love” seriously as a formal concept. The single word splits into four distinct relations, each with its own algebra. The disambiguation is not academic. It resolves at least three genuine puzzles:

  • Why mortal sinners can still love their families (eros and philia survive mortal sin)
  • Why “love requires freedom” and “involuntary attraction is not sinful” are not contradictions (different love-kinds)
  • Why self-love is legitimate but subordinate (the ordo amoris ranks targets, not kinds)

And it identifies four questions the Catechism does not answer — the composition of love-kinds, the possibility of zero total love, the identity (not merely predicate) nature of “God IS love,” and the teleological ordering of eros toward agape. These are not failures of formalization. They are gaps in the source material that only become visible when you try to make every claim precise.

The proof assistant found both the structure and the silence. Both matter.
