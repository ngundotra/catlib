Conjugal Ethics

The inseparability principle and its breakdown

How should faithful Catholics think about family size, contraception, and the mother’s health?

⁂

This is probably the most practically contested teaching in the Catholic Catechism. Not the Trinity, not the Eucharist, not purgatory — contraception. Surveys consistently show that the majority of self-identified Catholics in the West disagree with their Church on this point. And yet the teaching has never changed.

Why? Because the teaching rests on a single axiom — the inseparability principle — that the Catholic Church considers non-negotiable. And that axiom has a history that most Catholics (and most Protestants) have never heard: every Christian denomination on earth agreed with it until 1930. Then one conference changed one axiom, and the entire Christian consensus on contraception collapsed within a generation.

This article traces what happened, formalizes the reasoning in Lean 4, and lays out the Catholic position with its hidden assumptions exposed. The goal is not to argue that the teaching is easy. The goal is to show that it is coherent — and to make visible the precise point where other Christians diverge.

❅

Every Christian Denomination Agreed — Until 1930

For nineteen centuries, the Christian position on contraception was unanimous. Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, Reformed — every tradition that had anything to say on the subject said the same thing: deliberately frustrating the procreative purpose of the conjugal act is morally wrong.

This was not a Catholic invention. The earliest Christian text outside the New Testament, the Didache (c. 100 AD), condemned abortifacients. Clement of Alexandria, John Chrysostom, Augustine, and Jerome all taught that contraception violated the natural order of marriage. The Reformers agreed: Luther called contraception “far more atrocious than incest and adultery” (Commentary on Genesis, 38:10). Calvin was equally direct. John Wesley preached against it. Every major Protestant confession either condemned it or assumed its wrongness as obvious.

This consensus held through the twentieth century. As late as 1920, the Anglican Lambeth Conference reaffirmed the traditional teaching. Resolution 68 of 1920 stated that the Conference “utters an emphatic warning against the use of unnatural means for the avoidance of conception.”

The 1920 position was ecumenical. Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Reformed, Orthodox, Methodist, Baptist — all held the inseparability principle. This was not a denominational distinctive. It was the universal Christian teaching.

Then came 1930. The seventh Lambeth Conference, under pressure from the growing birth control movement (Margaret Sanger’s American Birth Control League, Marie Stopes in Britain) and pastoral concern for couples in genuine economic hardship during the Great Depression, passed Resolution 15. It read:

“Where there is a clearly felt moral obligation to limit or avoid parenthood, the method must be decided on Christian principles. The primary and obvious method is complete abstinence from intercourse (as far as may be necessary) in a life of discipline and self-control lived in the power of the Holy Spirit. Nevertheless in those cases where there is such a clearly felt moral obligation to limit or avoid parenthood, and where there is a morally sound reason for avoiding complete abstinence, the Conference agrees that other methods may be used, provided that this is done in the light of the same Christian principles.”
— Lambeth Conference, Resolution 15 (1930)

The language was cautious, hedged, and pastoral. But the axiom change was absolute. For the first time in Christian history, a major Christian body had officially declared that the unitive and procreative meanings of the conjugal act could be deliberately separated when there was “morally sound reason.”

The Catholic Church responded almost immediately. On December 31, 1930, Pope Pius XI issued Casti Connubii, which reaffirmed the inseparability principle in the strongest possible terms. Thirty-eight years later, Pope Paul VI repeated it in Humanae Vitae (1968), the most controversial encyclical of the twentieth century.

Meanwhile, every other Protestant denomination followed the Anglican lead. The Federal Council of Churches (a major American Protestant body) accepted contraception in 1931. By the 1960s, the consensus had completely reversed: Protestantism now overwhelmingly accepted contraception within marriage. The change happened so fast, and so thoroughly, that most Protestants today are unaware there was ever a different position.

The Inseparability Principle, Formalized

The Catholic teaching on contraception rests on one axiom, declared in Humanae Vitae §12:

“The Church… teaches that each and every marital act must of necessity retain its intrinsic relationship to the procreation of human life. This particular doctrine… is based on the inseparable connection, established by God, which man on his own initiative may not break, between the unitive significance and the procreative significance which are both inherent to the marriage act.”
— Humanae Vitae §12

In the Lean formalization, this becomes: 

structure ConjugalAct where
  spouse1 : Person
  spouse2 : Person
  unitiveIntact : Prop      -- expresses spousal love
  procreativeIntact : Prop  -- open to life

axiom inseparability_principle :
  ∀ (act : ConjugalAct),
    act.unitiveIntact → act.procreativeIntact →
    act.bothMeaningsIntact
Hidden assumption: the inseparability principle is asserted, not derived. Humanae Vitae says this connection is “established by God” and grounded in natural law, but never provides a formal derivation of why the two meanings are inseparable. Aquinas grounded it in natural teleology — the procreative end of sex is evident from biology. But whether teleological facts (what sex is for) generate moral obligations (what we must do with sex) is itself a philosophical question — the is-ought gap. The Catechism treats the inseparability principle as a starting point, not a conclusion.

Once you accept this axiom, the Catholic position on contraception follows with logical necessity. Contraception’s moral object is to retain the unitive meaning while deliberately frustrating the procreative meaning. The inseparability principle says you cannot do this. Therefore contraception is intrinsically evil.

And once you reject this axiom, the Catholic position does not follow. That is exactly what happened after 1930.

❅

NFP vs. Contraception — Same Outcome, Different Object

This is the most contested distinction in Catholic sexual ethics. Critics have called it “moral hair-splitting” and “a distinction without a difference.” The objection is simple: if a couple uses NFP to avoid pregnancy, and another couple uses a condom to avoid pregnancy, the outcome is the same — no baby. How can one be moral and the other immoral?

The answer depends on an axiom we already formalized in SourcesOfMorality.lean: the object-independence axiom — the moral character of an act’s object is independent of its consequences. This is the deontological core of Catholic moral theology.

Under this framework, the two acts have different moral objects:

The Object Distinction
NFP Object: not having sex during the fertile period. Abstaining from sex is never intrinsically evil. You cannot sin by choosing not to do something that is not obligatory. When the couple does have relations (during the infertile period), both meanings are intact: the act is unitive and naturally (not artificially) infertile.
Contraception Object: having sex while rendering procreation impossible. The conjugal act is performed, but its procreative meaning is deliberately frustrated. Under the inseparability principle, this separates what cannot be separated. The object itself is disordered.

In the Lean formalization, this distinction is captured precisely: 

-- NFP: the object is abstinence (not evil)
def NFPObject : Prop := True

-- Contraception: the object separates the meanings
def ContraceptionObject (act : ConjugalAct) : Prop :=
  act.unitiveIntact ∧ ¬act.procreativeIntact

The theorem contraception_intrinsically_evil then shows that contraception’s object is never compatible with the inseparability principle, making it intrinsically evil under the IntrinsicallyEvil definition from the three-source framework.

Hidden assumption: the act/object distinction does real work here. The Catholic argument requires that two acts with the same consequence (no child) can have different moral objects. This is the object-independence axiom in action. A consequentialist rejects this axiom: if the outcome is the same, the morality is the same. Under consequentialism, NFP and contraception are morally equivalent. The entire Catholic distinction depends on a non-consequentialist framework.

This is worth sitting with. If you find the NFP/contraception distinction persuasive, you implicitly accept the object-independence axiom. If you find it absurd, you are likely operating from consequentialist premises. Neither position is stupid. They are different axiom sets.

The Mother’s Health — Double Effect

One of the most pastorally urgent questions is: what about a woman who needs hormonal treatment for a medical condition (endometriosis, polycystic ovary syndrome, severe menstrual disorders) and that treatment has a contraceptive side effect?

The Catholic answer uses the principle of double effect, the same principle formalized in LegitimateDefense.lean for self-defense. A medical treatment with a contraceptive side effect is permissible when all four conditions are met:

  1. The act itself is not intrinsically evil. Taking medicine for a medical condition is not evil. The act is “treating an illness,” not “contracepting.”
  2. The good effect is intended; the bad effect is not. The woman intends to treat her endometriosis. The temporary infertility is foreseen but not desired.
  3. The bad effect is not the means to the good. The infertility is not the mechanism by which the medicine heals. The medicine works on the underlying condition; the infertility is a separate pharmacological side effect.
  4. Proportionality. The medical benefit outweighs the side effect of temporary infertility.

The Lean theorem medical_treatment_permissible formalizes this directly: 

theorem medical_treatment_permissible
    (dec : DoubleEffectConditions)
    (h_not_evil : dec.actNotIntrinsicallyEvil)
    (h_intended : dec.goodIntended)
    (h_not_means : dec.badNotMeansToGood)
    (h_proportionate : dec.proportionate) :
    dec.isPermissible := by
  exact ⟨h_not_evil, h_intended, h_not_means, h_proportionate⟩

This is not a loophole. It is the principle working exactly as designed. The moral object of the act is “treating an illness” — a good object. The contraceptive effect is a side effect, not the purpose. Double effect permits tolerating a bad side effect when the act itself is good, the intention is good, the bad effect is not the means to the good, and the good outweighs the bad.

Practical note. This means a Catholic woman on hormonal medication for a genuine medical condition is not contracepting, even though the medication prevents conception. The moral object is different. Her confessor, spiritual director, and physician should all be involved in discerning the situation, but the principle is clear: medical treatment with a contraceptive side effect is morally permissible under double effect.

The Denominational Landscape

Here is the full picture of how Christian positions on conjugal ethics have shifted over time. The pre-1930 column represents the universal Christian consensus that held for nineteen centuries.

Position Pre-1930 All Catholic Today Catholic Protestant Today Protestant
Inseparability principle All accepted Still accepted (HV §12) Dropped (Lambeth 1930)
Contraception All rejected Still rejected (CCC §2370) Permitted in marriage
NFP Not yet developed Permitted for just reasons (HV §16) Unnecessary (contraception OK)
Family size “Be fruitful and multiply” Responsible parenthood (CCC §2368) Personal decision

The pattern is striking. The Catholic column is essentially unchanged from the pre-1930 consensus, with the addition of NFP as a permitted method for spacing (a development, not a reversal). The Protestant column represents a complete inversion — made possible by one axiom swap.

The Anglican Axiom Swap

What did the 1930 Lambeth Conference actually change? In the language of the Catlib project, it performed an axiom swap. Here is the precise mapping:

Dropped The Inseparability Principle

The unitive and procreative meanings of the conjugal act are inseparable; man may not break this connection on his own initiative.

This had been the universal Christian axiom for nineteen centuries. Lambeth 1930 did not explicitly say “we reject inseparability.” But by permitting contraception “where there is a morally sound reason,” it implicitly adopted the negation.

Added A1_SEPARABILITY

The unitive and procreative meanings can be deliberately separated when there is a morally sound reason. In the Lean formalization: 

def A1_SEPARABILITY
    (act : ConjugalAct)
    (justReason : Prop) : Prop :=
  justReason →
    (act.unitiveIntact ∧ ¬act.procreativeIntact → True)

Kept Other Moral Constraints

Resolution 15 preserved several constraints: sex should be within marriage, contraception should be by mutual consent, the decision should be made “in the light of Christian principles.” Lambeth did not endorse a free-for-all. It endorsed a principled exception — but the exception swallowed the rule within a generation.

Once you accept A1_SEPARABILITY, the Catholic position on contraception does not follow. And that is exactly what happened across Protestantism. If the two meanings can be separated for good reasons, then there is no principled objection to contraception within marriage. The only remaining questions are practical: which methods? under what circumstances? with what safeguards? These are important questions, but they are questions of prudence, not of principle. The principled objection died with the inseparability axiom.

The speed of the collapse is itself evidence of how much weight the inseparability principle was bearing. Once it was removed, the entire structure that depended on it fell. Within thirty years, virtually every major Protestant denomination had accepted contraception. Within fifty years, it was so thoroughly accepted that most Protestants were unaware it had ever been controversial.

The Catholic Church saw this coming. Casti Connubii (1930) explicitly warned that weakening the inseparability principle would lead to the acceptance of contraception, then sterilization, then abortion. Whether or not that slippery slope fully materialized is debatable. What is not debatable is that the Catholic Church identified the axiom swap correctly and refused to make it.

What the Catechism Actually Says About Family Size

Contrary to popular caricature, the Catholic Church does not teach that couples must have as many children as biologically possible. The Catechism teaches responsible parenthood — a concept that includes both generosity toward life and prudent consideration of the family’s circumstances.

Here is what the Catechism actually says:

  • No specific number is required. The Church has never taught that couples must have a certain number of children. “Responsible parenthood” is the standard.
  • Just reasons for spacing are permitted. CCC §2368: “For just reasons, spouses may wish to space the births of their children.” Just reasons include the mother’s health, economic hardship, and the welfare of existing children.
  • The method matters. Spacing must use methods that respect the inseparability principle (i.e., NFP, not contraception).
  • Selfishness is the wrong motive. CCC §2368 continues: “It is their duty to make certain that their desire is not motivated by selfishness.”
  • Conscience binds. The couple must follow their informed conscience (S9 from our axiom set). This means the final decision about family size rests with the couple, not with their pastor, bishop, or anyone else — provided their conscience is properly formed.
Hidden assumption: “just reasons” is undefined. In the Lean formalization, JustReasons is an opaque Prop — deliberately left undefined because the Catechism itself provides no decision procedure. The Catechism gives examples (health, economics, existing children) but never says exactly where the line is. Who decides what counts as “just”? The answer is: the couple’s informed conscience. This is a genuine gap in the formal system — and it is a gap the Catechism intentionally leaves open, trusting couples to exercise prudence.

The tension is real. On one hand, the Church holds that “sacred Scripture and the Church’s traditional practice see in large families a sign of God’s blessing and the parents’ generosity” (CCC §2373). On the other hand, the Church explicitly permits spacing for just reasons. The resolution lies in the word responsible: generous but not reckless, open to life but not blind to circumstances.

A Catholic couple with two children who are spacing for the mother’s health is doing nothing wrong. A Catholic couple with eight children who are open to more is doing nothing wrong. The Church trusts the conscience it demands be formed.

What the Formalization Reveals

Formalizing CCC §2366-2372 in Lean 4 exposed several things the Catechism text alone does not make visible:

  1. The inseparability principle is an axiom, not a theorem. It is asserted, not derived. The Catechism says it is “established by God” and grounded in natural law, but never provides a formal argument for why the two meanings are inseparable. Accepting or rejecting this axiom is the single decision that generates the Catholic or Protestant position on contraception.
  2. The NFP/contraception distinction is coherent but axiom-dependent. It depends on the object-independence axiom from SourcesOfMorality.lean. If you accept that the moral object of an act can be evaluated independently of consequences, the distinction makes sense. If you reject that axiom (consequentialism), the distinction collapses.
  3. The medical double effect case is the clearest application. Medical treatment with a contraceptive side effect is permitted under exactly the same framework that permits lethal self-defense. The four conditions of double effect map perfectly.
  4. The denominational fracture is a single axiom swap. Dropping the inseparability principle and adopting A1_SEPARABILITY is the one change that generates the entire Protestant position on contraception. Everything else follows.
  5. “Just reasons” is a free parameter. The Catechism provides examples but no formal definition. This is intentional: it delegates the decision to the couple’s conscience. But it means the formal system has an undefined term at a load-bearing position.

This teaching is difficult. It is the most practically contested point of Catholic moral theology, and no amount of formalization makes it pastorally easy. What formalization does is make the reasoning transparent: here are the axioms, here are the theorems, here is where the denominations diverge, and here are the hidden assumptions doing invisible work.

A person who rejects the inseparability principle is not being stupid. They are making a different axiom choice — the same choice the Anglican bishops made in 1930. A person who accepts it is not being rigid. They are holding an axiom that was universal in Christianity for nineteen centuries. Seeing this clearly is more honest than pretending either side has a knock-down argument.

The axiom set is the denomination. And the axiom that divides Christians on contraception is the inseparability principle — one axiom, with nineteen centuries of consensus behind it, and ninety-six years of fracture ahead.
