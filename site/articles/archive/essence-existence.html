The Deepest Hidden Assumption

Essence and Existence in the Catholic Catechism

What is the single most important philosophical assumption the Catechism never names?

⁂

The Catholic Catechism is a 2,865-paragraph document. It names God, Christ, the sacraments, the moral law, and prayer. It quotes Scripture thousands of times. It cites councils from Nicaea to Vatican II. And yet the single most important philosophical claim on which the entire structure depends — the claim that makes creation, contingency, divine simplicity, and sustaining providence coherent — is never stated anywhere in its pages.

That claim is the real distinction between essence and existence. In every creature, what it is (its essence, its nature) is distinct from that it is (its existence, its act of being). A horse’s “what it is to be a horse” does not include “actually existing.” Existence is something the horse receives, not something it has by nature.

Only in God are essence and existence identical. God does not have existence — God is existence itself. This is what “I AM WHO I AM” (Ex 3:14) means at its deepest metaphysical level: God’s essence is to exist.

When we formalized the Catechism in Lean 4, this distinction kept appearing as the invisible foundation beneath every major doctrine. Creation, contingency, simplicity, providence — all of them presuppose it, and none of them name it. The proof assistant forced us to make explicit what the Catechism treats as obvious. The result is in EssenceExistence.lean, and the finding is striking: this single distinction is to Catholic metaphysics what foundations are to a building — invisible, never mentioned in the upper stories, but everything stands on it.

❅

The Claim and Its Sources

This is not primarily a scriptural claim. It comes from Aquinas, specifically from De Ente et Essentia (chapters 4–5) and the Summa Theologiae (I, q.3, a.4). It is the deepest piece of Aristotelian-Thomistic machinery in the entire Catechism — and the CCC inherited it without citing it.

“I AM WHO I AM” (Ex 3:14)

Ecumenical Every Christian tradition reads God’s self-naming as a claim about divine aseity — God exists by nature, not by participation. God does not depend on anything else for being.

Catholic reading Aquinas takes this further: “I AM” reveals that God’s essence is existence itself (ipsum esse subsistens). God is not a being among beings who happens to exist necessarily. God is the act of being itself, in which all other beings participate. This is what §213 means when it says God “IS” — not just that God exists, but that God’s nature is to exist.

CCC §213: “God IS”

“God IS. He who is the fullness of Being and of every perfection, without beginning and without end.” The Catechism states the conclusion without giving the argument. The argument is Aquinas’s: if God’s essence is existence, then God is pure act, without potency, without beginning or end, the fullness of being. The CCC says “fullness of Being” and expects you to hear the metaphysics behind it.

CCC §271: Divine Simplicity

“In God, power, essence, will, intellect, wisdom, and justice are all identical.” (Lateran Council IV, cited in §271.) Why are they identical? Because God’s essence is existence, and pure existence admits of no internal differentiation. If there were parts in God, God would be composed — and composition requires a composer. The simplicity axioms in DivineAttributes.lean are downstream of the essence/existence identity.

CCC §290–301: Creation and Providence

“In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth.” Creation is the giving of existence to essences. But this only makes sense if essences and existence are really distinct in creatures. If a horse’s essence already included existence, creation would be tautological — existence giving existence to existences. The real distinction explains what creation is: the bestowal of existence on natures that do not have it on their own.

Similarly, §301 says God “upholds and sustains [creatures] in being” at every moment. Why do creatures need continuous sustaining? Because their essences do not include existence. If they did, creatures would be self-sustaining. The real distinction explains why Providence.lean’s S4 (universal providence) is not optional but necessary.

The CCC never names the essence/existence distinction. But §213 (divine being), §271 (simplicity), §290–301 (creation and providence) all presuppose it. Every time the Catechism says a creature “receives” being or grace, it is assuming that creatures are the kind of things that receive existence rather than having it intrinsically. This is Aquinas, not Scripture — and the CCC inherited it without acknowledgment.

Where They Agree

Before the disagreement, the common ground. It is more than most people on either side expect.

Fully Shared

  • God is self-existent. All classical Christian traditions affirm divine aseity — God exists by nature, not by participation or by the action of another. Ecumenical
  • Creatures are contingent. Everything that is not God could have failed to exist. No creature has being by its own right. Ecumenical
  • Creation is a free gift. God was not compelled to create. The world exists because God freely chose to give it being. Ecumenical
  • God sustains creation. Creatures depend on God not only for their origin but for their continued existence. Ecumenical
  • “I AM WHO I AM” is the deepest divine self-revelation. Exodus 3:14 reveals something fundamental about God’s nature. Ecumenical

The agreement is striking. Every consequence of the essence/existence distinction is shared across traditions. The dispute is about the metaphysical mechanism that explains these shared beliefs — not about the beliefs themselves.

The disagreement, precisely stated

All classical Christians affirm contingency, creation, divine self-existence, and sustaining providence. The question is: what metaphysical framework best explains why these are true? The Catholic-Thomistic answer is the real distinction between essence and existence. Most Protestants affirm the same consequences without committing to this specific metaphysical account. The disagreement is not about what we believe, but about how deep the philosophical explanation goes.

The Hidden Assumptions Formalization Exposed

Formalization does not merely encode what a text says. It forces you to state what the text assumes. Here are the assumptions the proof assistant would not let us avoid:

1. The distinction is real, not merely conceptual

The claim is not just that we can think about essence without thinking about existence (even Kant accepted that). It is a metaphysical claim: in the creature itself, the principle by which it is what it is and the principle by which it is are distinct components. This is the Thomistic position against Scotus (who held the distinction is merely formal) and Suarez (who held it is merely conceptual with a foundation in reality). The CCC implicitly sides with Aquinas without acknowledging the alternatives.

2. Existence is received, not self-originating

Creatures do not bootstrap themselves into being. If a creature’s essence does not include existence, then its existence must come from outside its own nature. “Received” implies a giver. The real distinction does not just say creatures lack self-existence — it implies they have existence from another. The axiom creatures_receive_existence makes this explicit.

3. Only one being can have essence = existence

If two beings both had essence = existence, they would be indistinguishable — existence itself is not individuated. Therefore God is unique. The theorem god_unique_essence_existence_identity proves that any being whose essence entails its existence must be divine. There cannot be two such beings.

4. Sustaining is continuous, not one-time

The CCC says God sustains creatures “at every moment” (§301). This means creation is not a past event but an ongoing act. If God stopped sustaining, creatures would not merely die — they would cease to exist entirely. The real distinction explains why: existence was never theirs intrinsically.

5. The creature/creator binary is exhaustive

Everything is either God or a creature. There is no third category — no being that is neither created nor divine. The axiom creature_or_divine makes this explicit. The Incarnation is not a counterexample: Christ has two natures, divine and human, in one person — the natures remain distinct per Chalcedon.

A philosopher who rejects the essence/existence distinction would need to rebuild Catholic metaphysics from scratch. Every doctrine that depends on creatures “receiving” being, on God being “simple,” on the world being “contingent” — all of these would need new foundations. The proof assistant makes the cost of rejection visible.

What Breaks Without It

The easiest way to see why the real distinction matters is to see what happens without it. If you deny that essence and existence are distinct in creatures, four central doctrines lose their metaphysical grounding:

Doctrine What the CCC Claims What Breaks Without the Distinction
Contingency Creatures could fail to exist (§302) If essence includes existence, creatures exist by nature and cannot fail to be. Contingency is unexplained.
Divine simplicity All attributes = essence in God (§271) Simplicity follows from essence = existence in God. Without the distinction, there is no argument for why God has no composition.
Creation God gives existence to creatures (§290–301) If creatures’ essences already include existence, “giving existence” is tautological. Creation has no metaphysical content.
Sustaining providence God upholds creatures at every moment (§301) If creatures have existence by nature, they are self-sustaining. Providence becomes optional or ceremonial.

The pattern is clear: without the real distinction, the CCC’s claims about creation and God’s relationship to creatures become assertions without metaphysical grounding. They remain true as statements of faith, but the philosophical architecture that connects them collapses.

⁂

Across Traditions

The essence/existence distinction has a complicated ecumenical status. Its consequences are shared by virtually all Christians. Its metaphysical formulation is distinctively Thomistic. Understanding this gap is essential for honest ecumenical dialogue.

Under Catholic-Thomistic axioms
The real distinction is the deepest metaphysical truth about creatures. It explains why creation, contingency, simplicity, and providence hold. The CCC inherits this framework from Aquinas, though it never names it. Within Catholic theology, Scotists and Suarezians offer modified versions of the distinction, but the Thomistic account is the one the CCC implicitly assumes.
Under Protestant axioms
Most Protestant theologians affirm contingency, creation, and divine self-existence without committing to the Thomistic metaphysical mechanism. The tendency is to ground these doctrines directly in Scripture and divine sovereignty rather than in a specific account of the internal structure of beings. Some Reformed philosophers (e.g., Alvin Plantinga) even question whether divine simplicity is coherent. The consequences are shared; the metaphysical scaffolding is not.
Under Orthodox axioms
The Orthodox tradition has a related but distinct framework: the Palamite essence/energies distinction. God’s essence is unknowable, but God’s energies (uncreated grace, divine activity in the world) are knowable and participable. This is not the same as the essence/existence distinction, but it serves a similar structural role — explaining how creatures participate in God without being identical with God.
❅

What This Article Cannot Answer

A proof assistant cannot tell you which axioms to accept. But it can show you what you are accepting and what follows from it. This article cannot answer:

  • Whether the real distinction is true. That is a metaphysical question, not a formal one. The formalization shows what follows if you accept it and what breaks if you reject it.
  • Whether Thomism is the only viable framework. Scotism, Suarezianism, and the Palamite tradition offer alternatives. The CCC happens to presuppose Thomism, but the question of which metaphysical framework is correct is not settled by formal verification.
  • Whether Scripture alone can ground these doctrines. The Protestant strategy of grounding contingency and creation in Scripture without Thomistic metaphysics may work. Whether it provides the same explanatory depth is a philosophical judgment, not a logical one.

Formal verification does not tell you who is right. It tells you: if you accept these premises, this conclusion follows. And it tells you what happens if you remove a premise. Seeing that clearly is better than arguing past each other.

The Invisible Foundation

The essence/existence distinction is to the Catechism what foundations are to a building: invisible from every floor, never mentioned in the text, but everything stands on it. The CCC says creatures are contingent, that God is simple, that creation is a gift, that providence sustains — and behind every one of these claims stands a single metaphysical principle it never names.

Both readings are internally consistent. The disagreement is not about whether creatures are contingent or whether God is self-existent — it is about how deep the philosophical explanation should go and which metaphysical framework best accounts for these shared convictions. Seeing this precisely is more honest than caricaturing either side.

The formalization revealed something else as well. The real distinction is to metaphysics what hylomorphism (P1) is to anthropology and simplicity is to theology — a claim about the internal structure of beings. All three say “this thing has a composition that that thing lacks.” Creatures: essence ≠ existence. Humans: body + soul. God: neither composition. The pattern is unmistakable: the more fundamental the being, the less composition it has. The CCC assumes this pattern throughout. The proof assistant makes it visible.
