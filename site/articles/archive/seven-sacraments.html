The Seven Sacraments, Formalized

Every assumption explicit. Every inference checked.

What does the sacramental system look like when you make a proof assistant verify all of it?

⁂

The Catholic Church teaches that there are exactly seven sacraments — no more, no fewer (§1210). They are the hinges of the liturgical life, the channels through which grace flows into the world. Ask a Protestant why they reject five of the seven, and the answer will often come down to a single word: Scripture. Ask a Catholic why they affirm all seven, and the answer will invoke Tradition and the Council of Trent.

But beneath the historical arguments, there is a structural question that neither side usually asks: What is each sacrament actually doing? Not “which verses support it” or “which council defined it,” but: what kind of change does it cause, what does it presuppose, and how does it relate to the others? If you had to write it all down with enough precision that a computer could check it, what would you discover?

We have now done exactly that. All seven sacraments of the Catechism are formalized in Lean 4, machine-checked, and connected to the fifteen base axioms. The proof assistant found things the Catechism never states as principles — an emergent layer correspondence, a single axiom that divides Catholic from Protestant sacramental theology, and a structural uniqueness in Anointing that no other sacrament shares.

❅

The Seven at a Glance

Every sacrament causes at least one type of real change under Catholic axioms. The proof assistant verifies this as every_sacrament_causes_change — a theorem that exhausts all seven cases. No sacrament is merely symbolic. That single result encodes the Catholic-Protestant divide more precisely than any catechetical paragraph: under T3, sacraments cause; without T3, they signify.

The formalization classifies sacramental effects into four types of change: ontological (what you are), relational (how you stand with God), juridical (your status before divine law), and dispositional (what you can now do). This taxonomy is our analytical tool — the Catechism uses all four modes of language without ever labeling them. Here is each sacrament’s profile:

Baptism

ontological relational juridical

New creature, adopted child of God, original sin removed. The richest causation profile of any sacrament — three types. Gateway to all the others (§1213).

Confirmation

ontological dispositional

Indelible character and full outpouring of the Spirit. Empowers the baptized for mission — “strength to spread and defend the faith” (§1303).

Eucharist

ontological relational

Transubstantiation of the elements and augmented union with Christ. The only sacrament where the ontological change is in the elements, not the recipient.

Reconciliation

relational juridical

Communion with God restored, guilt removed. But temporal punishment remains (§1472) — the thread that leads to the Reformation.

Holy Orders

ontological dispositional

Indelible character: “a priest forever” (Heb 5:6). The ontological mark enables the dispositional capacity to confect sacraments (§1581).

Marriage

relational

A bond created between spouses, sealed by God (§1639). The only sacrament whose primary effect is a relation between two persons rather than between a person and God.

Anointing of the Sick

dispositional relational

Spiritual strengthening and union with Christ’s Passion. Plus a conditional physical healing effect — the only sacrament whose primary effect depends on divine judgment about the recipient’s good (§1520).

⁂

The Emergent Layer Correspondence

The Catechism describes each sacrament’s effects independently. It never says “the three healing sacraments map to three layers of sin.” But when you formalize the sin model from SinEffects.lean and then formalize each sacrament’s primary target, the correspondence falls out:

Layer 1: Original wound → Baptism (§1263)
Layer 2: Guilt → Reconciliation (§1468)
Layer 3: Attachments → Eucharist (§1394)

The theorem all_sin_layers_have_sacramental_remedy verifies the completeness claim: every layer of sin has a sacramental remedy. No layer is left unaddressed. And the sacraments themselves create an ordering — the Eucharist requires what Reconciliation provides (state of grace), and Reconciliation requires what Baptism provides (membership in the Church). The sacramental system is not a menu. It is a sequence.

This layer correspondence is emergent — nowhere does the Catechism state it as a principle. It was discovered by the formalization, not read off the source text. The CCC describes each sacrament’s effects in its own section; the three-layer structure only becomes visible when you model all three simultaneously.
❅

One Axiom to Divide Them

The single most denominationally divisive axiom in the entire formalization is T3 — sacramental efficacy, or ex opere operato. Under T3, sacraments confer the grace they signify. Without T3, sacraments symbolize grace already received through faith.

This is not a vague theological claim. It is a precisely verifiable structural fact. The theorem t3_is_the_load_bearing_axiom shows the full chain for infant baptism: the infant has original sin, needs baptism, and if baptism confers grace (T3), the infant receives grace when baptized. Remove T3, and the argument fails at the first operative step.

The same pattern repeats across all seven sacraments. Every per-sacrament causation axiom in SacramentalCausation.lean is an application of T3 to a specific sacrament. The theorem every_sacrament_causes_change depends on these axioms, which depend on T3. Remove T3, and all seven reduce to symbols.

The denominational diagnosis

Catholic Accept T3: sacraments are effective signs — they cause what they signify. Baptism removes original sin. The Eucharist makes Christ present. Absolution confers forgiveness. Every sacrament causes real change.

Protestant Reject T3: sacraments are signs of grace received by faith. Baptism declares what God has done. The Lord’s Supper is a memorial. Confession is a practice, not a cause. The “causation” question does not arise.

A proof assistant cannot tell you whether T3 is true. It can tell you: if you accept T3, all seven sacraments cause real change and the entire Catholic sacramental system follows. If you reject T3, the system dissolves — not because it is refuted, but because its load-bearing axiom is removed. Both sides reason correctly from their premises.
⁂

Transubstantiation: The Only Substance/Accident Separation

Of the seven sacraments, six change the recipient. The Eucharist is different. Its primary ontological change is in the elements — bread and wine become the body and blood of Christ. The substance converts while the accidents (appearances) remain.

The formalization in Eucharist.lean models this through the EucharisticSubstance enumeration and the real_presence axiom: after valid consecration, the whole Christ is truly, really, and substantially present. The substance/accident distinction — drawn from P1 (hylomorphism) — is invoked only here among all seven sacraments. Baptism does not separate substance from accident. Neither does Reconciliation, Confirmation, Holy Orders, Marriage, or Anointing. Transubstantiation is structurally unique.

This gives transubstantiation its special place in ecumenical debate. Lutherans affirm real presence (consubstantiation — Christ present with the bread) but reject the substance-conversion model. Reformed Christians affirm spiritual presence. Zwinglians hold memorial only. Each position corresponds to a different metaphysical axiom about what happens at consecration. The axiom set is the denomination.

❅

Character Means Non-Repeatable

Three sacraments — Baptism, Confirmation, Holy Orders — imprint an “indelible spiritual mark” called a character (§1272, §1304, §1582). These are the three that cannot be repeated. The other four (Eucharist, Reconciliation, Anointing, Marriage) do not imprint a character and can be repeated.

The theorem character_iff_not_repeatable establishes the biconditional: a sacrament imprints a character if and only if it is non-repeatable. This is not a coincidence the CCC happened to observe. It is a structural equivalence verified across all seven sacraments. The indelible character is the reason for non-repeatability — the mark cannot be given twice because it cannot be removed.

Eucharist No Yes
Reconciliation No Yes

Anointing: The Only Conditional Sacrament

Every other sacrament’s primary effects are unconditional — given valid form and proper disposition, they always occur. Baptism always removes original sin. The Eucharist always makes Christ present. Reconciliation always restores communion. But Anointing of the Sick has one effect conditioned on divine judgment: “a restoration of health, if it is conducive to the salvation of his soul” (§1520).

The theorem anointing_uniquely_conditional verifies this as a biconditional across all seven sacraments: a sacrament has a conditional primary effect if and only if it is Anointing of the Sick. The proof exhausts all seven cases.

The conditionality is not weakness. The spiritual effects — strengthening, union with Christ’s Passion — are always conferred (anointing_always_achieves_spiritual_good). The conditional clause encodes a priority: spiritual goods over temporal goods. God might withhold healing because death is better for the person’s soul. This is the hidden assumption that gives the “if conducive” clause real content — physical health and eternal salvation can be in tension.

Five Faces of Non-Competition

P2 — two-tier causation, the principle that primary and secondary causes do not compete — does remarkable work across the sacramental system. It appears in at least five distinct domains:

Domain Primary Level Secondary Level
Causation (general) God causes Creatures cause
Prayer God acts Person prays
Intercession Christ mediates Saints intercede
Evil permission God permits Creature causes evil
Absolution God forgives Priest absolves

In each case, the same structural principle resolves the same apparent contradiction: more secondary-level activity does not mean less primary-level activity. The priest’s absolution does not diminish God’s forgiveness. The saint’s intercession does not diminish Christ’s mediation. The theorem priestly_absolution_coherent wires together all the components for the absolution case: God is the unique principal forgiver, the priest acts instrumentally, and the two levels do not compete.

The Protestant objection to priestly absolution is not a single rejection but three independent rejections: (1) the scope of the commission at Jn 20:23 (authority), (2) T3 / sacramental causation (mechanism), and (3) the ecclesial dimension of sin (purpose). A person can accept any one of these and reject the others. The Catholic position requires all three.

Where They Agree

Before the disagreements, the common ground. It is more than most people on either side expect.

Fully Shared

  • Baptism and the Lord’s Supper are dominical — instituted by Christ himself. Every major Christian tradition practices both.
  • Only God forgives sins (Mk 2:7) — no Christian tradition claims a human being forgives by his own power.
  • Grace is necessary for salvation — the dispute is over how grace arrives (through sacraments or through faith alone), not whether it is needed.
  • Marriage is sacred — even traditions that do not call it a “sacrament” treat it as a covenant before God.
  • Care for the sick is commanded (Jas 5:14–15) — the debate is whether anointing is a sacrament with ex opere operato effects or a prayer ministry.
  • Ministry exists — all traditions ordain or commission ministers. The disagreement is about what ordination does ontologically.

The disagreement, precisely stated

Catholics and Protestants do not disagree about whether grace is real, whether Christ instituted sacred rites, or whether the Church has ministers. They disagree about T3: whether those rites cause grace or signify grace already received by faith. Remove T3 from the Catholic axiom set, and the entire sacramental system reduces to what Protestants already affirm — sacred signs pointing to divine realities. Add T3, and the signs become causes, the ministers become instruments, and the system acquires its distinctive Catholic architecture.

What Formalization Exposed

A proof assistant forces you to write down every assumption. The Catechism does not. Here are the hidden assumptions the formalization brought to the surface:

1. The change-type taxonomy

The CCC describes sacramental effects in four modes of language — relational, ontological, juridical, dispositional — without ever classifying them. The formalization required us to make these categories explicit. This is our analytical framework, not the Catechism’s. But it reveals genuine structure: every change-type has at least one sacrament that instantiates it (every_change_type_has_sacrament).

2. The sacrament-layer correspondence is nowhere stated

Baptism targets Layer 1, Reconciliation targets Layer 2, the Eucharist targets Layer 3. This mapping is not in the CCC. The Catechism describes each sacrament in its own section and never connects the three healing sacraments to a unified sin model. The structure is real; the principle is silent.

3. Anointing’s hidden assumption: healing can harm salvation

The “if conducive to the salvation of his soul” clause (§1520) is only meaningful if there exist cases where healing would not be conducive. The CCC never says this explicitly. It simply says “if” — but the “if” only has force if the condition can fail. The hidden anthropology: continued earthly life brings continued temptation, and a person who dies in grace is safe.

4. The Church can act on behalf of a non-consenting member

Infant baptism presupposes that the Church is a corporate body that can act representatively — not merely a collection of individuals each acting alone. The CCC assumes this (§1251) without arguing for it. A strict individualist ecclesiology would reject it. This is the assumption that makes infant baptism possible after T3 is accepted.

5. Confirmation’s “completion” is genuinely ambiguous

The CCC calls Confirmation “necessary for the completion of baptismal grace” (§1285). But “completion” admits two readings: constitutive (adds what was missing) or developmental (matures what was planted). The CCC’s own language pulls in both directions. The separate indelible character (§1304) is the strongest evidence for the constitutive reading — if Confirmation merely deepened existing grace, a separate ontological mark would be otiose.

What This Cannot Answer

Formal verification proves logical validity given axioms. It does not prove axioms true. There are questions this formalization cannot touch:

  • Is T3 true? The proof assistant can show that T3 is the load-bearing axiom separating Catholic from Protestant sacramental theology. It cannot tell you whether to accept it.
  • Does transubstantiation actually occur? That is an empirical and metaphysical question, not a logical one.
  • Which axiom set is correct? Both the Catholic and Protestant positions are internally consistent. The disagreement is over premises, not over reasoning.
  • What does “indelible character” actually consist in? The CCC says it is “spiritual” and “permanent.” We track the claim without defining the metaphysics.

Seeing what follows from which axioms is more honest than arguing past each other. A proof assistant cannot tell you which axioms to accept. But it can show you what you are accepting and what follows from it.

The Architecture of Grace

What we found, across seven sacraments and eight Lean files, is this: the Catholic sacramental system is not a collection of isolated rituals. It is an architecture. The sacraments form a sequence (Baptism before Reconciliation before Eucharist), a hierarchy of change-types (ontological marks that cannot be undone, relational bonds that can be renewed), and a correspondence with the layers of sin that is nowhere stated in the source text but verified by the proof assistant.

The disagreement between Catholics and Protestants is not about logic. It is about whether T3 is among the axioms. Accept T3, and all seven sacraments cause real change, the priesthood is an instrumental cause of divine action, and the sacramental system acquires its full architecture. Reject T3, and the sacred rites become what Protestants have always said they are: meaningful signs of a grace received by faith alone.

Neither side is being foolish. Both sides reason correctly from their premises. Seeing that clearly is better than arguing past each other.
