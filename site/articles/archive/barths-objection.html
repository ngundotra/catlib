Barth’s Objection

Can we really formalize God?

If human language about God is only analogical, does this entire project mean anything?

⁂

In 1932, Karl Barth wrote that the analogia entis — the analogy of being — was “the invention of the Antichrist” and “the reason why one cannot become Catholic” (Church Dogmatics I/1, p. viii). This was not a casual remark. It was the sharpest theological rejection of the last century, aimed at the very foundation of Catholic natural theology: the claim that human reason, working from the created world, can say something genuinely true about God.

Barth’s objection strikes at the heart of what catlib attempts. Every axiom in this project applies a predicate to God — godIsLove, godCanBringAbout, knownToGod, godCreatesThruWisdom — and each of those predicates originates in human experience with creatures. If Barth is right that no analogy from creatures to God is legitimate, then every one of those predicates is a projection of human concepts onto a God who shatters all human concepts. And catlib would be not merely imprecise but fundamentally misconceived.

We formalized that finding. In AnalogyOfBeing.lean, the theorem barth_and_catlib_incompatible proves: if Barth’s rejection of the analogy of being is correct, then catlib’s methodology is unsound. We have picked a side. This article explains what that side is, why we picked it, and what we honestly cannot answer.

❅

The Strongest Version of the Objection

Barth’s objection is not that human language about God is imprecise. Every theologian admits imprecision. The objection is deeper: that any attempt to reason FROM creatures TO God — to say “creatures are good, therefore God must be good in some related sense” — presupposes that human reason has a reliable path to God. And for Barth, it does not.

Barth’s Core Claim

God is known only through God’s self-revelation in Jesus Christ. Human reason, no matter how disciplined, cannot reach God from below. The distance between Creator and creature is not a gap that analogy bridges — it is an abyss that only God can cross, and God crosses it in revelation alone.

The analogia entis (analogy of being) claims that because creatures are made by God, they resemble God, and this resemblance grounds our ability to speak truly about God. Barth says this gets the direction exactly backwards: it makes God accessible to human reason rather than making human reason dependent on God’s free decision to reveal himself.

This is not a misunderstanding. It is not a failure to read Aquinas carefully. Barth understood the Catholic position. He rejected it because he believed it domesticated God — made God an object of human inquiry rather than the free Subject who addresses us when and how he chooses.

If Barth is right, then every axiom in catlib that predicates something of God is a category error. Formal verification of theological claims would be like spell-checking a language that does not exist. The grammar would be perfect. The referent would be empty.
⁂

The theorem barth_and_catlib_incompatible is not a refutation of Barth. It is a proof that catlib has taken a position. The project assumes the CCC’s axiom set, which includes the claim that analogy of being works (§40–43). Barth’s axiom set denies this. Both sets are internally consistent. But they cannot both be true.

A proof assistant cannot tell you which axioms to accept. But it can show you what you are accepting and what follows from it. Here it shows: accepting catlib’s axioms means rejecting Barth’s position on analogy. Seeing that clearly is better than arguing past each other.

The Catechism’s Response: Analogy as Middle Way

The Catechism of the Catholic Church devotes §40–43 to this question. Its answer charts a middle course between two extremes, and that middle is the ground on which catlib stands.

Three Modes of Predication (Aquinas, ST I q.13 a.5)

When we say “God is good” and “this person is good,” the word “good” can mean one of three things:

Univocal — exactly the same meaning. “Good” means for God precisely what it means for creatures. The CCC denies this: §42 says “Our human words always fall short of the mystery of God.”

Equivocal — completely different meaning. “Good” applied to God has nothing to do with “good” applied to creatures. Barth’s position tends in this direction. The CCC denies this too: §43 says our language “really does attain to God himself.”

Analogical — a related but non-identical meaning, grounded in resemblance. This is the CCC’s position. Our language about God carries genuine but partial truth.

The ground of this analogy is stated in §41: “All creatures bear a certain resemblance to God… ‘For from the greatness and beauty of created things comes a corresponding perception of their Creator’” (Wis 13:5). Because creatures are made by God, they bear traces of their Maker. The resemblance is real — not projected by the mind — and it is the basis for saying something genuinely true about God from the creaturely side.

But §42 immediately qualifies: “Our human words always fall short of the mystery of God.” The resemblance is real but limited. God infinitely surpasses every creature, so every analogy limps. We can say “God is good” and mean something true. We cannot say “God is good in exactly the way a person is good.”

And §43 pulls the threads together: “In speaking about God like this, our language is using human modes of expression; nevertheless it really does attain to God himself, though unable to express him in his infinite simplicity.” The word “really” is load-bearing. The CCC insists this is not pious fiction. It is not merely useful language. Analogical predication touches the divine reality — genuinely, though never completely.

⁂

Where They Agree

Before the disagreement, the common ground. It is more substantial than most people on either side expect.

Fully Shared

  • God infinitely transcends all creatures. Neither Catholics nor Barthians think God is a bigger version of a creature. Both affirm the Creator-creature distinction is absolute.
  • Human language about God is radically inadequate. The CCC says our words “always fall short” (§42). Barth agrees, and goes further.
  • God’s self-revelation is necessary. Even the CCC (§38) says revelation is “morally necessary” for reliable knowledge of God, because sin weakens reason. Barth agrees, but makes it absolutely necessary rather than morally so.
  • Theological language must be used with humility. No tradition worth its salt thinks we have God figured out.
  • God is known through Jesus Christ. Both traditions affirm that the definitive revelation of God occurs in the person of Christ. The question is whether reason provides any reliable access prior to that revelation.

The disagreement, precisely stated

The CCC claims that creatures’ resemblance to God (§41) grounds a genuine — if limited — rational path from creation to Creator. Barth claims that no creaturely path reaches God; all genuine knowledge of God flows from God’s free self-revelation downward, not from human reason upward. The disagreement is not about whether God transcends creatures (both say yes) but about whether the creature-to-Creator direction carries any reliable truth.

The Permanent Gap: What Formalization Cannot Capture

Even granting the CCC’s position that analogy works, there remains a gap that formalization cannot close. This is not a temporary limitation awaiting a better proof. It is structural.

The Problem

Lean’s Prop type is binary: a proposition is true or false. When catlib states axiom godIsLove, this asserts “God is love” as a Prop — univocally true, full stop.

But the CCC says “God is love” is true analogically — genuinely but partially. Our words “really do attain to God himself” (§43) but “always fall short” (§42). There is no type in Lean for “analogically true.” A Prop does not have a mode of truth.

This means catlib’s formal system is stronger than what the CCC claims to know. Our axioms assert things about God with univocal precision. The CCC says those same things with analogical humility. The gap between formal truth (binary, univocal) and analogical truth (genuine but partial, non-binary) is a permanent methodological limit of formal theology.

We documented this honestly in AnalogyOfBeing.lean: every axiom about God (godIsLove, omnipotence, omniscience, and all the rest) implicitly assumes that formal predicates applied to God have genuine truth content. This is the deepest methodological assumption in the project — deeper than any individual axiom. It is the precondition for all axioms about God, not a consequence of them.

Formal verification does not tell you who is right. It tells you: if you accept these premises, this conclusion follows. Here it tells you something more: the formal system itself rests on a premise it cannot express — that analogical truth is genuine enough to formalize. If that premise is wrong, everything built on it is a castle in the air.
⁂

Divine Simplicity Makes the Problem Worse

There is a second, related problem that cuts even deeper. The CCC (§271) teaches divine simplicity: in God, “power, essence, will, intellect, wisdom, and justice are all identical” (Lateran Council IV). God does not have goodness the way a person has a quality. God is His goodness. His love, power, wisdom, and justice are not separate attributes composed together. They are the single, simple divine essence seen from different angles by our finite intellect.

Our formalization in DivineAttributes.lean does the opposite. It decomposes God into separate predicates: godIsLove, godCanBringAbout, knownToGod, godCreatesThruWisdom. Each predicate carries distinct information. That is what makes formal reasoning possible — you need separate propositions to derive conclusions. But divine simplicity says those predicates are, in God, identical. The decomposition that our methodology requires is the decomposition that the doctrine denies.

As we noted in DivineAttributes.lean, the formalization therefore enacts the problem it describes. Our inability to unify the attribute predicates without losing content is the formal analogue of the human intellect’s inability to grasp divine unity without multiple concepts. The methodology does not merely fail to capture simplicity — it demonstrates why simplicity is hard, by being an instance of the very limitation the doctrine describes.

The analogy of being explains why this decomposition is permitted: analogical predication gives genuine but partial access to a reality that is itself simple. Multiple predicates for one simple reality, each capturing a genuine aspect. Without analogy, simplicity would make God entirely unknowable. With univocity, simplicity would be incoherent.
❅

The Epistemological Package

Analogy does not stand alone. FaithAndReason.lean reveals the full structure of the CCC’s epistemology, and it is layered rather than competitive:

First, reason is genuinely reliable (§36, Vatican I) — grounded in the imago dei and the Logos who made the world intelligible. Second, reason is genuinely limited (§156) — some truths (Trinity, Incarnation) exceed its grasp. Third, reason is genuinely damaged (§37) — sin introduces errors. Fourth, faith addresses all three: it confirms reason’s reliability (§159), accesses truths beyond reason (§156), and corrects where sin distorts (§37).

The theorem barth_incompatible_with_ccc in FaithAndReason.lean proves that Barth’s denial of natural theology is formally incompatible with Vatican I’s claim that reason can know God from creation. Both cannot hold. The disagreement is real, not merely verbal.

But Barth does not deny that reason works for science or everyday life. He denies it can reach God. He would accept catlib’s formalization of logic, mathematics, even ethics. What he rejects is the step from “creatures are intelligible” to “God is intelligible through creatures.” That step is, for Barth, the Antichrist’s invention.

Under Catholic axioms (§36, §40–43)
Creatures resemble God (Wis 13:5). This resemblance grounds analogy. Analogy gives genuine but partial truth about God. Reason, aided by this analogy, can know God exists from the created world. Formal theology is possible, within acknowledged limits.
Under Barthian axioms (CD I/1)
No creaturely path reaches God. All knowledge of God comes from God’s self-revelation in Christ. What looks like “analogy of being” is actually human projection onto God. Formal theology using creaturely predicates is fundamentally misconceived.

Both readings are internally consistent. The disagreement is not about logic or about whether God transcends creatures — it is about whether the creature-to-Creator direction of knowledge is reliable. Seeing this precisely is more honest than caricaturing either side.

What Formalization Exposed

The proof assistant forced us to make five things explicit that are normally left implicit:

1. Analogy is the deepest assumption

Catlib has 15 named base axioms (P1–P3, S1–S9, T1–T3). But the assumption that analogical predication carries genuine truth content is deeper than any of them. It is the precondition for every axiom about God. Without it, S1 (“God is love”) might be meaningless.

2. The CCC never argues for analogy

§43 asserts that our language “really does attain to God himself” but never argues for this claim. The argument is Aquinas’s (ST I q.13 a.5–6): creatures are effects of God, effects resemble their cause, so creaturely perfections say something true about the cause. The CCC states the conclusion without the reasoning. Formalization made this gap visible.

3. The three modes are assumed to be exhaustive

Univocal, equivocal, and analogical predication are treated as covering all possibilities (Aquinas, ST I q.13 a.5). If there were a fourth mode of predication, the argument might not apply to all God-talk. This exhaustiveness is never defended; it is assumed.

4. Creaturely resemblance is assumed to be real, not projected

§41 says creatures bear “a certain resemblance to God.” A Kantian could say we impose the resemblance rather than discovering it. The CCC assumes the resemblance is in the things themselves. This is the vestigia Dei tradition, and it is doing more load-bearing work than is usually acknowledged.

5. The formal system overshoots the CCC’s claims

Every axiom in catlib treats God-predicates as univocally true. The CCC says they are analogically true. Catlib is therefore formally stronger than the CCC’s own epistemology. This is an honest methodological limit, not a bug to be fixed.

❅

What This Article Cannot Answer

Honesty requires naming the limits.

This article cannot tell you whether Barth or the CCC is right about analogy. The proof assistant shows that both positions are internally consistent and mutually exclusive. It cannot adjudicate between them. The disagreement is about which axioms to accept, and a proof assistant does not tell you which axioms to accept.

It cannot close the gap between formal truth and analogical truth. Lean has no type for “true in an analogical sense.” That gap is permanent. If someone builds a formal system with graded truth values or a type for analogical assertion, it would be a genuine advance. We do not have one.

It cannot prove that creatures genuinely resemble God. The claim in §41 (Wis 13:5) is a scriptural and philosophical commitment, not a formal result. If the resemblance is an illusion, the entire analogical apparatus collapses.

And it cannot answer whether divine simplicity is coherent. We can state the doctrine and demonstrate that our formalization enacts the difficulty it describes. We cannot resolve the difficulty. The question of whether “power, essence, will, intellect, wisdom, and justice are all identical” (§271) is ultimately a question about reality, not about logic.

⁂

A Genuine Humility

Catlib is a love letter to the Catholic intellectual tradition, written with precision. But a love letter is not a proof that the beloved exists. The entire project rests on the assumption that analogical predication carries genuine truth content when applied to God. If that assumption is wrong, the love letter is addressed to the author’s own imagination.

We believe the assumption is right — that creatures genuinely resemble their Creator, that human language genuinely attains to God even as it always falls short, and that formal theology is therefore possible within acknowledged limits. That is the CCC’s position (§40–43), and it is ours.

But we believe Barth deserves better than dismissal. His objection is not stupid. It is one of the most serious challenges in modern theology. It comes from a deep reverence for the freedom and transcendence of God — a reverence Catholics share, even if they draw different conclusions from it. Barth later softened his position, allowing an analogia fidei (analogy of faith) — that God can be known through God’s self-revelation in Christ, even if not through the analogy of being. The distance between analogia fidei and analogia entis may be smaller than Barth’s 1932 rhetoric suggests.

In the meantime, we have formalized what we can. We have been honest about what we cannot. And we have encoded the strongest objection to our own methodology as a theorem in the system itself: barth_and_catlib_incompatible. If Barth is right, the theorem proves it: catlib falls. That is not triumphalism. That is the kind of intellectual honesty a proof assistant is good at.
