DivineMode

inductive DivineMode where
  | sustaining
  | beatifying

SoulState

structure SoulState where
  sustained : Prop
  inBeatifyingCommunion : Prop
  purified : Prop

god_sustains_all

axiom god_sustains_all :
  ∀ (s : SoulState), s.sustained
-- §301, Col 1:17

hell_paradox_resolved

theorem hell_paradox_resolved :
    hellState.sustained ∧
    ¬hellState.inBeatifyingCommunion :=
  ⟨god_sustains_all hellState,
   fun h => absurd h (by simp [hellState])⟩

purgatory_is_transitional

theorem purgatory_is_transitional :
    purgatoryState.inBeatifyingCommunion ∧
    ¬purgatoryState.purified := by
  constructor
  · exact trivial
  · intro h
    exact absurd h
      (by simp [purgatoryState])

all_states_sustained

theorem all_states_sustained :
    heavenState.sustained ∧
    purgatoryState.sustained ∧ hellState.sustained :=
  ⟨god_sustains_all heavenState,
   god_sustains_all purgatoryState,
   god_sustains_all hellState⟩

The Three Afterlife States

def heavenState : SoulState :=
  { sustained := True
    inBeatifyingCommunion := True
    purified := True }

def purgatoryState : SoulState :=
  { sustained := True
    inBeatifyingCommunion := True
    purified := False }

def hellState : SoulState :=
  { sustained := True
    inBeatifyingCommunion := False
    purified := False }

The Two Modes of God

How a proof assistant resolved the deepest paradox in the Catechism

“In him all things hold together.” — Colossians 1:17

⁂

The Catholic Catechism says two things that, taken together, look like a flat contradiction. It says God sustains all existence at every moment — “in him we live and move and have our being” (Acts 17:28), “in him all things hold together” (Col 1:17). Without God’s sustaining power, nothing exists at all. Not a single atom, not a single soul. This is §301. It is not optional. It is the bedrock of Christian metaphysics.

And then it says hell is “the state of definitive self-exclusion from communion with God” (§1033). Separation from God. Permanent, irrevocable separation.

The Contradiction

If God sustains everything that exists, how can anything be separated from God? The sustaining claim is universal. The separation claim is specific. But you cannot be both sustained-by and separated-from the same being in the same sense. Something has to give.

We did not set out to find this. We were formalizing eschatology — the doctrines of heaven, hell, and purgatory — in the Lean 4 proof assistant. The proof assistant is merciless about consistency. You cannot assert both god_sustains_all and hell_is_separation_from_god without the type checker asking: separation in what sense?

It forced us to be precise. And the precision revealed something beautiful.

❅

The Resolution: Two Modes

God relates to creation in two distinct modes. This is not a new idea — it is implicit in Aquinas (Summa Theologiae I, q.8 and q.104) and runs through the entire Thomistic tradition. But it has never, to our knowledge, been stated as a formal structural principle and machine-checked for consistency. Here it is.

Mode 1: Sustaining

God holds everything in existence. This is the ground of all being. Without it, you do not fade or diminish — you simply are not. “In him all things hold together” (Col 1:17). “Sustaining all things by his powerful word” (Heb 1:3). “In him we live and move and have our being” (Acts 17:28).

Ecumenical You cannot be separated from sustaining mode. If you were, you would cease to exist. This is not a theological opinion; it is what the word “sustain” means.

Mode 2: Beatifying

God offers communion, love, friendship, and ultimately the beatific vision — seeing God “face to face” (1 Cor 13:12), knowing God “as he is” (1 Jn 3:2). This is the fullness of what humans were made for. It is the telos of the entire creation.

Ecumenical You can be separated from beatifying mode. You can refuse communion. And that refusal — that separation from God’s friendship while remaining in God’s existence — is hell.

The Resolution

Hell is not separation from God’s power. It is separation from God’s love-as-communion. The damned are sustained by God — they must be, they exist — but they are cut off from God’s friendship. Both statements in the Catechism are true. They are talking about different modes.

In Lean 4, this becomes the DivineMode inductive type: sustaining and beatifying. The theorem hell_paradox_resolved proves that hellState.sustained and ¬hellState.inBeatifyingCommunion hold simultaneously. The contradiction is gone. Not by weakening either claim, but by making both claims precise.

⁂

The Three States

Once you have the two modes, the three afterlife states stop looking like three arbitrary bins. They become the three possible combinations of sustaining mode, communion, and purification.

State Sustained? In Communion? Purified? Duration
Hell Ecumenical ✓ yes ✗ no ✗ no Permanent
Purgatory Catholic ✓ yes ✓ yes (assured) ✗ not yet Temporary
Heaven Ecumenical ✓ yes ✓ yes (full) ✓ yes Permanent

The table reads top to bottom as an increase in communion. Hell: sustained, nothing else. Purgatory: sustained, communion assured but not yet fully received — purification is underway. Heaven: sustained, fully in communion, fully purified. The structure is clean because it emerges from the two-mode distinction, not from ad hoc categorization.

The theorem all_states_sustained proves that the first column is always “yes.” God sustains the damned, the purifying, and the blessed alike. You cannot escape the ground of being.

The theorem purgatory_is_transitional proves the distinctive character of purgatory: in beatifying communion (the soul chose God; it died in grace) but not yet purified. This is why purgatory is temporary. Death finalized the choice — but purification is still needed before the soul can bear the full weight of the beatific vision.

❅

What This Unifies

The two-mode distinction is not an isolated result. It turns out to be the deepest structural principle in the entire Catlib project. Five previous findings, which we had treated as separate, all reduce to aspects of this one distinction.

Previous Finding How DivineModes Explains It
Hell paradox Separation from beatifying mode, not sustaining mode. The paradox dissolves once you distinguish the two senses of “separated from God.”
Purgatory Movement along the communion axis. Death finalizes the choice (communion: yes or no), not the purification. Purgatory is the completion of purification for souls whose choice is already settled.
Providence asymmetry God sustains beings that do evil without beatifying the evil. The two modes explain why God’s providence operates through sinful agents without God being the author of sin: sustaining mode is active; beatifying mode is not applied to the evil act.
P2 two-tier causation The two modes are two tiers. Sustaining mode is the primary causal tier (God as ground of being); beatifying mode is the relational tier (God as friend, lover, source of joy). The distinction in Axioms.lean is the same distinction, applied at a higher level.
P3 evil as privation Hell is the ultimate privation — existence with all good removed. Since God is the source of all good, separation from beatifying mode is the removal of all good. Hell is not a place where bad things happen to you. It is existence stripped bare.

The structural finding

One distinction — sustaining vs. beatifying — unifies eschatology, theodicy, and the metaphysics of evil into a single framework. We did not design this. The proof assistant forced us to resolve a contradiction, and the resolution turned out to be a unifying principle.

⁂

The Open Question

The two-mode model resolves the paradox of how the damned can be separated from God. But it raises a harder question: why does God sustain the damned?

If existence without communion is suffering — and the tradition uniformly says it is — then sustaining the damned is sustaining them in suffering. Why does God do this? Three answers compete in the tradition. The Catechism holds all three without resolving the tension among them. We formalized all three as competing axioms in DivineModes.lean.

Option 1: Existence is intrinsically good

Even diminished existence — even existence in hell — is better than non-existence. Being itself is a good. God sustaining the damned is an act of goodness, because the alternative (annihilation) would be the removal of a good.

Aquinas, Summa Theologiae I, q.5, a.2: “Being is itself a good.”

Option 2: Respect for freedom

Annihilating the damned would override their choice. If they chose to exist apart from God, God honors that choice by sustaining their existence. Love does not coerce — not even by annihilation. This connects directly to S1 (God is love, and love requires freedom) and T1 (libertarian free will).

C.S. Lewis, The Great Divorce: “The doors of hell are locked from the inside.”

Option 3: Justice requires experienced consequences

The damned must experience the consequences of their choice. Annihilation would remove the consequence — it would be, in a sense, an escape. Justice requires that the choice to reject God be met with the reality of what that rejection means.

Aquinas, Summa Theologiae Supp., q.99.

These three options are not mutually exclusive, and the tradition has never definitively chosen among them. We formalized each as a separate axiom (existence_is_intrinsically_good, respect_for_freedom_sustains, justice_requires_consequences) precisely because the Catechism leaves this open. Honest formalization means admitting where the tradition itself is underdetermined.
❅

The Spectrum of Communion

Here is what emerges when you step back and look at the whole picture. The afterlife is not three disconnected bins. It is a spectrum of communion with God.

Hell No communion
Purgatory Assured, being purified
Heaven Full communion
Sustained only Sustained + in communion
Degree of communion with God

Everything is sustained. The sustaining axis never varies — it is always “yes,” because god_sustains_all admits no exceptions. What varies is the other axis: how much of God’s friendship you receive.

At one end: nothing. Existence without any good. The ultimate privation. At the other end: everything. Face to face with the source of all being, all truth, all beauty, all love. And in between: the souls in purgatory, who have chosen God, who are assured of reaching the far end, but who are not yet ready to bear the full weight of that encounter.

This is structurally beautiful. It is not three categories bolted together by theological fiat. It is one axis (communion) varying continuously across a fixed background (sustenance). The three “states” are really three regions on the same spectrum. And the spectrum has a direction: toward God, or away.

Why this matters

The two-mode model gives the afterlife a geometry. It is not just a list of outcomes; it is a space with structure. That structure was always implicit in the tradition. The proof assistant made it explicit — not because it added something, but because it refused to let us get away with the ambiguity that hid the structure from view.

⁂

Discovery, Not Design

We did not set out to find a unifying principle for eschatology and theodicy. We set out to formalize a growing set of Catechism passages and connected doctrines and see if the proof assistant would find anything interesting.

It found a contradiction. We had to resolve it. The resolution required a distinction (sustaining vs. beatifying) that we had not planned. And that distinction, once in place, turned out to explain five other things we had already formalized.

This is what formal verification does at its best. It does not just check your work. It forces a level of precision that can uncover structure you did not know was there. The two-mode model of divine relation was always implicit in the tradition — Aquinas knew it, the Catechism presupposes it, preachers gesture at it when they say God is always “holding you in being.” But no one, as far as we can tell, had ever isolated it as a formal structural principle and shown that it unifies five doctrines into one framework.

A proof assistant is a strange tool for theology. But perhaps a tradition that produced Aquinas’s architectonic precision should not be surprised that a tool designed for architectonic precision finds something in it worth finding.
