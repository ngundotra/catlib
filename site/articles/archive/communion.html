What Is Communion?

Formalizing a concept the Catechism never defines

The word “communion” appears over 300 times in the Catechism. It never gets a definition.

⁂

The Catechism uses “communion” to mean at least three different things. A person can be in communion with God (§1024). The Church is a communion of saints (§946). A person can lose communion through mortal sin (§1855). Excommunication severs communion with the Church. The Trinity is communion — Father, Son, and Spirit in eternal mutual self-gift (§255).

When we first formalized these passages, we ended up with three separate representations of “communion” in our Lean code — each invented independently by the file that needed it, each with a different type:

The Three Communions (Before)

1. A structure in Basic.lean — CommunionWithGod with fields for person, lovesGod, and inGrace. Used by Hell.lean.

2. An opaque predicate in Axioms.lean — inCommunion : Person → Prop. A bare assertion that “this word exists.” No definition, no content, no relation. Used by the base axiom S5 (sin separates) and bridge theorems.

3. A field on a structure in DivineModes.lean — SoulState.inCommunion : Prop. Here it meant “the beatifying mode is active.” Used by the heaven/hell/purgatory definitions.

Three concepts, three types, zero connection between them. The Lean compiler was perfectly happy — the names resolved by namespace, so they never collided. But they should have been the same thing. A proof assistant won’t tell you when two things should be related but aren’t. That requires a human noticing.

❅

What Communion Actually Is

Communion, from the Latin communio (“sharing in common”), is a mutual participatory relationship between two parties. That’s the dictionary definition, and it turns out to be exactly the right type.

Not a property of one person. Not a structure with fields. A relation — binary, taking two arguments. You are in communion with someone.

The key insight: communion is not a state you’re in. It’s a relationship you’re in with.

Once you see it as a binary relation, the structural properties fall out naturally:

Properties of Communion

Symmetric. If I am in communion with you, you are in communion with me. Communion is mutual by definition — it is shared participation.

Not reflexive for persons. You cannot be in communion with yourself. Communion requires an other. Self-communion is not a meaningful theological concept for creatures.

Reflexive for God. The Trinity is communion. Father, Son, and Spirit are in eternal mutual communion. God is in communion with Godself (§255).

Reflexive for the Church. The communion of saints (§946) — the Church is in communion with itself.

❅

Definition, Relation, Claim

The unified formalization has three clean layers, each in its proper place:

Layer 1: Vocabulary Basic.lean

Who can be in communion? The CommunionParty type: a person, God, or the Church. This is pure vocabulary — no claims, no axioms, just the cast of characters.

Layer 2: The Relation Axioms.lean

What is communion? A binary relation inCommunion : CommunionParty → CommunionParty → Prop with the structural axioms above (symmetry, non-reflexivity for persons, reflexivity for God and Church). Still no theological content — just the relational structure.

Layer 3: The Theological Claim DivineModes.lean

What does communion with God mean? A bridge axiom connects the abstract relation to its theological content: being in communion with God is God’s beatifying mode being active toward that person. This is the Catholic claim — the claim that makes hell, purgatory, and heaven fall out as three points on a spectrum of communion.

Each layer does exactly one thing. The vocabulary doesn’t make claims. The relation doesn’t interpret. The theological axiom connects the abstract to the concrete. If you disagree with the Catholic interpretation of what communion with God means, you swap Layer 3. Layers 1 and 2 are ecumenical.

❅

What This Fixes

The old formalization had three problems that the new one solves.

Hell.lean used to say “without free will, there does not exist a CommunionWithGod structure where this person loves God.” That’s not how anyone thinks about losing communion. Now it says: ¬ inCommunion (.person p) .god. You lose communion with God. Simple.

Sin.lean and Reconciliation.lean used a bare predicate inCommunion p that said “this person is in communion” without specifying with whom. Now it says inCommunion (.person p) .god — explicit that sin breaks your communion with God specifically, not communion in general. A person in mortal sin can still be in communion with the Church (until excommunication) and in communion with other persons (friendship survives sin).

DivineModes.lean had an inCommunion field on a structure that happened to share a name with the base predicate in Axioms.lean but was a completely unrelated declaration. Now the field is called inBeatifyingCommunion (the specific mode), and a bridge axiom connects it to the global relation: beatifying communion is communion with God.

❅

The Spectrum of Communion

Once communion is formalized as a relation, the afterlife states stop being three disconnected bins and become points on a spectrum:

State Sustained? In Communion with God? Purified?
Heaven Yes Yes (full) Yes
Purgatory Yes Yes (assured) Not yet
Hell Yes No No

God sustains everything — you cannot be separated from sustaining mode. The only axis that varies is communion. Hell is ¬ inCommunion (.person p) .god. That’s all it is.

❅

Where Denominations Diverge

Layers 1 and 2 are ecumenical. Every Christian tradition agrees that communion is a mutual relationship, that it involves God and persons, and that sin disrupts it. The disagreements are all in Layer 3.

Catholic Communion with God = beatifying mode active. Grace transforms. Sacraments are instruments that restore communion (Reconciliation). The Church mediates.

Lutheran Communion with God = faith in Christ’s promise. Grace declares righteous (forensic). Communion is restored by faith alone, not through a sacramental instrument.

Shared Sin breaks communion with God. Love requires freedom. The Trinity is eternal communion. The Church is a communion of saints. All of this is in Layers 1–2.

The formal structure makes the disagreement precise: it is not about what communion is but about how it is restored and what constitutes it theologically.

❅

What We Learned

The Catechism uses “communion” over 300 times without once defining it. When we formalized the passages that depend on it, we independently invented three separate types. The proof assistant didn’t catch this — it was perfectly happy with three unrelated declarations that happened to share a name. A human had to notice.

The fix was to ask the dictionary: communion is a mutual participatory relationship between two parties. That single insight — inCommunion : CommunionParty → CommunionParty → Prop — unified hell, purgatory, heaven, excommunication, the Trinity, the communion of saints, and the sin/grace dynamic under one relation.

Sometimes the proof assistant finds the hidden assumption. Sometimes you have to find it yourself and then let the proof assistant hold you to it.
