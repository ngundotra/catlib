The Hidden Geometry of Grace

Four theological concepts. One mathematical shape.

When we pointed a proof assistant at the Catholic Catechism, we found something we did not expect: culpability, love, freedom, and grace all have the same underlying structure.

⁂

This article is about a discovery. Not a discovery we set out to make, but one that emerged when we tried something unusual: we took the reasoning in the Catholic Catechism and fed it into a proof assistant — a computer program that checks whether conclusions actually follow from premises. Think of it as a calculator for reasoning instead of numbers. You type in your assumptions, you type in your claim, and the machine tells you whether the claim follows — or whether you are smuggling in something you have not stated.

We started with a specific question about sin. What we found was a pattern. And then we found the same pattern again in a completely different part of the Catechism. And again. And again. Four different theological concepts — culpability, love, freedom, and grace — all turned out to have the same mathematical shape.

This is the story of that pattern, told without any technical prerequisites. If you know that calculus exists and that math can describe the physical world, you have everything you need.

❅

A Calculator for Reasoning

Before the pattern, a word about the tool. Most people think of math as being about numbers. But there is a branch of mathematics that is about structure — the study of how things relate to each other, what can be compared to what, and what follows from what.

A proof assistant is software that works in this structural world. You tell it your starting assumptions (axioms), and then you try to prove claims. The machine will not let you skip a step or sneak in an assumption you have not declared. It is brutally honest. If your conclusion does not follow from your stated premises, the program refuses to accept it — and it tells you exactly what is missing.

We used a proof assistant called Lean 4 to formalize parts of the Catholic Catechism. “Formalize” means: take the Catechism’s claims and reasoning, translate them into precise mathematical language, and let the machine check whether the conclusions follow. This project is called Catlib.

The proof assistant does not tell you whether the Catholic faith is true. It tells you: if you accept these starting premises, then these conclusions follow. It separates the question of logic from the question of belief. And in the process, it reveals structure that is invisible to the naked eye.

⁂

It Started with Sin

The Catechism distinguishes between mortal sin (which destroys your relationship with God) and venial sin (which wounds it but does not destroy it). Most people think of this as a simple binary: either your sin is mortal or it is not. A light switch — on or off.

But when we formalized the Catechism’s actual reasoning, we found something richer. The Catechism says mortal sin requires three things: grave matter, full knowledge, and complete consent. And then it lists factors that diminish these — fear, habit, ignorance, psychological pressure, affective immaturity. The word “diminish” is key. You do not “diminish” something that has only two values. You diminish something that lives on a spectrum.

So culpability is not a light switch. It is more like temperature — a continuous spectrum, with the mortal/venial boundary acting like the freezing point of water. Above the threshold: mortal sin. Below it: venial. The threshold is real, but the underlying reality is a gradient, not a binary.

And the gradient is not one-dimensional. It lives in a three-dimensional space: knowledge, consent, and the gravity of the act. Some pairs of sinners cannot be compared — like trying to decide whether a tall short person is “bigger” than a short tall person. The comparison does not have a single answer. Mathematicians call this a partial order: some things can be compared, and some genuinely cannot.

The mortal/venial boundary is not a line. It is a surface in three-dimensional space — like a phase transition in physics, separating two qualitatively different states.

This was already surprising. The Catechism implies a precise mathematical structure that no one had spelled out before. But the real surprise was what came next.

❅

The Pattern Repeats

When we formalized love, we found the same shape. The Catechism uses “love” to mean at least four different things: agape (selfless love, charity), eros (desire and attraction), philia (friendship), and self-love. Each is a separate dimension. Each admits of degrees. And just like culpability, the different kinds of love cannot always be ranked — is a person with deep friendship but little charity “more loving” than someone with burning charity but few friends? The comparison breaks down. It is a partial order again.

When we formalized freedom, the same shape appeared a third time. The Catechism does not treat freedom as binary (free or not free). It treats freedom as graded. A person struggling with addiction is less free than someone without that burden, but more free than someone under total coercion. And here is the part that surprises people: the Catechism says that more goodness means more freedom. Perfect freedom is not the ability to choose evil. It is the state where you are so fully aligned with the good that evil holds no attraction at all. The saints are more free, not less. Freedom is a spectrum, oriented toward an endpoint.

And when we formalized grace — God’s healing action in the soul — there it was again. The Catechism teaches that original sin wounded four faculties: the intellect (darkened), the will (weakened), the desires (disordered), and the body (subject to suffering). Grace heals each of these independently and progressively. You can have a well-formed intellect but a weak will. You can have strong desires oriented toward the good but a body that still suffers. Each faculty lives on its own spectrum from wounded to healed. The complete healing of all four — what the tradition calls heaven — is the point where all four reach their maximum: (1, 1, 1, 1).

With a striking exception: the body stays wounded (locked at zero) until the resurrection. A saint in heaven before the general resurrection is at (1, 1, 1, 0). Only after the resurrection of the body does the healing become complete.

⁂

Four Spaces, One Pattern

Here is the discovery, laid out in one view. Four theological concepts. Each one a multi-dimensional space with its own axes. Each one graded, not binary. Each one a partial order. And each one has the same open question at its heart.

Domain Dimensions What We Found The Open Question
Culpability Knowledge × Consent × Matter Partially ordered, not binary. The mortal/venial boundary is a surface, not a line. How do diminishers (fear, habit, anxiety) combine?
Love Agape × Eros × Philia × Self-love Not symmetric — you can love enemies (agape without philia). Mortal sin destroys only agape; eros, philia, and self-love survive. How do the four kinds compose in conjugal love?
Freedom Level × Can-choose-evil × Oriented-to-good Graded, not binary. More good = more free. Perfect freedom = inability to sin. Where is the threshold between free and coerced?
Grace / Healing Intellect × Will × Desires × Body Four faculties healed independently and progressively. Heaven = (1,1,1,1). Body locked at 0 until resurrection. How do sacraments map to per-faculty healing?

All four domains have the same open question: what is the composition operator? The Catechism asserts that these relationships exist but never specifies the algebra — the recipe for how the pieces combine. Same gap, four times.

To make this concrete: if a person acts out of fear and habit and anxiety simultaneously, how do those three factors interact to reduce their culpability? Do they add up? Multiply? Does the worst one dominate? The Catechism says each factor diminishes culpability, but it never says how they combine. It is like being given a list of ingredients but not the recipe.

The same gap appears in love (how do agape and eros combine in marriage?), in freedom (where exactly does the line between diminished freedom and no freedom fall?), and in grace (does confession heal the will more than the intellect? does the Eucharist heal the desires?). Four different theological domains, four instances of the same structural silence.

❅

Not Four Systems — One

The pattern would be interesting even if the four domains were independent. But they are not. The proof assistant revealed that they are connected — different faces of the same underlying structure, like different views of the same building.

How the Four Domains Connect

Freedom → Your freedom level is your will-healing level in the grace framework. Freedom is not separate from grace — it is grace measured along one axis.
Love → Your agape (charity) degree is your will-healing expressed as love. The capacity for selfless love is not a different thing from a healed will — it is the same thing, named differently.
Desire → The capacity for properly ordered desire (what the tradition calls “nuptial capacity”) is the desire-healing level. Conjugal ethics is not a separate system — it is grace measured along the desire axis.
Culpability → The mortal sin threshold depends on your freedom level, which depends on your healing level. The four domains are not parallel — they are nested.

This is like the moment in physics when people realized that electricity and magnetism are not two separate forces but two aspects of one force — electromagnetism. The proof assistant did not just find four similar structures. It found that they are the same structure, measured along different axes.

Making It Tangible

If this still feels abstract, here are some analogies that may help.

Temperature, not a light switch

We are used to binary labels: hot or cold, guilty or innocent, free or unfree. But the reality underneath is a spectrum. Water is not “hot” or “cold” — it has a temperature. The freezing point is real (water genuinely becomes ice), but the underlying reality is continuous. That is what the Catechism describes: real thresholds (mortal vs. venial, free vs. coerced) imposed on continuous spectrums.

Ingredients without a recipe

Imagine someone hands you flour, eggs, sugar, and butter and says: “These combine to make something.” That is true. But how they combine matters enormously — you could get cake, cookies, or bread depending on proportions and method. The Catechism gives us the ingredients (fear, habit, ignorance all diminish culpability) but not the recipe (how they combine). The proof assistant identified exactly where the recipe is missing.

A fully healed patient

Think of someone recovering from an illness that affected their vision, their strength, their coordination, and their endurance. Each recovers independently — you might have sharp eyes but still be weak. Full health is the point where all four are restored. That is what heaven looks like in this framework: (1, 1, 1, 1) — intellect clear, will strong, desires rightly ordered, body glorified. The endpoint of a healing process, not a location you are teleported to.

Newton’s unification

Before Newton, people thought the force that makes apples fall and the force that keeps the moon in orbit were different things. Newton showed they are the same force — gravity — just measured at different scales. The proof assistant did something similar here: it showed that culpability, love, freedom, and grace, which seem like separate topics in the Catechism, are actually different measurements of one underlying structure.

What the Proof Assistant Found — and What It Did Not

Let us be precise about what we are claiming and what we are not.

What we found: The Catechism contains mathematical structure that has never been made explicit. Four different theological concepts share the same shape: multi-dimensional, graded, partially ordered spaces with a shared open question about how their dimensions compose. These four spaces are connected — they are different views of one deeper system.

What we did not find: The recipe. The Catechism tells us the shape of these spaces but not the algebra — the rules for how the pieces combine. This is not a failure of our analysis. It is a genuine gap in the source material. The Catechism asserts that fear diminishes culpability, that agape and eros both operate in marriage, that grace heals the intellect and the will. But it does not say how these combine. Every confessor, every spiritual director, every married couple is implicitly working with a composition model that the Catechism does not provide.

What we cannot say: Whether the Catechism is right. The proof assistant checks logic, not truth. It says: if you accept these axioms, then this structure follows. Whether to accept those axioms is a question of faith, not mathematics.

The proof assistant found both the structure and the silence. Both matter. The structure is beautiful — more coherent than most people expect. The silence is honest — a genuine open question, not a defect, and one that formal analysis made visible for the first time.

Why This Matters

You might wonder: who cares about the mathematical structure of theology? Here is why it matters.

The Catechism is a document read by over a billion people. Its reasoning about culpability affects how confessors evaluate sin. Its reasoning about freedom affects how the Church thinks about moral responsibility. Its reasoning about grace affects how people understand their own spiritual development. And its reasoning about love shapes how Catholic marriages are understood.

In all four of these areas, the Catechism is doing something more sophisticated than most readers realize. It is not saying “guilty or innocent.” It is not saying “free or unfree.” It is describing graded, multi-dimensional spaces with real thresholds — and then leaving the hardest question (how the dimensions interact) to pastoral judgment.

Knowing that this structure exists does not replace pastoral judgment. But it does clarify what that judgment is doing. A confessor evaluating someone who acted under fear, habit, and psychological duress is not just “making a call.” They are implicitly choosing a composition operator in a partially ordered space. Seeing that clearly is better than doing it blindly.

And the fact that four different theological concepts share the same mathematical shape suggests something about the Catechism itself: it may be more unified, more internally coherent, than even its authors knew. The hidden geometry was there all along. The proof assistant just made it visible.
