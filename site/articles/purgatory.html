How Do We Know Purgatory Exists?

The canon question, the hell tension, and what a proof assistant found

⁂

Purgatory is not in the Bible.

That is the Protestant objection, and it deserves a serious answer. Not a defensive one. Not a dismissive one. A precise one.

The Catlib project formalized CCC §1030–1032 (the Catholic doctrine of purgatory) in the Lean 4 proof assistant, alongside the doctrine of hell (§1033). The results were illuminating: the case for purgatory depends on which Bible you use, creates a genuine tension with hell that the Catechism never addresses, and rests on hidden assumptions about holiness, time, and prayer that deserve to be seen clearly.

This article walks through what the proof assistant found.

❅

It Depends on Which Bible

The Protestant says “purgatory is not in the Bible,” and under the 66-book Protestant canon, that claim has real force. But the Catholic Bible has 73 books. And the strongest verse for purgatory is in one of the seven books Luther removed.

2 Maccabees 12:46 — The Strongest Text

Catholic canon “It is a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead, that they may be loosed from sins.”

This verse explicitly endorses prayer for the dead and implies that the dead can be “loosed from sins” after death. If this is Scripture, purgatory has direct biblical support.

But 2 Maccabees is a Deuterocanonical book — one of the seven that Luther excluded using the Hebrew Bible as his Old Testament standard. Under Luther’s L4 axiom (the canon is determinable without Church authority), this verse is not Scripture. The strongest textual support vanishes.

This is one of the clearest examples in all of theology of how the canon question (L4 from Luther’s Axioms) cascades into a specific doctrine. Accept the 73-book canon, and purgatory has direct scriptural support. Accept the 66-book canon, and the scriptural case becomes contested interpretation.

Under the 66-book canon, the remaining scriptural evidence is:

1 Corinthians 3:13–15

Shared canon “Each one’s work will become manifest, for the Day will disclose it, because it will be revealed by fire, and the fire will test what sort of work each one has done… If anyone’s work is burned up, he will suffer loss, though he himself will be saved, but only as through fire.”

Catholic reading The “fire” that purifies while the person is “saved” is purgatorial fire — a purification that results in salvation.

Protestant reading The “fire” is the fire of final judgment that tests works, not a post-mortem purification. The person is saved despite poor works, not purified through a process.

Matthew 12:32

Shared canon “Whoever speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come.”

Catholic reading The phrase “in the age to come” implies that some sins ARE forgiven in the age to come — which requires a state of post-mortem purification.

Protestant reading The phrase is emphatic — “not even in the next age” — not an implication that other sins are forgiven after death.

Revelation 21:27

Shared “Nothing unclean will enter it.” Both traditions agree: holiness is required for heaven. The question is HOW that holiness is achieved — by a post-mortem purification (Catholic), or by Christ’s imputed righteousness at justification (Protestant).

The Tension with Hell

The proof assistant found something the Catechism does not address. In Hell.lean, the formalization declares:

Hell.lean, Axiom 3
death_is_final — No post-mortem change of state. Death is a hard boundary. The Catechism treats it as absolute.

But in Purgatory.lean, we must declare:

Purgatory.lean, Axiom 3
post_mortem_purification — Those who die in grace but imperfectly purified undergo purification AFTER death.

These appear to contradict each other. If death is final and no post-mortem change is possible, how can post-mortem purification occur?

The Catholic resolution requires a distinction that the Catechism never makes explicit: death is final for the choice (you cannot switch from rejecting God to accepting Him), but not final for purification (you can be cleaned up after choosing God). Purification is not a change of will — it is the completion of a will already oriented toward God.

This is formalized in Purgatory.lean as death_finalizes_choice_not_state — a new axiom required to make both doctrines coexist without contradiction. The proof assistant forced this distinction to the surface. The Catechism assumes it silently.

The structural consequence: the hell-purgatory boundary is absolute. Theorem hell_purgatory_boundary proves that no one who died in mortal sin can enter purgatory. And theorem grace_and_mortal_sin_exclusive proves that dying in grace and dying in mortal sin are mutually exclusive. The choice is binary and final, even though holiness is a spectrum.

Where They Agree

Before the disagreement, the common ground. It is more than most people on either side expect.

Fully Shared

  • Holiness is required for heaven. Rev 21:27 is in both canons. Both traditions affirm that nothing impure can enter God’s presence. Axiom 1 (holiness_required_for_heaven) is tagged Ecumenical.
  • Salvation is by grace. Neither side claims humans earn their way to heaven. The person in purgatory is there because of God’s grace, not despite it.
  • Hell is real and permanent. Both traditions hold that those who finally reject God are permanently separated from Him.
  • The saved are truly saved. Both traditions affirm that those in God’s grace will see Him. The Catholic adds a purification step; the Protestant says Christ’s righteousness handles it at justification.

The disagreement, precisely stated

The disagreement is not about WHETHER holiness is needed for heaven (both agree), or WHETHER salvation is by grace (both agree), or WHETHER hell is real (both agree). The disagreement is about when and how the holiness required for heaven is achieved: at the moment of justification by imputed righteousness (Protestant), or through a process that may continue after death (Catholic).

The Hidden Assumptions

The proof assistant identified five assumptions that the Catechism treats as obvious but never argues for.

# Hidden Assumption Why It Matters
1 Holiness is a spectrum, not binary If holiness were binary (holy / not-holy), purgatory would be unnecessary. You either have it or you don’t. Purgatory requires degrees.
2 Post-mortem change is possible In tension with death_is_final from Hell.lean. Resolved only by distinguishing choice-finality from state-finality.
3 Temporal process after death Purification takes “time” in some sense. How does time work for a disembodied soul? The Catechism never says.
4 Prayer crosses the death boundary The living can aid the dead. This is a strong metaphysical claim, assumed without argument.
5 The 73-book canon The strongest scriptural support (2 Macc 12:46) requires the Deuterocanonical books. Under the 66-book canon, the exegetical case is significantly weaker.
None of these assumptions are unreasonable. But they are assumptions. A proof assistant does not judge them — it makes them visible. And visibility is the first step toward honest conversation.

The Axiom Dependencies

Here is the complete dependency map for the purgatory doctrine, showing which base axioms from Axioms.lean it requires:

Axiom Role Scope
S8 Grace is transformative — purification is a real change in the person, not merely a legal declaration Catholic
T3 Sacramental efficacy — the Eucharistic sacrifice offered for the dead actually aids their purification Catholic
L4 The canon question — 73 vs. 66 books determines whether 2 Macc 12:46 counts as Scripture Disputed
S5 Sin separates from God — impurity must be removed before communion is complete Shared

The load-bearing axioms are S8 (transformative grace) and L4 (which Bible). If grace is merely forensic (Lutheran L2), then Christ’s righteousness covers the believer completely at justification, and no further purification is needed. If the canon excludes 2 Maccabees (Lutheran L4), the strongest scriptural warrant disappears.

Purgatory, more than almost any other doctrine, shows how Luther’s axiom modifications cascade. Change L2 (forensic justification) and L4 (66-book canon), and purgatory becomes both theologically unnecessary and scripturally unsupported. Keep the Catholic axioms, and purgatory follows with logical coherence.

The Honest Answer

How do we know purgatory exists? The honest Catholic answer is: we know it the same way we know the canon of Scripture — through the Church’s teaching authority, grounded in Scripture and Tradition together.

The Protestant who says “purgatory is not in the Bible” is right — under the 66-book canon. The Catholic who says “yes it is” is also right — under the 73-book canon. The disagreement about purgatory is, at its root, a disagreement about the canon. And the disagreement about the canon is a disagreement about authority.

The proof assistant added one more insight: purgatory creates a genuine tension with the doctrine of hell that requires a distinction the Catechism never states. Death must be final for choices but not for purification. That distinction is theologically defensible, but it is a hidden assumption — and making hidden assumptions visible is what formal verification does best.

Neither side is being foolish. The Protestant is guarding a real principle: sufficiency of Christ’s work. The Catholic is guarding a different real principle: the holiness required to stand in God’s presence. Both are reading Scripture. They are reading different Scriptures, under different axioms, and reaching different conclusions — each with logical coherence.

Seeing that clearly is better than arguing past each other.
