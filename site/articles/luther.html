structure JustificationModel where
  justType : JustificationType
  humanRole : HumanRole
  instrument : JustificationInstrument
  isProcess : Bool

catholicModel

def catholicModel : JustificationModel :=
  { justType := .transformative
    humanRole := .cooperation
    instrument := .baptism
    isProcess := true }

protestantModel

def protestantModel : JustificationModel :=
  { justType := .forensic
    humanRole := .receptionOnly
    instrument := .faithAlone
    isProcess := false }

models_differ_on_three_axes

  catholicModel.justType
    ≠ protestantModel.justType ∧
  catholicModel.humanRole
    ≠ protestantModel.humanRole ∧
  catholicModel.instrument
    ≠ protestantModel.instrument :=
  by refine ⟨?_, ?_, ?_⟩
     <;> intro h
     <;> simp [catholicModel,

Luther's Axioms

The Reformation as axiom swap

What if we could see exactly where Catholics and Lutherans agree and disagree — not in vague terms, but with mathematical precision?

⁂

For five centuries, Catholics and Lutherans have argued about justification, Scripture, grace, and the saints. The arguments have been passionate, sometimes bitter, and often marked by mutual caricature. Each side has, at times, reduced the other to a straw man.

But what if we could do something different? What if, instead of arguing about who is right, we first agreed on exactly what each side assumes?

That is what a proof assistant does. It does not tell you whether an axiom is true. It tells you: if you accept these premises, this conclusion follows. It makes hidden assumptions visible. It replaces vagueness with precision. And precision, it turns out, is more loving than polemics — because it removes the possibility of caricature.

The Catlib project formalized a growing set of Catechism passages and connected doctrines in the Lean 4 proof assistant. One of the most striking findings was this: the Catholic-Protestant disagreement on justification can be modeled as a difference in axiom sets. The axiom set is the denomination.

This article applies that insight to Martin Luther's broader disagreements with the Catholic faith. The goal is not to declare a winner. The goal is to see clearly.

❅

The Five Solas as Axiom Modifications

Luther's theology is often summarized in five Latin slogans — the five solas. Each one corresponds to a specific modification of the Catholic axiom set formalized in Axioms.lean. Here is the mapping:

Sola Meaning Axiom Change
Sola Scriptura Scripture alone is the supreme authority Lutheran Rejects T1–T3's authority basis. Tradition and Councils can inform but cannot bind. The axioms T1–T3 are not necessarily rejected in content, but their source of authority (Sacred Tradition, conciliar decree) is denied independent weight.
Sola Fide Justification by faith alone Lutheran Modifies T2 (grace preserves freedom / human cooperation). Luther does not deny that the justified person does good works — he denies that those works are part of justification. Cooperation follows justification; it does not constitute it.
Sola Gratia By grace alone Shared Both sides affirm S8: grace is necessary for salvation. The disagreement is whether grace transforms (Catholic) or declares/covers (Lutheran). Luther agrees humans cannot save themselves; he disagrees about the mechanism.
Solus Christus Through Christ alone Shared Both traditions confess Christ as sole mediator (1 Tim 2:5). The implications differ: Catholics hold that saints participate in Christ's mediation without competing with it (an application of P2, two-tier causation); Lutherans see this as risking the uniqueness of Christ's role.
Soli Deo Gloria To the glory of God alone Shared Broadly affirmed by both. No axiom change required. Catholics and Lutherans agree that all glory belongs to God. The disagreement, if any, is downstream: whether honoring saints detracts from God's glory or participates in it.
Notice the pattern: three of the five solas are largely shared. The deep disagreements are concentrated in sola scriptura (the question of authority) and sola fide (the question of how justification works). These are axiom-level disagreements. The rest are differences of implication.

The Justification Disagreement, Formalized

The Catlib project formalized the Catholic doctrine of justification (CCC §1987–1993) and constructed both a catholicModel and a protestantModel in Justification.lean. The theorem models_differ_on_three_axes proves they differ on exactly three structural axes, with a fourth difference in temporal character. Here is what each axis looks like:

Axis 1: Nature of Justification
Catholic Transformative. Grace actually changes the person internally. They become genuinely righteous. The soul is renewed. (CCC §1990: "purifies his heart of sin")
Lutheran Forensic. Grace declares the person righteous without changing them internally. The sinner remains a sinner, covered by Christ's righteousness. (Simul justus et peccator)
Axis 2: Human Role
Catholic Cooperation. The person cooperates with grace through free acts of faith, hope, and charity. This cooperation is itself enabled by grace. (CCC §1993)
Lutheran Reception only. The person receives grace through faith alone. Good works follow justification but do not contribute to it. (Monergism: God alone acts in justification)
Axis 3: Instrument
Catholic Baptism. Justification is conferred through the sacrament of baptism. (CCC §1992; an application of T3, sacramental efficacy)
Lutheran Faith alone. No sacramental requirement. Luther did value baptism highly, but located justification in faith rather than in the rite itself.
Axis 4: Temporal Character
Catholic Process. Justification unfolds over time: conversion, then forgiveness, then purification, then healing. One is "being justified." (CCC §1989–1990)
Lutheran Event. Justification is a single divine act of declaration. Sanctification (growth in holiness) follows, but justification itself is complete in a moment.

The structural finding

The Catholic and Lutheran models differ on exactly four binary axes. Every axis is a specific axiom choice. Change the axiom, change the model. The axiom set is the denomination.

Luther's Axioms Made Explicit

If the Catholic position rests on 14 base axioms (P2–P3, S1–S9, T1–T3, as formalized in Axioms.lean), what does the Lutheran position require? Here is a charitable reconstruction of Luther's axiom set — the assumptions that make his positions logically coherent.

L1 Sola Scriptura: Scripture Alone Is the Supreme Authority

No Church council, papal decree, or tradition has authority independent of Scripture. Tradition may illuminate Scripture, but it cannot add to it or override it. Where Tradition and Scripture conflict (as Luther believed they did on indulgences, purgatory, and the treasury of merit), Scripture wins.

Effect on the Catholic axiom set: T1–T3 lose their independent authority. They are accepted only insofar as Luther judges them consistent with Scripture. The content of some Tradition axioms may survive; their binding force as Tradition does not.

L2 Forensic Justification: Declared, Not Transformed

Justification is God's act of declaring the sinner righteous on account of Christ's merits, not God's act of making the sinner righteous internally. The believer remains a sinner in their nature (simul justus et peccator — simultaneously righteous and sinner). Sanctification (actual moral transformation) is real but is a separate, subsequent process.

Effect on the Catholic axiom set: Replaces the "transformative" reading of S8. Luther affirms grace is necessary (the sola gratia agreement), but denies it transforms at the moment of justification. This is not a denial of transformation altogether — it is a claim that transformation belongs to sanctification, not justification.

L3 Monergism: God Alone Acts in Justification

Human cooperation plays no role in justification. The human will, corrupted by original sin, cannot cooperate with grace until it has already been renewed by grace. Justification is entirely God's work; the human person is the recipient, not a co-agent. Good works are the fruit of justification, not its cause or component.

Effect on the Catholic axiom set: Drops T2 (grace preserves freedom / human cooperation in justification). Note that Luther does not deny free will in daily life — he denies it specifically in the act of justification. His De Servo Arbitrio (On the Bondage of the Will) argues that the will is bound with respect to salvation, free with respect to earthly matters.

L4 Canon Without Church: The Canon of Scripture Is Determinable Without Church Authority

The books of the Bible can be identified by internal criteria (apostolic authorship, doctrinal consistency, self-authenticating quality) rather than by Church decree. Luther used the Hebrew Bible's canon for the Old Testament, which excludes seven books (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch) that the Catholic canon includes.

Effect on the Catholic axiom set: This is a meta-axiom — it changes which texts count as authoritative input to the entire system. Under L4, arguments that rely on the Deuterocanonical books (such as 2 Maccabees 12:46 for purgatory, or Sirach 15:14 as partial grounding for T1) lose their scriptural status.

Luther's reasoning was coherent. Given L1–L4, his conclusions follow with logical consistency. The question is not whether Luther could reason — he obviously could. The question is whether to accept L1–L4 as premises. That is a question of faith, not logic.

Where They Actually Agree

Before the disagreements, the agreements. They are vast, and seeing them clearly is as important as seeing the differences.

The Scriptural Core (S1–S5): Fully Shared

  • S1. God is love, and love requires freedom (1 Jn 4:8; Dt 30:19)
  • S2. God desires all to be saved (1 Tim 2:4)
  • S3. The moral law is inscribed on every heart (Rom 2:14–15)
  • S4. God governs all things providentially (Mt 10:29)
  • S5. Grave sin breaks communion with God (Rom 6:23)

The Nicene Creed: Fully Shared

  • One God, Father Almighty, maker of heaven and earth
  • Jesus Christ, true God and true man, crucified, risen, ascended
  • The Holy Spirit, the Lord and giver of life
  • One, holy, catholic (universal), and apostolic Church
  • The resurrection of the dead and the life of the world to come

Philosophical Commitments: Largely Shared

  • S6. Moral realism — moral facts exist independently of opinion
  • S7. Teleological freedom — sin is bondage, not liberty
  • P3. Evil is a privation of good, not a positive substance

Sola Gratia: The Deepest Agreement

  • Humans cannot save themselves
  • Grace is absolutely necessary
  • Pelagianism (salvation by human effort alone) is rejected by both
  • Both affirm S8: without God, humans can do no saving good

The ratio

Of the 14 base axioms in the Catholic system, Catholics and Lutherans share at least 10 without significant dispute. The deep disagreements live in approximately 4–5 axiom positions. The two traditions are far more alike than different.

The Deuterocanonical Books Question

Luther excluded seven books from the Old Testament that the Catholic Church includes: Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, and Baruch (plus additions to Esther and Daniel). This is not an arbitrary decision. Under Luther's axioms, it follows logically. Here is why.

The question of which books belong in the Bible is the question of the canon. And determining the canon requires an authority. This is where axiom L4 does its work:

If you accept... Then the canon is determined by... Result
Catholic The Church has authority to define the canon (an application of Tradition's binding force) The Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), and Trent (1546), which included the seven Deuterocanonical books 73 books (46 OT + 27 NT)
Lutheran The canon is determinable without Church authority (L4) An independent criterion — Luther chose the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) as the standard for the Old Testament, since these books were accepted by the Jewish community 66 books (39 OT + 27 NT)

The logic is tight on both sides. If the Church has canonical authority, you keep the seven books — because the Church included them. If the Church does not have that authority, you need another criterion. Luther's criterion (the Hebrew canon) is not arbitrary; it has genuine historical weight. The books in question were written in Greek (or their Hebrew originals were lost) and were not part of the Pharisaic canon that crystallized after the destruction of the Temple.

The Catholic counter-argument is equally precise: the early Church used the Greek Septuagint (which included these books), the Councils formally defined the canon, and Christians accepted these books as Scripture for over a thousand years before Luther. Under the Catholic axiom set, this history is authoritative.

The disagreement is not about the books themselves. It is about who has the authority to define the canon. That is an axiom-level question — and it is the same axiom (L1/L4 vs. the authority of Tradition) that drives the other disagreements. The Reformation is, at its root, an argument about authority.

Analysis The removal of the Deuterocanonical books was not theologically neutral. 2 Maccabees 12:46 — “it is a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead, that they may be loosed from sins” — is the strongest scriptural basis for purgatory. And purgatory was directly tied to indulgences, which was what ignited the Reformation in 1517. The axiom dependency chain runs: L4 (canon without Church authority) → remove 2 Maccabees → lose the strongest purgatory proof text → purgatory loses its scriptural basis → indulgences lose their theological foundation. Luther’s axiom L4 didn’t just change the Bible — it dismantled the theological infrastructure that supported the practice he originally protested.

The Complete Axiom Map

Here is the full picture: what a Lutheran keeps, what a Lutheran modifies, and what a Lutheran drops from the Catholic axiom set.

Axiom Catholic Lutheran Status
P1 Hylomorphism Not central; neither affirmed nor denied in most Lutheran theology Neutral
P2 Two-tier causation Generally accepted (God and creatures both genuinely act) Shared
P3 Evil is privation Generally accepted Shared
S1–S7 The scriptural core Fully accepted Shared
S8 Grace is necessary and transformative Grace is necessary; forensic, not transformative at justification Modified
S9 Conscience binds Accepted (Luther at Worms: "to go against conscience is neither right nor safe") Shared
T1 Libertarian free will (Trent) Denied in justification (De Servo Arbitrio); accepted in earthly matters Modified
T2 Grace preserves freedom / human cooperation Dropped. Monergism: God alone acts in justification Dropped
T3 Sacramental efficacy (ex opere operato) Modified. Sacraments are important but efficacy depends on faith of the recipient Modified

Count it up: of 14 axioms, approximately 10 are shared without significant dispute, 3 are modified, 1 is dropped, and 1 is neutral. Luther did not reject Catholicism wholesale. He modified a specific, identifiable subset of its axioms.

❅

What Formal Verification Reveals

Formal verification does not tell you who is right. It tells you something more useful: if you accept these axioms, this follows.

The Catholic position is coherent. Given the 14 axioms (P2–P3, S1–S9, T1–T3), the Catechism's conclusions about justification, grace, sacraments, and the canon follow with logical necessity. The reasoning is valid.

The Lutheran position is equally coherent. Given the modified axiom set (keeping most of P and S, replacing T2, modifying S8 and T1 and T3, adding L1 and L4), Luther's conclusions about sola fide, sola scriptura, and the canon follow with the same logical necessity. The reasoning is valid.

The disagreement is not about logic. Both sides reason correctly from their premises. The disagreement is about which axioms to accept. And that is a question of faith — specifically, a question about the nature and scope of Church authority. Nearly every other disagreement is downstream of that one.

The root axiom of the Reformation is not about justification. It is about authority. Does the Church have binding teaching authority independent of Scripture? If yes, you get Catholicism. If no, you get Protestantism. Almost everything else follows.

The authority delegation model is now formalized in Authority.lean, with specific instances for absolution, exorcism, and teaching — all deriving from one general theorem (authority_chain_specific).

Seeing this clearly is more loving than pretending the other side is foolish. Luther was not stupid; he was working from different axioms. The Council of Trent was not merely reactionary; it was articulating axioms that Luther had rejected. Each side was doing exactly what rigorous thinkers do: drawing conclusions from premises.

A proof assistant cannot tell you which axioms to accept. But it can show you, with perfect precision, what you are accepting and what follows from it. It can strip away five centuries of mutual caricature and replace them with a clear, honest diagram of agreement and disagreement.

That is not a small thing. It might even be the beginning of something.
