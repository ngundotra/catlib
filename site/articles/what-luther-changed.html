Where Did Protestants Actually Go Wrong?

The Reformation was 4 changes. That’s it.

⁂

Ask most people what the Reformation was about and you get a grab bag. Corruption. Indulgences. Martin Luther nailing something to a door. The 95 Theses. Sola scriptura. It sounds like a thousand complaints rolled into a movement.

It wasn’t.

We used a proof assistant — software that checks logical reasoning the way a calculator checks arithmetic — to model Catholic and Protestant theology as sets of starting assumptions. What we found: the entire Reformation reduces to four changes. Four places where Protestants swapped one foundational belief for another. Everything else — the different prayers, the different practices, the different feelings about saints and sacraments — flows downstream from those four.

This is not an attack on Protestantism. It’s a defense of Protestant intelligence. The Reformers were not confused. They made four precise philosophical moves, and the rest of their theology followed with remarkable logical consistency. The question is not whether they reasoned well. The question is whether those four starting points are the right ones.

❅

The Four Swaps

Catholics and Protestants share an enormous amount of common ground. God is love. Sin is real. Grace is necessary. Christ is the one mediator. The Nicene Creed. The Ten Commandments. On the vast majority of foundations, both traditions stand on exactly the same ground.

But in four specific places, the Reformers made a different choice.

Swap 1: Does grace change you, or cover you?
Catholic When God justifies you, something real happens inside you. Grace enters your soul and actually transforms you. You are not merely declared righteous — you are made righteous. The stain is not hidden. It is being washed away.
Protestant When God justifies you, He declares you righteous because of what Christ did, but you remain a sinner in your nature. Christ’s righteousness covers your sin like snow covers a landscape. You are simultaneously saint and sinner — simul justus et peccator.

Are you actually becoming holy, or is God choosing not to look at your sin? The Catholic says: grace is medicine that heals. The Protestant says: grace is a verdict that acquits. Both agree you desperately need it. They disagree about what it does.

Swap 2: Can you cooperate with God, or is your will too broken?
Catholic Your free will is wounded by sin but not destroyed. When God offers grace, you can say yes. That “yes” is itself enabled by grace — you cannot do it alone — but it is genuinely your yes. You are a real participant in your own salvation, not a passenger.
Protestant When it comes to salvation, your will is not just wounded — it is dead. You cannot cooperate with grace any more than a corpse can cooperate with a defibrillator. God must do all the work. Your faith itself is a gift from God, not your contribution.

When you choose to pray, is that you or God moving you? When you resist temptation, is that your cooperation with grace, or God acting through you unilaterally? The Catholic tradition says both you and God are genuinely acting. The Protestant tradition says that in the things that matter most — salvation — God is the sole actor.

Swap 3: Do you and God work together, or does God do it all?
Catholic Salvation is synergistic — a collaboration. God initiates, but you respond. “Work out your salvation with fear and trembling” (Phil 2:12) is a real command, not a metaphor. Your works, animated by grace, genuinely contribute to your sanctification.
Protestant Salvation is monergistic — God alone. Yes, you will do good works after being justified, but those works are the fruit of salvation, not its cause. You are saved by faith alone. Works follow as evidence, not as contribution.

Phil 2:12 says “work out your salvation.” Is that real work or theater? The Catholic reads it straight: work, because your working matters. The Protestant reads it as describing what the justified life naturally looks like — of course you will work, but the working does not save you. The saving was already done.

Swap 4: Do sacraments do something, or just mean something?
Catholic Sacraments cause what they signify. When the priest says “This is my body,” the bread really changes. Baptism really washes away original sin. Confession really absolves. The sacraments are not symbols of grace — they are instruments of grace. They work ex opere operato — by the very fact of being performed.
Protestant Sacraments are important, but their power depends on the faith of the person receiving them. Without faith, the ritual is empty. The bread and wine are signs that point to Christ, but they do not become Christ. Baptism is an outward sign of an inward reality, not the cause of that reality.

When the priest says “This is my body,” does the bread change? For Catholics, yes — substantially and really. For most Protestants, no. This is not a small disagreement. It reshapes everything about worship, priesthood, and what happens on Sunday morning.

What the Machine Found

We did not just describe these four differences. We formalized them — wrote both the Catholic axioms and the Protestant alternatives as precise logical statements, then asked a computer to check what follows from each set.

Finding 1: The Protestant system is internally consistent

If you accept the four Protestant swaps, the rest of Protestant theology follows logically. Sola fide, the rejection of purgatory, the reshaping of the priesthood — none of these are arbitrary. They are logical consequences of four starting beliefs. The Reformation is not a mess. It is a system.

Finding 2: The Catholic system is internally consistent

If you keep the Catholic axioms, the Catechism follows logically. Transubstantiation, purgatory, the communion of saints, the role of works in salvation — all logical consequences of the Catholic starting points. The Catholic system is not a collection of arbitrary traditions. It is a system too.

Finding 3: You cannot hold both

When the machine tried to combine both axiom sets, it derived a contradiction. Grace cannot both transform you and merely cover you. Your will cannot both cooperate with God and be too dead to cooperate. These are genuine either/or choices. You have to pick.

That third finding is the crucial one. The Reformation is not a disagreement about facts — it is a disagreement about axioms. An axiom is a starting point you accept without proof, because it is where your reasoning begins. You do not prove axioms. You choose them. And Catholics and Protestants chose differently in exactly four places.

⁂

Why This Matters

Most ecumenical dialogue argues about conclusions. Purgatory. The role of Mary. Whether James 2 contradicts Romans 4. But those are downstream questions — consequences of the four swaps, not independent disagreements. If you share the same axioms, you will reach the same conclusions. If you don’t, no amount of arguing about conclusions will resolve anything.

That is why five centuries of debate have produced so little movement. Both sides have been arguing about the fruit while disagreeing about the roots. The real conversation — the one that might actually get somewhere — is about the four swaps themselves.

❅

A Different Kind of Disagreement

Luther wasn’t wrong. That is a strange thing for a Catholic formalization project to say, but it is what the mathematics shows. Given his four starting points, his conclusions follow with iron logic. He was not confused. He was not stupid. He was not merely angry about indulgences, though he was that too.

He was playing a different game with different rules.

The question isn’t who reasons better — it’s which starting points you accept. And that is a question no machine can answer, because it is a question about faith.

But at least now we can see the question clearly. And clarity, in five centuries of fog, is no small gift.
