Sola Scriptura: The Axiom That Divides Christianity

“Scripture alone” is not one doctrine. It’s four.

⁂

Ask a Baptist what “sola scriptura” means and they will tell you the Bible is the only authority. Ask a Lutheran and they will tell you the Bible is the highest authority — but the Creeds and Confessions are valuable witnesses. Ask an Anglican and they will tell you the Bible contains all things necessary for salvation — but the Church has real authority in controversies of faith. Ask a Presbyterian and they will give you a careful systematic account involving self-authentication, perspicuity, and the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit.

These are not the same doctrine.

We used a proof assistant to formalize each of these variants as a precise set of axioms, then compared them against the Catholic axiom set. What we found: the entire debate reduces to three binary choices. Three places where you must pick Catholic or Protestant. Every other disagreement about Scripture, Tradition, and authority flows downstream from those three.

We also found something the formalization made visible that ordinary theological language hides: two claims that sound completely different turn out to be literally the same proposition.

❅

The Distinction Most People Miss

When a Protestant says “Scripture is sufficient” and a Catholic says “no it isn’t,” they are usually talking past each other. The word “sufficient” is doing double duty, and neither side notices.

There are two very different claims hiding behind that word:

Material sufficiency: Scripture contains all necessary doctrine, at least implicitly. Surprisingly, many Catholic theologians accept this. Vatican II’s Dei Verbum deliberately left the question open.

Formal sufficiency: Scripture alone, without an external interpretive authority, is adequate as the rule of faith. Catholics deny this emphatically.

The analogy that clarifies it: a law code can contain all necessary laws (materially sufficient) and still need a Supreme Court to interpret them (not formally sufficient). The laws are all there. But “all there” does not mean “self-interpreting.”

The Protestant sola scriptura claim is formal sufficiency. The Catholic counter-arguments target formal sufficiency, not material sufficiency. The debate is not primarily about whether the Bible has enough content. It is about whether the Bible needs an authoritative reader.

❅

The Four Variants

We formalized four distinct versions of sola scriptura, each from its own confessional document. They range from the most radical to the most restrained.

Baptist — The Most Radical
Baptist Faith & Message (2000): Scripture is “truth, without any mixture of error, for its matter” and “the supreme standard by which all human conduct, creeds, and religious opinions should be tried.” Soul competency — each believer has direct access to God through Scripture. No confessional authority. No norma normata. The canon is simply assumed.
Reformed — The Strongest System
Westminster Confession of Faith (1646): The most sophisticated formulation. Scripture is materially sufficient, formally sufficient, self-authenticating (“our full persuasion… is from the inward work of the Holy Spirit”), perspicuous on essentials, and self-interpreting (“the infallible rule of interpretation of Scripture is the Scripture itself”). Tradition is explicitly excluded (“traditions of men”).
Lutheran — The Moderate Form
Formula of Concord (1577): Scripture is the sole norma normans (norming norm). But the Confessions, Creeds, and Church Fathers are the norma normata (normed norm) — witnesses to scriptural truth with real but derivative authority. They are not “traditions of men” to be discarded. They are a hermeneutical lens. Luther himself famously questioned James, Hebrews, Jude, and Revelation — introducing a “canon within the canon” concept that no other tradition accepts.
Anglican — The Weakest Form
39 Articles of Religion (1571): “Holy Scripture containeth all things necessary to salvation.” Note the narrow scope — “to salvation,” not “for all matters of faith and life.” Article XX grants the Church “authority in Controversies of Faith,” bounded by Scripture. Article XXI concedes that councils “may err, and sometimes have erred.” This is arguably prima scriptura — Scripture first among several authorities — rather than strict sola scriptura.

These are not minor variations. The Baptist rejects all confessional authority; the Lutheran values it highly. The Reformed excludes tradition entirely; the Anglican gives the Church real (if bounded) authority. They all claim sola scriptura, but the words cover very different commitments.

The Three Choice Points

The formalization reveals that the entire Catholic-Protestant debate on the rule of faith reduces to three binary decisions. Every denominational variant is a specific combination of positions on these three axes. Pick your answers, and your ecclesiology follows.

Choice 1: Is everything in the Bible?
Catholic No. Some revealed teaching exists only in Tradition. “Not everything was written” (CCC §76; Jn 21:25). The apostles transmitted revelation two ways: orally and in writing. Some oral teaching was never committed to writing.
Protestant Yes. All necessary doctrine is in Scripture, at least implicitly. “The whole counsel of God concerning all things necessary for… salvation, faith and life, is either expressly set down in Scripture, or by good and necessary consequence may be deduced from Scripture” (WCF I.6).
Choice 2: How do you know which books are Scripture?
Catholic The Church decided. The canon was determined by magisterial judgment — the Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397). No Bible verse lists which books belong in the Bible. The act of canonization is not in Scripture. Scripture’s authority presupposes the Church that canonized it.
Protestant Scripture is self-authenticating. “Our full persuasion and assurance of the infallible truth and divine authority thereof, is from the inward work of the Holy Spirit bearing witness by and with the Word in our hearts” (WCF I.5). The Church recognized the canon; it did not determine it.
Choice 3: Does the Church make doctrine binding, or just discover it?
Catholic Constitutive authority. The Church’s authority derives from apostolic succession and is not reducible to Scripture. The Council of Nicaea defined the Trinity as binding doctrine in 325 AD — before the canon was even settled. That authority came from succession, not from a book whose boundaries were still being debated.
Protestant Recognitive authority. Councils have authority only insofar as they faithfully teach Scripture. Their authority is ministerial (serving Scripture), not magisterial (over Scripture). Nicaea was right because it got the Bible right, not because bishops have independent authority.

That is the complete map. Every Protestant-Catholic disagreement about Scripture, Tradition, and authority reduces to one or more of these three decisions. The Reformed, Lutheran, and Baptist take the Protestant position on all three. The Anglican hedges on the first (restricting sufficiency to “things necessary for salvation”) and gives the Church more room on the third.

What the Machine Found

We formalized each variant as a Lean 4 structure, then ran incompatibility checks against the declared Catholic axioms. The results:

Finding 1: Three variants fall to the same axiom

The Reformed, Lutheran, and Baptist variants all claim full material sufficiency: every revealed teaching is in Scripture. The Catholic axiom not_everything_written directly negates this. Three lines of proof. Direct contradiction.

Finding 2: The Anglican has an escape hatch

The Anglican variant only claims sufficiency for things “necessary to salvation.” This means the Tradition-only teaching from the Catholic axiom might be non-essential — and if so, the Anglican position survives. The Anglican is the only Protestant variant that does not directly contradict not_everything_written.

But there is a catch. To use this escape hatch, the Anglican must draw a principled line between “essential” and “non-essential” doctrine. Who draws that line? That question is itself a doctrinal judgment requiring authority — and it circles back to Choice 3.

Finding 3: Two claims that sound different are the same proposition

This was the surprise. When we defined “Tradition has normative authority” formally, we got: there exists a revealed teaching that is in Tradition and not in Scripture. When we checked the Catholic axiom not_everything_written, it says: there exists a revealed teaching that is in Tradition and not in Scripture.

They are literally the same proposition. The proof is Iff.rfl — reflexivity. Zero steps.

This means: when Protestant apologists say “we reject normative Tradition” and Catholic apologists say “not everything was written,” they are having the same argument in different words. The formalization stripped away five centuries of theological dialect and found one proposition underneath.

Finding 4: The combination is stronger than any single argument

The Catholic case against sola scriptura rests on four independent arguments, each from a different Lean file:

  1. Content: Not everything was written (RuleOfFaith.lean)
  2. Canon: The canon presupposes the Church (RuleOfFaith.lean)
  3. Temporal: Nicaea defined doctrine before the canon was settled (ConciliarAuthority.lean)
  4. Interpretation: Sincere readers disagree, and private judgment has no adjudicator (PrivateJudgment.lean)

Each argument alone has a plausible Protestant counter. But the counters pull in different directions. Self-authentication says Scripture needs nothing external; ministerial authority says the Church played a real role. Material sufficiency says everything is in Scripture; perspicuity says only essential things are clear — so you still need an interpreter for the rest. The Catholic position does not have this tension because it gives the Church genuine constitutive authority.

❅

The Orthodox Break the Binary

There is a sixth position that does not fit the Catholic-Protestant binary. The Eastern Orthodox reject sola scriptura as firmly as Catholics do — but they also reject the papal Magisterium.

Their model: Scripture exists within Tradition. Not alongside it. Not as a parallel source. Within it. Georges Florovsky put it precisely: “Tradition is Scripture rightly understood.” Scripture is the written crystallization of a larger living reality. Remove Tradition and you do not have a standalone book; you have a text ripped from its interpretive home.

We formalized this as set inclusion: every teaching in Scripture is also in Tradition. Scripture ⊆ Tradition. This is weaker than Florovsky’s full claim — he means something closer to “Scripture is unintelligible outside Tradition” — but the structural point is the same. You cannot extract Scripture from Tradition and expect it to work on its own.

The Orthodox show that rejecting sola scriptura does not require accepting papal authority. There is a third option: conciliar authority, confirmed by the reception of the whole Church over time. The formalization captures this as a distinct position on the three-axis map.

❅

Every System Works on Its Own Terms

The Catholic arguments above show that sola scriptura is incompatible with Catholic axioms. They do not show that sola scriptura is incoherent. This distinction matters.

Under Reformed axioms, the Reformed system works. Material sufficiency entails that there is no Tradition-only teaching. Perspicuity entails that sincere readers converge on essentials. Self-authentication entails that the canon does not depend on the Church. No internal contradictions.

Under Lutheran axioms, the Lutheran system works. Material sufficiency holds. Tradition serves as a hermeneutical lens — a witness to what Scripture teaches, not an independent source of doctrine.

Under Anglican axioms, the Anglican system works. Essential doctrine is in Scripture. The Church has real authority in controversies, bounded by Scripture. Non-essential doctrine may exist in Tradition. No contradictions.

Each system is internally consistent. The machine verified this. The debate is not about logic — it is about which axioms accurately describe reality.

⁂

Three Decisions, Six Traditions, One Question

The formalization cannot tell you which axioms are true. No formal system can prove that the canon is self-authenticating rather than Church-determined, or vice versa. No formal system can prove that Church authority is constitutive rather than recognitive. These are judgments about reality that go beyond what logic can establish.

What the formalization can do is make the choice points visible. It strips away five centuries of accumulated dialect and shows you the three places where you must choose. A thoughtful Presbyterian and a thoughtful Catholic can now see exactly where they diverge, exactly what each side is assuming, and exactly what follows from those assumptions.

And they can see something the theological language hid: that “we reject normative Tradition” and “not everything was written” are not two arguments. They are one argument in two dialects.

Clarity is not agreement. But it is the prerequisite for an honest conversation. And after five hundred years, an honest conversation would be no small thing.
