You Are Right to Be Suspicious

Here’s what this project can and cannot do

⁂

We put Catholic theology through a proof assistant. If your first reaction is “that’s absurd,” good. That means you’re paying attention.

Proof assistants run on logic. They verify that conclusions follow from premises. They check structure, catch contradictions, and refuse to let you hand-wave past a gap in your reasoning. They are very, very good at this.

But God is not a logic problem. God is a belief. Faith is not a theorem you prove — it’s a relationship you live. And if someone told you they had “proven” Catholicism on a computer, you should be suspicious.

So let us be clear about what we are actually doing here.

❅

You Still Get to Choose

Nothing in this project tells you what to believe. Not a single theorem. Not a single line of code.

A proof assistant works like this: you give it premises, and it tells you what follows. That’s it. The premises are yours to accept or reject. The machine just traces the consequences.

We gave it the premises of the Catholic Catechism. The axioms. The starting beliefs. And then we asked: if these are true, what follows? Where do they lead? Where do they bump into each other? Where do they hold together beautifully?

The project doesn’t ask you to accept those premises. It asks: if you do accept them, do you know what you’re signing up for?

⁂

What We’re Actually Doing

The Catechism of the Catholic Church is a massive document. It makes hundreds of claims. Some of those claims depend on others. Some are in tension with each other. Some require hidden assumptions that the text never states explicitly.

We are trying to find all of that. Every hidden assumption. Every chain of logic. Every place where one teaching quietly depends on another.

We are trying to formally state the set of beliefs that make the Catechism true and sound — not to prove that it is true and sound. There’s an enormous difference.

Think of it like this: a structural engineer doesn’t build the cathedral. She maps its load-bearing walls. She finds which stones hold up which arches. She can tell you “if you remove this pillar, that vault collapses.” She is not the architect. She is not the one who decided to build a cathedral in the first place.

We are the structural engineers. The Church is the architect.

❅

What We Are Not Doing

We are not trying to disprove the Catechism. We are not looking for “gotchas.” We are not atheists with a Lean compiler and a grudge.

We are doing our best to take the Church’s arguments as seriously as she takes them herself. That means identifying hidden assumptions and making them explicit. It means tracing the logic so that anyone — Catholic, Protestant, atheist, curious — can follow the same chain of reasoning and see exactly where it leads.

If the logic holds, great. If it reveals a tension, that’s not an attack — it’s a gift. Tensions are where the interesting theology lives. They’re where the Church has done her deepest thinking, and where she might still have more thinking to do.

⁂

The Language Problem (For the Extremely Paranoid)

Here is the sharpest objection, and it deserves a straight answer.

When you say “God is love,” you are using analogy. The word “love” does not mean exactly the same thing when applied to God as when applied to your mother. All human language about God is reaching — pointing at something infinite with finite words. The Catechism itself says this (§42): “Our human words always fall short of the mystery of God.”

This is what theologians call the analogy of being, and Karl Barth — one of the greatest Protestant theologians of the twentieth century — called it “the invention of the Antichrist.” His point was simple: if human language can never fully capture God, how can you formalize it in a computer?

He has a point.

Every axiom in this project is a human approximation of something infinite. When we write godIsLove in Lean, we are not claiming to have captured divine love in a data type. We are saying: “The Catechism asserts this. Let us trace what follows from the assertion.”

The map is not the territory. Our formal system is the map. God is the territory. We know the difference.

And yes — this is propositional logic, one logic system among several. Analogical reasoning, modal logic, fuzzy logic — other frameworks exist and capture different things. We chose the one that is best at catching contradictions and tracing implications. It has limits. We are not pretending otherwise.

❅

But the Map Still Helps

Here is what the map is good for.

The Catechism teaches that “intrinsically evil acts exist” (§1756) — some things are always wrong, no matter the circumstances. It also teaches that “conscience excuses” (§1790–1794) — a person who genuinely does not know an act is wrong bears less guilt.

These two teachings are in tension. Not contradiction — tension. The Church has worked out how they fit together, but it took centuries of careful moral theology. A proof assistant found that tension in ten seconds.

That is what formalization does. It does not replace the centuries of thinking. It surfaces the places where the thinking is needed. It makes the invisible structure visible.

A theology student might wrestle with the relationship between objective evil and subjective culpability for years before realizing the tension is structural, not a failure of their own understanding. A proof assistant makes that structural tension explicit on the first pass.

⁂

What Formalization Can Do

It can find hidden assumptions — the beliefs that the Catechism relies on but never states out loud. Every system has them. Ours makes them visible.

It can show where denominations actually disagree. Not where they think they disagree, which is often a misunderstanding, but where their starting axioms genuinely diverge. We formalized both Catholic and Lutheran axiom sets and proved them mutually incompatible. The proof doesn’t say who is right. It says: here is the exact point where you part ways. Now you can have an honest conversation about that, instead of arguing past each other for another five hundred years.

It can catch contradictions. If two teachings cannot both be true given the same axioms, the proof assistant will find it. Not as an opinion. Not as an interpretation. As a mathematical fact about the logical structure of the claims.

It can trace implications. If you accept teaching A, you are implicitly committed to teachings B, C, and D. You might not have known that. Now you do.

❅

What Formalization Cannot Do

It cannot tell you what to believe.

It cannot tell you whether God exists. It cannot tell you whether the Catholic Church is right. It cannot tell you whether to receive communion, go to confession, or pray the rosary. It cannot replace the lived experience of faith, the grace of the sacraments, or the quiet voice you hear in prayer.

It cannot capture the full richness of analogical language about God. Our formal system is, by its nature, more rigid than the reality it models. When the Catechism says “God is love,” it means something that overflows any logical predicate. We know that. The proof assistant knows that too, in its own way — we formalized the limits of our own formalization.

And it cannot replace the Church’s living tradition of interpretation. The Catechism is not a standalone document. It lives inside two thousand years of prayer, argument, councils, saints, and sinners. Formalization captures the structure of the claims. It does not capture the life of the community that makes those claims.

⁂

A Love Letter, Not a Proof

This project is a love letter, not a proof.

We took the Church’s arguments as seriously as she does. We read her claims not with the hermeneutics of suspicion but with the hermeneutics of respect — and then we asked a machine that cannot be impressed, cannot be persuaded, and cannot hand-wave to check our work.

Where the logic held, we were grateful. Where it revealed tensions, we were honest. Where it hit limits, we said so.

That is the highest form of respect we know how to offer: to take something seriously enough to formalize it, and to be honest about what we found.

You still get to choose what you believe. You always did. We just wanted to make the map a little clearer for the journey.
