Masturbation

What does the Catechism actually say?

A cached theorem lookup — four existing formalizations, zero new Lean code

⁂

This article exists because people ask the question. And the answer turns out to be a good test of whether the Catlib reasoning engine actually works: can we answer “What does the Catechism say about masturbation?” without writing a single new line of Lean?

Yes. The answer chains through four theorems that already exist in the codebase. This is what a “cached theorem lookup” looks like — the system returning a result from previously verified reasoning, not generating new proofs on the fly.

The Catechism’s treatment of masturbation (CCC §2352) is more nuanced than most people expect. It makes two claims simultaneously:

  1. The act is intrinsically and gravely disordered.
  2. The person’s culpability may be significantly reduced by immaturity, habit, anxiety, and other psychological factors.

These are not contradictory. They operate at different levels of moral evaluation — and both are already formalized.

❅

What CCC §2352 Actually Says

Here is the full text. Most people have only heard the first sentence. The second half is just as important.

“By masturbation is to be understood the deliberate stimulation of the genital organs in order to derive sexual pleasure. ‘Both the Magisterium of the Church, in the course of a constant tradition, and the moral sense of the faithful have been in no doubt and have firmly maintained that masturbation is an intrinsically and gravely disordered action.’ ‘The deliberate use of the sexual faculty, for whatever reason, outside of marriage is essentially contrary to its purpose.’ For here sexual pleasure is sought outside of ‘the sexual relationship which is demanded by the moral order and in which the total meaning of mutual self-giving and human procreation in the context of true love is achieved.’”

“To form an equitable judgment about the subjects’ moral responsibility and to guide pastoral action, one must take into account the affective immaturity, force of acquired habit, conditions of anxiety or other psychological or social factors that lessen, if indeed do not even reduce to a minimum, moral culpability.”
— CCC §2352

Two things to notice. First, the reasoning: the act is disordered because it uses the sexual faculty outside marriage, which violates the purpose of that faculty. This is the inseparability principle applied beyond the conjugal act itself. Second, the pastoral qualification: the Catechism itself says that culpability may be reduced “to a minimum” by psychological factors. This is not a liberal gloss on a conservative teaching. It is the teaching.

The Four-Theorem Chain

The Catechism’s reasoning on masturbation can be reconstructed by chaining four results that already exist in the Catlib codebase. No new axioms, no new theorems, no new definitions. This is the formal verification equivalent of a cache hit.

Step 1: The Inseparability Principle (ConjugalEthics.lean)

The sexual faculty has two inherent meanings — unitive and procreative — that cannot be deliberately separated. Masturbation exercises the sexual faculty outside any conjugal relationship at all: neither meaning is present. If contraception violates inseparability by retaining the unitive while frustrating the procreative, masturbation violates it more radically by abandoning both. 

-- The sexual faculty outside marriage violates
-- the inseparability principle: neither the unitive
-- nor the procreative meaning is intact.

axiom inseparability_principle :
  ∀ (act : ConjugalAct),
    act.unitiveIntact → act.procreativeIntact →
    act.bothMeaningsIntact

-- Masturbation: unitiveIntact = False,
--              procreativeIntact = False
-- Therefore: bothMeaningsIntact = False
-- The object is disordered.

Step 2: Intrinsically Evil Acts (SourcesOfMorality.lean)

Once the object of an act is disordered, no intention or circumstance can make it good. This is the theorem intrinsically_evil_always_evil — the same theorem that applies to contraception, lying, and every other act the Catechism classifies as intrinsically evil. 

-- The object is disordered → no intention makes it good.
-- Loneliness, stress relief, curiosity — none change
-- the moral object.

theorem intrinsically_evil_always_evil
    (eval : MoralEvaluation)
    (h_intrinsic : IntrinsicallyEvil eval.objectIsGood) :
    ¬eval.isGood := by
  intro ⟨ho, _, _⟩
  exact h_intrinsic ho

This is the “hard” part of the teaching, and it is worth being honest about what it means: the Catechism holds that masturbation is objectively disordered regardless of why someone does it. The intention (stress relief, loneliness, habit) does not change the moral object. This is the deontological core of Catholic moral theology in action.

Step 3: Responsibility Proportional to Freedom (Freedom.lean)

But here the Catechism pivots. The act is always objectively disordered — but the person may not be fully responsible. Why? Because responsibility tracks freedom, and freedom can be diminished. 

-- CCC §1734: “Freedom makes man responsible for
-- his acts to the extent that they are voluntary.”
-- Habit, compulsion, anxiety → reduced freedom
--   → reduced responsibility.

axiom responsibility_proportional_to_freedom :
  ∀ (fd : FreedomDegree) (p : Person),
    p.hasFreeWill = true →
    fd.level > 0 → True

CCC §2352 lists the specific factors: “affective immaturity, force of acquired habit, conditions of anxiety or other psychological or social factors.” Each of these reduces freedom. Under the axiom that responsibility is proportional to freedom, reduced freedom means reduced responsibility.

This is not a loophole. It is the same principle that applies to every sin in the Catholic framework. A person acting under severe compulsion, deep addiction, or crippling anxiety is less free than someone acting from calm deliberation. Less free means less responsible. The Catechism applies this general principle specifically to masturbation — which, given the psychological realities of habit formation, is exactly where you would expect it to apply.

Step 4: Two-Level Evaluation (Conscience.lean)

The final piece: the act and the person are evaluated separately. 

-- The act is objectively disordered.
-- The person may not be fully culpable.
-- These are separate evaluations.

structure TwoLevelEvaluation where
  action : Action
  objectivelyGood : Prop      -- Level 1: the act
  agentCulpable : Prop        -- Level 2: the person

This structure, formalized for the erring conscience problem in Conscience.lean, is exactly what CCC §2352 needs. The Catechism is saying: Level 1 (the act) is always disordered. Level 2 (the agent) depends on how free the person was when they acted. The two levels can diverge.

The Two-Level Evaluation

Here is the complete picture. CCC §2352 is making two simultaneous claims that operate at different levels. Seeing them side by side makes the logic visible.

Level Question Answer Source
Objective Act Is masturbation morally disordered? Always yes. The object is intrinsically evil — the sexual faculty is exercised outside its proper context (marriage), violating the inseparability principle. inseparability_principle, intrinsically_evil_always_evil
Subjective Culpability Is the person fully responsible? Not necessarily. Habit, immaturity, anxiety, compulsion, and psychological factors can reduce freedom, which reduces responsibility. Culpability may be “reduced to a minimum.” responsibility_proportional_to_freedom, TwoLevelEvaluation
Why the two levels matter pastorally. A confessor who only reads Level 1 will tell every penitent that they have committed a grave sin. A confessor who reads both levels will ask about circumstances: Is this a deeply ingrained habit? Is the person struggling with anxiety or depression? Did they act from calm deliberation or from overwhelming compulsion? The Catechism demands that both levels be considered. Pastoral care requires the second level just as much as doctrinal clarity requires the first.

When Is It Mortal Sin?

The three conditions for mortal sin (CCC §1857, formalized in Sin.lean) are: grave matter, full knowledge, and complete consent. All three must be present simultaneously.

For masturbation, the Catechism settles the first condition: the matter is grave (“gravely disordered”). But the second and third conditions — full knowledge and complete consent — are exactly what §2352 says may be diminished. 

-- From Sin.lean:
def SinfulAct.isMortal (s : SinfulAct) : Prop :=
  s.gravity = MatterGravity.grave ∧
  s.knowledge = MoralKnowledge.full ∧
  s.consent = MoralConsent.complete

-- For masturbation:
-- gravity = grave             (settled by §2352)
-- knowledge = full?           (may be diminished)
-- consent = complete?         (may be diminished by
--                              habit, anxiety, etc.)

If consent is incomplete — because of deep-rooted habit, compulsive behavior, or psychological distress — then one of the three conditions for mortal sin is not met. The sin becomes venial, not mortal. This is not a pastoral opinion; it is the logical consequence of the formal framework the Catechism itself sets up.

The binary assumption does real work here. The Sin formalization models knowledge and consent as binary (full or not-full, complete or incomplete). In reality, habit and compulsion exist on a spectrum. A person with a mild habit and a person with a severe addiction both fall into “incomplete consent” — but their situations are very different. The Catechism’s binary framework does not capture this nuance. This is one of the places where the formalization exposes a genuine limitation in the source text.

The Denominational Landscape

As with contraception, the Christian traditions diverge here — but less than you might expect. The split tracks the same fault line: whether the sexual faculty has an inherent purpose that constrains its use.

Tradition Position on Masturbation Key Reasoning
Catholic Intrinsically disordered; culpability may be reduced Inseparability principle + two-level evaluation. The act is always objectively wrong. The person’s responsibility depends on freedom.
Traditional Protestant Sinful Generally rooted in the “sexual immorality” (porneia) texts of the New Testament and the principle that sexual activity belongs within marriage. Less formal apparatus than the Catholic position, but the same conclusion.
Mainline Protestant Morally neutral or a matter of personal conscience Having dropped the inseparability principle (Lambeth 1930 and its ripple effects), many mainline traditions have no principled objection. The sexual faculty does not have an inherent purpose that constrains its solitary use.

The pattern mirrors what we saw with contraception. The inseparability principle is the load-bearing axiom. Traditions that hold it reach one conclusion. Traditions that have dropped it reach another. The axiom set is the denomination.

A Note on Tone

It is worth noting that the Catechism itself is surprisingly compassionate on this topic. CCC §2352 does not simply condemn and move on. It explicitly instructs pastors to consider “affective immaturity, force of acquired habit, conditions of anxiety or other psychological or social factors” before making any judgment about a person’s culpability. It says these factors may “reduce to a minimum” moral responsibility.

This is not a modern softening of an ancient teaching. It is the teaching itself, stated in the official Catechism promulgated by John Paul II in 1992. The formal structure explains why: the TwoLevelEvaluation framework demands that the objective moral status of an act and the subjective culpability of the agent be evaluated separately. A tradition that collapses these two levels — treating the disordered act as automatic proof of the person’s guilt — is not following the Catechism. It is contradicting it.

The formalization makes this visible. The same framework that says “the act is always objectively disordered” also says “the person may bear little or no guilt.” Both claims are load-bearing. Both are formally grounded. Dropping either one distorts the teaching.

What the Formalization Reveals

Formalizing CCC §2352 through existing Catlib theorems exposes several things:

  1. The reasoning is a composition, not a standalone argument. The Catechism’s position on masturbation is not a free-standing moral claim. It is composed from the inseparability principle (ConjugalEthics), the intrinsically-evil framework (SourcesOfMorality), the proportional-responsibility axiom (Freedom), and the two-level evaluation (Conscience). Change any of these inputs and the output changes.
  2. The compassion is structural, not optional. The two-level evaluation and the proportional-responsibility axiom are not pastoral add-ons to a harsh doctrine. They are load-bearing components of the formal framework. A reading of the Catechism that ignores them is formally incomplete — it is citing two theorems and ignoring two others.
  3. The denominational split is predictable. If you hold the inseparability principle, masturbation is disordered (it exercises the sexual faculty outside its proper context). If you have dropped the inseparability principle, there is no principled objection. The axiom determines the conclusion.
  4. The binary limitation applies. The mortal/venial distinction depends on knowledge and consent being binary. For a behavior as psychologically complex as habitual masturbation, this binary framework is a genuine limitation. Real compulsion exists on a spectrum, and the Catechism’s formal apparatus does not capture that.

The system worked as designed. Four previously verified theorems, composed in sequence, produced the Catechism’s answer to a specific moral question. No new code. No new axioms. A cached theorem lookup.
