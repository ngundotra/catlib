Who Forgives Sins?

God, priests, and the delegation question

A Protestant asks: “Why do Catholics believe priests can forgive sins? Doesn’t God alone do that?”

⁂

You are right that God alone forgives sins. Catholics agree completely. Mark 2:7 asks the question plainly: “Who can forgive sins but God alone?” and the Catholic answer is: nobody. No priest, no bishop, no pope forgives sins by his own power. On this point there is no daylight between Catholic and Protestant theology.

The question is not who forgives, but how God exercises that forgiveness. Does He forgive directly when a person repents in their heart? Or did He establish an instrument — a sacrament — through which He forgives? Catholics believe He did both. The sacrament of Reconciliation is not a replacement for God’s forgiveness; it is the channel He instituted for it.

That is the claim. Let us trace it through Scripture, see what assumptions it requires, and understand exactly where Catholic and Protestant readings diverge.

❅

The Scriptural Case

The Catholic position rests on a handful of passages that, read together, form an argument about delegated authority. Here they are, in the order the argument builds.

John 20:21–23 — The Commission

Catholic reading On Easter evening, Jesus appears to the Apostles and says: “As the Father has sent me, so I send you.” He breathes on them and says: “Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you retain the sins of any, they are retained.”

Catholics read this as Jesus delegating His authority to forgive sins to the Apostles. The phrasing “if you forgive… they are forgiven” implies a real power to act, not merely a declaration that God has already acted. The “retaining” clause implies judgment — the Apostles must hear what the sin is in order to decide whether to forgive or retain. This is the scriptural basis for auricular confession.

Protestant reading Many Protestants read this as a general commission to proclaim the gospel of forgiveness, not as a delegation of judicial authority. When Christians share the gospel and someone repents, their sins are forgiven — by God. The Apostles are messengers, not judges.

Matthew 16:19 & 18:18 — Binding and Loosing

Catholic reading To Peter: “Whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven” (Mt 16:19). Then to all the Apostles: the same power (Mt 18:18). Catholics understand “binding and loosing” as judicial authority over sin and discipline — including the authority to forgive sins sacramentally.

Protestant reading “Binding and loosing” refers to teaching authority and church discipline, not to sacramental absolution. It means the Apostles can declare what is permitted and forbidden, not that they personally absolve sins.

2 Corinthians 5:18–20 — The Ministry of Reconciliation

Shared Paul writes: “God gave us the ministry of reconciliation… entrusting to us the message of reconciliation. So we are ambassadors for Christ, God making his appeal through us.” Both traditions agree that Christians have a ministry of reconciliation. The question is whether “ambassador” implies a sacramental role or a proclamatory one.

James 5:16 — Confess to One Another

Shared “Confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed.” Both traditions acknowledge this verse. Catholics see it as consistent with sacramental confession; Protestants often read it as mutual accountability among believers, not priestly absolution.

Both sides cite Scripture. Neither side is ignoring the text. The disagreement is about how to read these passages — and that reading depends on prior assumptions about authority, sacraments, and apostolic succession.
⁂

The Authority Delegation Chain

The Catholic argument follows the same structural pattern formalized in Catlib’s Exorcism.lean — an authority delegation chain. For exorcism, the chain runs: Christ → Apostles → Bishops → Priests. For forgiveness of sins, the identical pattern applies:

Step Claim Source
1 God alone has authority to forgive sins Shared Mk 2:7; Is 43:25
2 God the Son exercises this authority on earth Shared Mk 2:10 — “the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins”
3 Jesus delegates this authority to the Apostles Catholic Jn 20:21–23
4 The Apostles pass this authority to their successors Catholic Apostolic succession (Tradition; CCC §77, §861–862)
5 Priests act in persona Christi — in the person of Christ Catholic CCC §1548

Steps 1 and 2 are shared ground. The disagreement begins at step 3 and deepens at step 4. A Protestant who accepts steps 1–2 but rejects apostolic succession will naturally reject steps 3–5.

This authority chain is now formalized as a general theorem in Authority.lean. The absolution_authority theorem proves that the forgiveness authority chain follows the same pattern as exorcism — both are instances of authority_chain_general.

The crucial point: the priest does not forgive by his own power. The absolution formula is “I absolve you from your sins, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit.” The “I” is Christ speaking through the priest. The priest is a conduit — an instrument — not the source. This is the same two-tier causation model formalized as axiom P2: God (primary cause) acts through the priest (secondary cause) without the two competing.

The Axiom Dependencies

What does the Catholic position on priestly absolution actually require? Mapping it to the 15 base axioms, the answer is precise:

Axiom Role in the Argument Scope
T3 Sacramental efficacy — the sacrament of Reconciliation actually confers the forgiveness it signifies Catholic
P2 Two-tier causation — God acts through the priest without competition Shared
S5 Sin separates from God — grave sin breaks communion, which is why reconciliation is needed Shared
S8 Grace is transformative — absolution does not merely declare forgiveness; it restores the person to communion Catholic
Apostolic succession The authority chain from Christ to present-day priests is unbroken Catholic
The load-bearing axiom is T3 — sacramental efficacy. If sacraments actually confer what they signify (ex opere operato), then the sacrament of Reconciliation actually confers forgiveness. If sacraments are signs of grace received by faith (the Protestant reading), then no sacramental instrument is needed — God forgives directly upon repentance and faith.
Under Catholic axioms
God forgives sins through the sacrament He instituted for that purpose. The priest is the instrument; Christ is the agent. Repentance is necessary. The sacrament provides certainty — you hear the words of absolution and know you are forgiven.
Under Protestant axioms
God forgives sins directly when a person repents and believes. No human intermediary is needed. The believer trusts God’s promise of forgiveness (1 Jn 1:9: “If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive us”) and receives it by faith.

Where They Agree

Before listing the disagreements, it is worth pausing on how much common ground there is. It is more than most people on either side realize.

Fully Shared

  • God alone has the authority to forgive sins. No Catholic doctrine claims otherwise.
  • Repentance is necessary. The Catholic sacrament requires contrition — genuine sorrow for sin. No repentance, no forgiveness. Both agree.
  • The forgiven person is genuinely forgiven. This is not a legal fiction on either account. The person is truly restored.
  • Sin separates from God (S5). Both traditions take sin seriously.
  • Grace is necessary. Neither side claims humans can earn or manufacture forgiveness.

The disagreement, precisely stated

The disagreement is not about the source of forgiveness (God) or the condition for forgiveness (repentance) or the reality of forgiveness (genuine). It is about the mechanism: did Jesus establish a sacramental instrument for forgiveness, or does direct repentance to God suffice?

❅

The Charitable Conclusion

The Protestant asks: “Doesn’t God alone forgive sins?” And the Catholic answers: “Yes — through the instrument He established.”

The disagreement is about whether Jesus established a sacramental instrument for forgiveness (Catholic) or whether direct repentance to God suffices (Protestant). Both cite Scripture. Both reason coherently from their premises.

The difference reduces to the authority and sacramental efficacy axioms. If you accept apostolic succession and T3 (sacraments confer what they signify), priestly absolution follows logically. If you reject them, direct confession to God follows just as logically. The axiom set determines the answer.

Neither side is being foolish. The Protestant is guarding a true principle — that God alone forgives — and the Catholic is affirming the same principle while adding that God chose to exercise that forgiveness through a visible sign. Seeing where the premises diverge is more honest than pretending the other side has not read Scripture.
