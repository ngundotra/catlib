A proof assistant is a tool that checks reasoning the way a calculator checks arithmetic. It won't let you skip steps — it forces you to say out loud what you were assuming in silence.

We pointed one at the Catholic Catechism — not to attack it, but to see what it assumes. The findings surprised us.

This is a love letter to the Catholic intellectual tradition. The Church has 2,000 years of rigorous philosophical reasoning — Aquinas, Augustine, the Scholastics (medieval Catholic philosophers who systematized the faith). We are doing what they did — examining premises — with a tool they didn't have.

We are not debunking. We are not attacking. The tone is: "We took your arguments as seriously as you do. Here is what we found when we tried to write them down with total precision."

A worked example

1

The claim

There are acts which, in and of themselves, independently of circumstances and intentions, are always gravely illicit by reason of their object; such as blasphemy and perjury, murder and adultery." — Catechism of the Catholic Church
2

We tried to formalize it

The Catechism says morality depends on three things: what you do (the "object" — what the action fundamentally is), why you do it (the intention), and the circumstances. An act is good when all three are good. An act is evil when any one is bad.

So far, so clear. But then it claims some acts are always evil — no matter the intention, no matter the circumstances.

3

The assumption we were forced to state

Before the proof assistant would let us write the theorem, it asked a question the text doesn't answer in this passage:

Can the moral character of what an action fundamentally is be evaluated independently of its circumstances?

This three-part test doesn't require that. It says all three matter — but it's compatible with a world where what an action is depends on context. To get "intrinsically evil," you need an extra rule: the action has a fixed moral nature, regardless of anything else.

This is the core of Catholic moral philosophy — the idea that some acts are wrong in themselves, regardless of consequences. The broader tradition argues for it at length, but this passage assumes it without stating it. The proof assistant wouldn't let us do the same.

-- The Catechism's three-part test:
-- An act is good when object, intention, AND circumstances are all good.
def MoralEvaluation.isGood (eval : MoralEvaluation) : Prop :=
  eval.objectIsGood ∧ eval.intentionIsGood ∧ eval.circumstancesAppropriate

-- "Intrinsically evil" means the object is NEVER good.
-- This is an ADDITIONAL assumption — not derivable from the framework.
def IntrinsicallyEvil (objectPredicate : Prop) : Prop := ¬objectPredicate

-- Given that assumption, the conclusion follows easily.
-- The proof is trivial. The FINDING is that we needed the assumption.
theorem intrinsically_evil_always_evil
    (eval : MoralEvaluation)
    (h_intrinsic : IntrinsicallyEvil eval.objectIsGood) :
    ¬eval.isGood := by
  intro ⟨ho, _, _⟩
  exact h_intrinsic ho

You can stop here

The Catechism's claim about intrinsically evil acts works — but only if you accept an unstated assumption: that what an action is has a fixed moral character, independent of everything else. That's not a flaw. It's a philosophical commitment. The proof assistant just made it visible.

You need grace to prepare for grace

The claim: "The preparation of man for the reception of grace is already a work of grace."

The circularity: You need grace to prepare for grace. The proof assistant won't let you write a definition that requires itself.

This is a genuine bootstrapping problem — like needing a password to get the password. The resolution requires at least two distinct kinds of grace: one that God gives without any prior human action (theologians call it "prevenient grace"), and one that this first grace prepares you to receive (sanctifying grace). The Catechism uses "grace" as if it were one thing, but the formal structure demands two.

Two paragraphs compress an entire theological debate — whether humans can do good without God's help — that has been running since the 5th century. The proof assistant forced it all back open.

More findings

We've formalized twelve passages so far. Every one revealed assumptions the text didn't state.

The mortal sin system only works in binary

Mortal sin requires grave matter, full knowledge, and complete consent. But to make the mortal/venial distinction work, every property must be yes-or-no — no spectrum allowed. Knowledge is "full" or not. Consent is "complete" or not. A graded model would break the system. The Catechism relies on this binary structure without spelling it out.

Hell requires a specific model of free will

The Catechism calls hell "definitive self-exclusion from God." But "self-exclusion" only carries its full moral weight if you could genuinely have chosen otherwise — not just that you were uncoerced, but that real alternatives existed. This is a specific philosophical position on freedom that the text assumes without announcing. And the proof assistant asked a question the Catechism never answers: why does love require freedom?

The Trinity requires distinguishing two kinds of "same"

If Father = God and Son = God, then by normal logic, Father = Son. But the Catechism says they're distinct. The proof assistant refused to let us have it both ways. The resolution: sharing the same nature is not the same as being the same person. "Same God" and "same person" are two different claims. The Trinity requires keeping them separate — something standard logic doesn't do by default.

If people disagree about morality, someone is reasoning badly

The Catechism says natural law is universal, immutable, and accessible to reason. But if it's truly universal AND accessible to reason, then persistent moral disagreement must always be a failure of reason — never a genuine feature of moral complexity. That's an extraordinarily strong claim hidden in the word "universal." The text also creates a tension: natural law is discoverable by reason alone, but only has binding force because it comes from God.

You're wrong either way: the erring conscience paradox

The Catechism says you must always obey your conscience — but your conscience can be wrong. If it tells you to do something evil, you're wrong if you follow it (the act is evil) and wrong if you don't (you disobeyed conscience). The resolution creates a hidden asymmetry: acting against conscience is categorically worse than following an erring one. This is the unstated decision rule of Catholic moral thought.

God causes good but only permits evil

The Catechism says God "operates in and through" all creature actions — but is "in no way" the cause of moral evil. God's relationship to causation changes depending on whether the outcome is good or evil. This asymmetry requires evil to be an absence of good rather than a thing in itself — otherwise God would need to cause it. Six axioms in ten paragraphs, none stated.

Your soul is not what you think it is

Most people think the soul is a separate thing living inside the body — a ghost in a machine. The Catechism says something more radical: the soul is what makes your body a body. Without it, the matter isn't a corpse — it's not a body at all. This is a specific philosophical model (Aristotelian hylomorphism) that the Catechism adopts without naming as a philosophical commitment. It's incompatible with the mind-body dualism most modern people assume.

The freest being cannot sin

The Catechism says "the more one does what is good, the freer one becomes." Choosing evil is an "abuse of freedom" that leads to slavery. Taken to its conclusion: a being that can only choose good is the freest of all. God, who cannot sin, is perfectly free — not despite being unable to sin, but because of it. This directly contradicts what most people mean by "freedom."
