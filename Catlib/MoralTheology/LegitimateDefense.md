# CCC §2263–2267: Legitimate Defense

## The Catechism claim

Self-defense can be legitimate even if it results in the aggressor's
death (§2263-2264). Force must be proportionate (§2264). Defense can be
a duty for those responsible for others (§2265).

## What we found

### Two of four conditions are invisible

The double effect doctrine has four standard conditions. The Catechism
states only two (good intention, proportionality). The other two — the
act must not be intrinsically evil, and the bad effect must not be the
MEANS to the good — are assumed but never stated. The means/ends
condition is particularly important: without it, double effect would
permit intentional killing as long as it's proportionate.

### The epistemic gap

Proportionality assumes the defender can accurately assess the threat.
Real self-defense happens under fear, adrenaline, and imperfect
information. The Catechism never addresses what happens when perceived
threat diverges from actual threat.

### Hidden assumptions

1. **Four conditions, two unstated** — full double effect is compressed
2. **Epistemic access to threat** — proportionality assumes accurate
   assessment under crisis conditions
3. **Defense as duty** — positive obligation to use violence for those
   responsible for others
4. **Means/ends condition** — unstated but prevents collapsing defense
   into assassination

### Assessment

**Tier 3** — The epistemic gap and the unstated means/ends condition
are both genuine findings.
