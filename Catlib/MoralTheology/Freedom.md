# CCC §1730–1738: Freedom and Responsibility

## The Catechism claim

Freedom is "the power to act or not to act" (§1731). "The more one does
what is good, the freer one becomes" (§1733). Choosing evil "leads to
the slavery of sin" (§1733). Responsibility is proportional to freedom
(§1734).

## What we found

### Two incompatible concepts of freedom

The Catechism holds two concepts simultaneously without explaining their
relationship:

1. **Freedom-as-choice** (§1731): ability to choose between alternatives
2. **Freedom-as-flourishing** (§1733): power to do what is genuinely good

Under (1), choosing evil is an exercise OF freedom.
Under (2), choosing evil diminishes freedom.

### Perfect freedom = inability to sin

The most counterintuitive consequence: a being that CAN ONLY choose good
is MORE free than one that can choose good or evil. God (who cannot sin)
is the freest being — not despite being unable to sin, but because of it.

This directly contradicts the modern assumption that freedom means having
all options open.

### Hidden assumptions

1. **Teleological freedom** — freedom has a direction, oriented toward God
2. **Freedom is graded** — not binary, you become more/less free
3. **Evil is self-undermining** — choosing evil reduces capacity for good
4. **Perfect freedom = inability to sin** — counterintuitive core claim
5. **Proportional responsibility** — graded freedom = graded responsibility
6. **Two concepts held simultaneously** — choice and flourishing coexist

### Assessment

**Tier 3** — The perfect freedom paradox is the most counterintuitive
finding in the entire project. The Catechism's freedom is radically
different from what most modern readers assume.
