# CCC §1954–1957: The Natural Moral Law

## The Catechism claim

Natural law is "written in the soul of each and every man" and
discoverable "by reason" (§1954). It is "universal in its precepts,"
"immutable and eternal" (§1956). Yet "application of the natural law
varies greatly" by place, time, and circumstances (§1957).

## How we modeled it

- **MoralPrecept**: The abstract, immutable content of a natural law
  principle (with properties: accessible to reason, universal)
- **MoralContext**: Place, time, and circumstances
- **Application**: A precept applied to a specific context, yielding a
  concrete judgment
- Five axioms: moral realism, rational accessibility, universality,
  law/application distinction, divine grounding

## What we found

### The rational convergence assumption

If natural law is universal AND accessible to reason, then all honest
reasoners should converge on the same moral conclusions. If two people
disagree about a moral precept, at least one must be reasoning badly.
This is an extraordinarily strong claim hidden in the word "universal."

The Catechism assumes that persistent moral disagreement is always a
failure of reason, never a genuine feature of moral complexity. The
proof assistant forced us to state this explicitly.

### The divine grounding tension

§1954 says natural law is both:
- Accessible to reason alone (no revelation needed)
- Only binding because it's "the voice of a higher reason" (God)

So an atheist can discover the natural law but cannot account for why
it binds them. Natural law is "natural" in that you can find it without
Scripture, but it requires theism to have the force of law. The
Catechism doesn't resolve this tension.

### The immutability problem

If you can only observe the law through its applications, and the
applications vary greatly, what does "immutable" mean? The law/application
distinction requires Platonic realism — the abstract precept exists
independently of all its concrete instances. This is a major metaphysical
commitment the text leaves implicit.

### Hidden assumptions

1. **Moral realism** — moral facts exist independently of human opinion
2. **Rational accessibility** — reason alone discovers moral truth
3. **Rational convergence** — disagreement = failure of reason
4. **Law/application distinction** — Platonic realism about moral laws
5. **Divine grounding** — law only binding because it comes from God

### Assessment

**Tier 3** — Five hidden premises in four paragraphs. The rational
convergence assumption and the divine grounding tension were the most
surprising findings.
