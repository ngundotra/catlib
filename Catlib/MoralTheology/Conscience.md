# CCC §1776–1791: Moral Conscience

## The Catechism claim

Conscience is "a judgment of reason" (§1778). You must always obey it
(§1790). But conscience can err (§1790). If the error is your fault,
you're culpable (§1791).

## The paradox

If your conscience erroneously tells you to do something evil:
- You're wrong if you follow it (the act is objectively evil)
- You're wrong if you don't follow it (you must obey conscience)

## What we found

### The culpability asymmetry

The resolution requires a hidden asymmetry: acting AGAINST conscience
is categorically worse than following an erring conscience. There are
no exceptions to the first; the second depends on whether the error
was your fault. This asymmetry is unstated but load-bearing.

### Hidden assumptions

1. **Two-level moral evaluation** — act-goodness and agent-culpability
   can diverge (you can do evil without being guilty)
2. **Culpability asymmetry** — acting against conscience is always
   worse than following an erring one
3. **Binary ignorance** — culpable or innocent, no spectrum
4. **Duty to inquire** — you're obligated to form conscience properly
   (without this, all ignorance would be innocent)
5. **Conscience as reason** — not feeling or intuition

### Assessment

**Tier 3** — The culpability asymmetry was genuinely surprising.
