# CCC §355–365: The Soul as "Form" of the Body

## The Catechism claim

"The unity of soul and body is so profound that one has to consider the
soul to be the 'form' of the body" (§365). "Man, though made of body
and soul, is a unity" (§364). "Spirit and matter, in man, are not two
natures united, but rather their union forms a single nature" (§365).

## What we found

### The Catechism's anthropology is not what most people think

Most people (including most Catholics) assume soul/body = mind/body:
two separate things joined together (Cartesian dualism). The Catechism
says something much more radical: the soul is the FORM of the body —
what makes matter into a living human body. Without the soul, the matter
is not a dead body — it's not a body at all.

This is Aristotelian hylomorphism, a specific philosophical model the
Catechism adopts without naming as a philosophical commitment.

### Hidden assumptions

1. **Hylomorphism** — the soul-as-form model from Aristotle/Aquinas,
   adopted without naming it as philosophical
2. **Form is a real principle** — not metaphor, not emergence, not
   pattern-talk
3. **Anti-dualism** — explicitly denies soul-in-body model
4. **Anti-materialism** — explicitly denies body-is-all model
5. **Soul hierarchy** — human souls categorically differ from animal
   souls (only humans can know God)
6. **Non-standard composition** — the soul is not a PART of you but
   the PRINCIPLE of you

### Assessment

**Tier 3** — The incompatibility with common-sense dualism is a genuine
surprise. The Catechism's anthropology is far more philosophically
committed than most readers realize.
