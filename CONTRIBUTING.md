**Metaphysical and Christological foundations**

- [x] **Theological grounds for scientific inquiry** — Formalized in `Creed/ScientificInquiry.lean`. Derives two propositions: (A) the world is logical (from §299 + §306 + P2) and (B) humans can understand that logic (from §1700/imago_dei + §299 + §36). Key finding: P2 (two-tier causation) and imago_dei are equally load-bearing — drop P2 and you get occasionalism, drop imago_dei and you have no reason to trust reason. - [ ] **Define what "the world is logical" means** — `worldIsLogical` in `ScientificInquiry.lean` is currently opaque. The CCC grounds science metaphysically but never commits to a specific philosophy of science. Three candidate definitions, each progressively stronger: (1) **Regularity** — same causes produce same effects. Weakest; even an occasionalist can observe regularities, they just ground them in God's habits. (2) **Essentialism** — things have stable natures with real causal powers. Fire burns BECAUSE OF WHAT FIRE IS. This is Aristotelian and aligns with P2/secondary causation. (3) **Mathematical describability** — the world's order is expressible in mathematical language (Galileo's "book of nature is written in mathematics"; Wigner's "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics"). CCC §299 ("measure and number and weight") leans toward this but doesn't commit. Main question: does the CCC's argument require a specific account of natural order, or does it work with any of the three? If it works with all three, that's itself a finding — the metaphysical grounding is more general than the specific philosophy of science.

- [ ] **Define what "fittingness" means** — The argument from fittingness (*convenientia*) is central to several Catholic doctrines (IC, Assumption, Incarnation) but we don't understand it well enough to formalize. Research agenda: (1) **Scotus** (*potuit, decuit, fecit*) — seems to treat fittingness + omnipotence as sufficient for actuality. But why? What distinguishes "fitting" from "merely nice"? Is there a threshold? Scotus's *Ordinatio* III, d.3, q.1 is the primary source. (2) **Aquinas** (ST III, q.1, a.1-2) — argues the Incarnation was "fitting" but not strictly necessary. His notion of *conveniens* is weaker than Scotus's: fitting means "harmonious with God's wisdom," not "therefore actual." Does Aquinas's version support the IC or not? (3) **Bonaventure** — uses fittingness differently again, more Platonically (beauty as participation in divine beauty). (4) **CCC usage** — §491 uses "singular grace and privilege" language but doesn't invoke fittingness explicitly. How much is the fittingness argument load-bearing vs. merely rhetorical in the actual definition? (5) **The Protestant objection** — God COULD do many fitting things He hasn't done (fitting ≠ actual). What additional premise closes the gap? Is it papal authority (the Magisterium declares it actual)? Or is fittingness itself sufficient under a certain theology of divine action? (6) **Modern Catholic theology** — has the fittingness principle been refined since Scotus? Does it function as a probability argument, an aesthetic argument, or a metaphysical argument? Main question: is fittingness a formal inference rule (can be axiomatized) or an irreducible judgment call (should stay opaque)?

Answer to the main question: "Logos" functions as BOTH a metaphysical principle and a Christological identity claim — and the "both" is the point. - [x] **Why should truth be intelligible at all?** — Formalized in `Creed/Intelligibility.lean`. Logos alone gives intelligibility but not mechanism (WHY can we grasp the order?). Realism alone gives mechanism but not ground (WHY is being intelligible?). Together: being is intelligible (realism) BECAUSE it comes from Logos (theology), and the mind fits the world BECAUSE both come from the same rational source. Answer to the main question: BOTH — and the "both" is the point. The *adaequatio rei et intellectus* has a theological infrastructure: both res and intellectus are expressions of the same Logos. Cross-file connections to ScientificInquiry.lean (worldIsLogical, humansCanUnderstandIt as downstream effects), Logos.lean (groundsIntelligibility, wisdomIsPersonal as theological ground), NaturalLaw.lean (moral_realism as instance of the same intelligibility), and Axioms.lean (P2, S6). - [x] **Why is the Cross necessary if God can simply forgive?** — Formalized in `Creed/Atonement.lean`. Eight axioms model the satisfaction theory of atonement (CCC §599-618, Aquinas ST III q.46-49). - [x] **Heresy as axiom violation** — Formalized in `Creed/Heresies.lean`. - [x] **Why does evil permission not collapse into evil causation?** — Formalized in `Creed/Providence.lean` as the `permission_does_not_collapse_into_causation` theorem. The CCC's answer: ALL FOUR, distributed across the seven sacraments with different emphasis per sacrament. Key findings: (1) Every sacrament causes at least one type of real change (`every_sacrament_causes_change`) — none is merely symbolic under T3. (2) Character ↔ non-repeatability: the three character sacraments (baptism, confirmation, holy orders) are exactly the non-repeatable ones (`character_iff_not_repeatable`). (3) The sin-layer mapping is complete: baptism → Layer 1 (original wound), reconciliation → Layer 2 (guilt), Eucharist → Layer 3 (attachments) — every layer has a sacramental remedy (`all_sin_layers_have_sacramental_remedy`). (4) Baptism has the richest causation profile (three types: ontological + relational + juridical), matching its status as the "gateway sacrament" (§1213). (5) The entire causation question DISSOLVES under Protestant axioms — if T3 is rejected, sacraments signify but do not cause. 22 axioms, 17 theorems, connections to SinEffects.lean (SinEffect) and Axioms.lean (Sacrament, confers). Hidden assumption: the four change-types are real categories (the CCC uses all four modes of language without labeling them as a taxonomy).

- [x] **Why does confession to a priest make sense if God alone forgives sins?** — Formalized in `Sacraments/PriestlyAbsolution.lean`. Six axioms model the CCC's three-part answer (§1441-1467): (1) delegated authority (Jn 20:23) explains WHO can absolve, (2) T3 sacramental efficacy explains HOW absolution works (effective sign, not mere declaration), (3) ecclesial visibility (§1443-1445) explains WHY visible confession matters (sin wounds the Church, not just the God-person relationship). Key finding: all three steps are genuinely load-bearing but for DIFFERENT things — authority is necessary but not sufficient, T3 is the mechanism, ecclesial visibility is the purpose. The Protestant position is a THREE-LEVEL rejection (scope of Jn 20:23, T3, ecclesial mediation), not a single disagreement. P2 does quintuple duty across the project: causation, prayer, intercession, evil permission, and now absolution. The deepest hidden assumption: instrumental causes can genuinely effect what only the principal cause has the power to do (Aquinas, ST III q.64 a.1).

- [x] **Why pray if God already knows and wills the good?** — Formalized in `Creed/Prayer.lean`. - [x] **Why ask saints to pray for us if Christ is the one mediator?** — Formalized in `Creed/Intercession.lean`. Key finding: P2 does quadruple duty across the project (causation, prayer, absolution, intercession). The Protestant position is precisely that P2 doesn't extend to mediation.

- [x] **What is the exact difference between veneration and worship?** — Formalized in `MoralTheology/Veneration.lean`. - [x] **Infant Baptism** — Formalized in `Sacraments/Baptism.lean`. **Authority, canon, and doctrinal development**

- [x] **Why can't private judgment settle doctrine by itself?** — Formalized in `Creed/PrivateJudgment.lean`. Four axioms (sincere_disagreement, no_private_adjudicator, magisterium_established, magisterium_can_adjudicate) derive the core result: private judgment is insufficient as the ultimate rule of faith because it produces disagreements it cannot resolve. Key finding: the decisive problem is the ABSENCE OF A PRINCIPLED ADJUDICATOR, not epistemic disagreement alone. Disagreement is the evidence; the lack of a resolver is the structural defect. Connection to NaturalLaw.lean: rational_convergence fails for interpretation, meaning interpretation requires more than reason applied to text. The Protestant perspicuity defense is internally consistent but SHIFTS the problem from "which interpretation is correct?" to "which teachings are essential?" — and that boundary question cannot be answered by private judgment without circularity. Combined with RuleOfFaith.lean: sola scriptura fails on BOTH content (materially incomplete) and interpretation (no principled adjudicator).

- [x] **Scripture and Tradition / rule of faith** — Formalized in `Creed/RuleOfFaith.lean`. Four axioms (dual_transmission, not_everything_written, canon_depends_on_church, canon_act_not_in_scripture) derive two convergent arguments: (1) the Tradition argument — Scripture is materially incomplete because some apostolic teaching was never written (§76); (2) the canon argument — Scripture's own authority presupposes a non-scriptural authority because the Church determined the canon (§120). Key finding: the canon argument IS load-bearing, but the DEEPER issue is whether authoritative unwritten transmission can exist at all (`not_everything_written`). The Protestant position (sola scriptura + self-authentication) is internally consistent but requires different axioms. The debate cannot be settled by logic — it is a disagreement about which axioms are true.

- [x] **Nicaea, conciliar authority, and the biblical canon** — Formalized in `Creed/ConciliarAuthority.lean`. Key result: `three_arguments_converge` — three independent anti-sola-scriptura arguments hold simultaneously: (1) Scripture is materially incomplete (from RuleOfFaith.lean), (2) binding doctrine preceded the closed canon (temporal argument, new), (3) conciliar authority is grounded in non-scriptural succession (structural argument, new). Answer to the main question: the COMBINATION is decisive, not any single argument — each alone has a plausible Protestant counter, but the counters pull in different directions. The Protestant ministerial-authority response is internally consistent under different axioms but is formally incompatible with the Catholic succession axioms (`protestant_ministerial_contradicts_succession_axiom`). Deepest hidden assumption: whether conciliar authority is constitutive (creates binding doctrine) or recognitive (discovers pre-existing truth).

### Priority 6: Body & Soul open questions (from HumanNature formalization)

- [ ] **What is a separated soul's experience?** — After death, the soul exists without the body (§366). The CCC implies awareness (purgatory and hell involve suffering/purification). But what can a soul DO without a body? Can it think? Will? The CCC doesn't specify the mechanism. Connects to P1 (hylomorphism): if the soul is the form of the body, what does "form without matter" mean?

- [ ] **What does resurrection ADD?** — Saints in heaven are already in full communion with God (§1023). But they're INCOMPLETE persons — soul without body. Resurrection restores the body (§997). What does completeness add to someone already in beatific vision? Is it experiential (embodied joy)? Ontological (they become fully what they are)? The CCC says resurrection is essential but doesn't explain what the soul was MISSING.

- [x] **Can a separated soul think and will?** — Formalized in `Creed/SeparatedSoul.lean`. YES — intellect and will are powers of the SOUL (§1705), so they persist through death (§366). But the MODE of cognition changes: embodied cognition (through senses) in life, separated cognition (without sensory data) after death. Key finding: the CCC requires MORE than minimal hylomorphism — it needs a SUBSISTENT soul (Aquinas, ST I q.75 a.2) that can operate without the body. The conjunction of hylomorphism (§365) + immortality (§366) + conscious afterlife states (§1023, §1030-1037) logically requires subsistent hylomorphism. `ccc_requires_subsistent_hylomorphism` shows all three properties must hold simultaneously. Soul sleep (psychopannychia) is formally incompatible with all three afterlife states. 4 axioms, 11 theorems, 1 new type (CognitionMode), 2 opaques (isExercisingPowers, cognitionModeOf). Cross-file connections to Soul.lean (soul_is_immortal, death_separates, separated_soul_is_incomplete) and DivineModes.lean (personInHeaven, personInPurgatory, personInHell). Hidden assumptions: (1) spiritual powers persist because they belong to the soul, not the body, (2) the soul is subsistent — it can operate on its own, (3) divine illumination compensates for lost sensory input (Aquinas ST I q.89, CCC does not specify mechanism).

- [x] **How does soul "form" matter into body?** — Formalized in `Creed/HylomorphicFormation.lean`. Five axioms model the Aristotelian hylomorphic formation that §365 borrows: (1) soul forms matter into living body (§365), (2) living body requires soul-animation (§364), (3) departure of soul causes decay (§997), (4) body's dignity derives from soul (§364), (5) soul created immediately by God (§366). Key finding: the formation model provides EXPLANATORY DEPTH for three things Soul.lean asserts but doesn't explain — WHY `corporeal_requires_spiritual` holds (the soul is what MAKES matter into body), WHY the body decays at death (the form departs, so the matter loses organization), and WHY the body shares in the image of God (its dignity is derived from the soul's animation). The capstone theorem `complete_hylomorphic_asymmetry` connects to SeparatedSoul.lean: the soul can subsist without matter (SeparatedSoul) but matter cannot be a body without the soul (this file) — form is metaphysically prior to matter. The key opaque `isInformedBy` is honestly opaque: the CCC says the soul "forms" matter but does not explain the mechanism. Aquinas gives the most detailed account (ST I q.76) but even he grounds it ultimately in God's creative act. 5 axioms, 11 theorems, 6 opaques. Cross-file connections to Soul.lean (corporeal_requires_spiritual, death_separates, soul_is_immortal) and SeparatedSoul.lean (separated_soul_can_think_and_will). Hidden assumptions: matter is pure potentiality without form, the soul is substantial form (not accidental), decay follows from loss of form. All are Aristotelian/Thomistic philosophical infrastructure the CCC assumes.

### Priority 7: New topics from CCC research (2026-03-23)

- [x] **Cardinal and Theological Virtues** — Formalized in `MoralTheology/Virtues.lean`. Seven axioms model the CCC's two-category virtue system (§1803-1845): cardinal virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) are naturally acquirable by human effort (§1810), while theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) require divine infusion (§1813). Key finding: the cardinal/theological distinction does NOT cleanly map onto natural/supernatural. §1811 says even cardinal virtues need grace for PERSEVERANCE after the Fall — so the distinction is about ORIGIN (how you get the virtue), not about independence from grace. This creates a precise three-position denominational spectrum: Pelagianism (nature suffices for all virtue — condemned), Catholic (cardinal acquirable by nature, need grace for perseverance), total depravity/Calvin (no virtue without grace). Both extremes are formally excluded under Catholic axioms: `catholic_excludes_pelagianism` (A4+A7 contradict grace-free perseverance) and `catholic_differs_from_total_depravity` (A1 asserts natural acquirability). Charity = agape (Love.lean bridge via A6); virtue connects to teleological freedom (Freedom.lean bridge). 7 axioms, 8 theorems, 6 opaques. Cross-file connections to Love.lean (TypedLove, LoveKind), Freedom.lean (good_increases_freedom), Axioms.lean (graceGiven, S8). Hidden assumptions: (1) the natural/supernatural distinction is sharp enough to classify acquisition modes, (2) "informing" (charity as form of virtues) is a real causal relationship not just rhetorical, (3) universal woundedness from original sin applies to all moral agents.

- [x] **The Beatitudes as Moral Program** — Formalized in `MoralTheology/Beatitudes.lean`. Seven axioms model the CCC's Beatitude doctrine (§1716-1729): (1) natural desire for happiness is universal (§1718), (2) only God satisfies that desire (§1718+§1723), (3) the Beatitudes are the charter of Christian life — not optional (§1717), (4) the paradoxical inversion — deprivation is blessed through Christ's Passion (§1717), (5) the Beatitudes create eschatological tension (§1723), (6) the Beatitudes respond to the natural desire (§1718), (7) charity informs the Beatitudes (§1717+§1827). Key finding: the Beatitudes are PARTIALLY DERIVABLE from the existing axiom base. The moral content (charity requirement, freedom increase, grace dependence for perseverance) follows from Virtues.lean + Freedom.lean + Soteriology.lean. But the PARADOXICAL INVERSION (suffering = blessed through Christ's Passion) is genuinely NEW content not derivable from virtue theory — virtue theory says virtue leads to flourishing, not that suffering IS flourishing. This inversion requires specifically Christological grounding (§1717: "associated with the glory of his Passion"). The anthropological foundation (A1+A2: natural desire → directed to God → salvation) is also new — it narrows S7's "the good" to specifically GOD. The eschatological tension (already/not-yet) adds temporal modality absent from the static virtue framework. Assessment: the CCC's moral teaching is MORE UNIFIED than its presentation suggests, but the Beatitudes add Christological content that transforms virtue theory. 7 axioms, 9 theorems, 6 opaques. Cross-file connections to Virtues.lean (charity_is_agape, grace_needed_for_perseverance), Freedom.lean (good_increases_freedom), Soteriology.lean (step8_perseverance_needed, inStateOfSalvation), Love.lean (TypedLove, LoveKind). Hidden assumptions: (1) natural desire for happiness is universal and ineradicable, (2) only God can satisfy it, (3) suffering can be redemptive through Christ, (4) the Beatitudes are binding not advisory.

- [x] **Particular Judgment and the Two-Judgment Structure** — Formalized in `Creed/Judgment.lean`. Four axioms model the CCC's two-judgment structure (§1021-1022, §1038-1041). Key result: `two_judgments_are_non_redundant` — the Last Judgment adds THREE things that particular judgment cannot provide, each addressing a distinct deficiency: (1) PUBLIC VINDICATION — particular judgment is not publicly manifest (§1039 implies prior hiddenness); the Last Judgment makes justice visible to all creation. (2) BODILY JUDGMENT — particular judgment judges the separated soul (an incomplete person under hylomorphism §365); the Last Judgment judges the risen person (the complete body-soul composite). (3) COSMIC COMPLETION — particular judgment settles individual stories; the Last Judgment reveals the meaning of history as a whole (§1040: "God's justice triumphs over ALL the injustices"). The Last Judgment does not CHANGE any outcome — it COMPLETES the act of judgment along three independent axes: epistemic (who sees), anthropological (what the subject is), and historical (what the whole means). 4 axioms, 10 theorems, 2 opaques (`justicePubliclyManifest`, `revealsCosmicMeaning` — both honestly opaque). Cross-file connections to Soul.lean (`separated_soul_is_incomplete`, `resurrection_reunites`, `isCompletePerson`), DivineModes.lean (`personInHeaven`, `personInPurgatory`, `personInHell`). Hidden assumption: there is a meaningful distinction between justice enacted and justice publicly witnessed — the CCC implies this (§1039 "laid bare") but never articulates it as a principle.

- [x] **The Holy Spirit's Personal Identity and Mission** — Formalized in `Creed/HolySpirit.lean`. Seven axioms model CCC §683-747 and §246-248. Key findings: (1) The Son/Spirit mission distinction (Son reveals, Spirit sanctifies) is REAL but mediated through APPROPRIATION (§258) — all three persons act inseparably in every divine operation, and we attribute sanctification to the Spirit by reason of the Spirit's relational character (procession). Without appropriation, the distinction either collapses (same operation) or becomes tritheistic (genuinely separate operations). (2) The filioque is a GENUINE axiom difference with a POSSIBLE reconciliation: `both_procession_formulas` shows the CCC affirms BOTH "from Father and Son" (Western, §246) AND "from Father through Son" (Eastern, §248) simultaneously. (3) The Spirit's grace-giving (`spirit_gives_grace`) connects to Grace.lean's prevenient grace analysis, and `grace_giving_inseparable` shows that even grace-giving appropriated to the Spirit is inseparable (uses Axiom 4, mission_is_appropriation). 7 axioms, 7 theorems, 4 opaques. - [x] **Anointing of the Sick — Grace at the Boundary of Death** — Formalized in `Sacraments/Anointing.lean`. Key finding: the "if conducive" clause makes Anointing UNIQUE among the seven sacraments — it is the ONLY sacrament whose primary effects include one conditioned on divine judgment about the recipient's good (`anointing_uniquely_conditional`, biconditional across all seven). The sacrament's certain spiritual effects are ALWAYS conferred (`anointing_always_achieves_spiritual_good`); the conditional physical effect ADAPTS to the person's situation. When death leads to beatifying communion, the sacrament pivots from healing to preparation for death (`anointing_pivots_at_deaths_door`). The exhaustive coverage theorem shows the sacrament always acts — healing OR preparation for death, never neither (`anointing_exhaustive_coverage`). Deepest hidden assumption: `healing_salvation_tension` — physical healing and spiritual salvation CAN be in tension, giving the "if conducive" clause non-vacuous content. Without it, Anointing would simply always heal. Cross-file connections to SacramentalCausation.lean (dispositional + relational change types), DivineModes.lean (SoulState, beatifying communion as explanation for why death can be good). 12 axioms, 10 theorems, 9 opaques. Answer to the main question: YES — the conditional clause makes Anointing fundamentally different. Other sacraments have FIXED targets; Anointing has an ADAPTIVE target that encodes the priority of spiritual over temporal goods.

- [x] **Truth and Lying — The Eighth Commandment's Hidden Metaphysics** — Formalized in `MoralTheology/TruthAndLying.lean`. Five axioms model the CCC's doctrine on lying (§2464-2513): (1) lying is intrinsically evil — the moral object of a lie is always bad (§2483), (2) gravity depends on circumstances — harm to reputation makes a lie grave (§2484), (3) reputation harm makes speech acts evil regardless of truth value (§2477), (4) no obligation to disclose without right to know (§2489), (5) professional secrecy limits the right to know (§2490). Key finding: the three-source framework IS sufficient for lying — no fourth element needed. The two-element definition of lying (falsehood + deceptive intent, §2482) IS the three-source framework applied to speech acts: falsehood = bad object, deceptive intent = bad intention. False statements without deceptive intent (jokes, fiction) are NOT lies because they lack the intention element. True statements CAN be morally wrong (detraction, calumny) because circumstances still matter. The hidden assumption is the RIGHT TO TRUTH: the CCC distinguishes lying (always wrong) from legitimate non-disclosure (sometimes right) via the premise that not everyone has a right to know everything (§2489). Withholding truth is NOT lying — the CCC explicitly distinguishes them. 5 axioms, 9 theorems, connections to SourcesOfMorality.lean (IntrinsicallyEvil, MoralEvaluation) and Axioms.lean (obligated). Mostly ECUMENICAL; mental reservation doctrine is Catholic distinctive. Hidden assumptions: (1) deceptive intent is separable from content, (2) the right-to-truth boundary is knowable.

### Priority 8: Deeper CCC topics

- [x] **First Commandment — Idolatry, Superstition, and Atheism** — Formalized in `MoralTheology/FirstCommandment.lean`. Six axioms model the three violations of the First Commandment: idolatry (treating non-God as ultimate, §2112-2114), superstition (attributing independent causal power to practices, §2110-2111, violates P2), and atheism (denying what reason can know, §36/§2128). Key result: `practical_atheism_reduces_to_idolatry` — a practical atheist (one who lives as if God doesn't exist) necessarily has an idol, because (1) every moral agent has an ultimate concern (§2113-2114, Tillich), (2) the practical atheist's ultimate concern is not God, (3) by the uniqueness of God (Veneration.lean), nothing else has ultimate status, (4) therefore their ultimate concern is directed at a non-God entity = idolatry. The reduction requires a hidden assumption: `everyone_has_ultimate_concern` — no human life is truly centerless (Aquinas ST I-II q.1 a.4). Theoretical atheism does NOT automatically reduce to idolatry — the reduction requires the practical form. Superstition is the causal-order analogue of idolatry's honor-order error: idolatry treats secondary as primary in the ORDER OF HONOR, superstition treats secondary as primary in the ORDER OF CAUSATION. Both are P2 violations at different levels. 6 axioms, 4 theorems + 3 bridges, connections to Veneration.lean (hasUltimateStatus, god_has_ultimate_status), Axioms.lean (P2, S6), NaturalLaw.lean (moral realism). ### Priority 9: Aquinas and Augustine (primary sources)

- [ ] **[RESEARCH] Aquinas: The Five Ways (ST I q.2 a.3)** — The five proofs of God's existence are the most famous arguments in natural theology: (1) motion → unmoved mover, (2) efficient causation → first cause, (3) contingency → necessary being, (4) degrees of perfection → maximum being, (5) teleology → intelligent designer. The CCC acknowledges proofs of God's existence (§31-35) but does not reproduce the Five Ways. What we'd need: each Way assumes specific metaphysical premises (e.g., the First Way assumes that an infinite regress of movers is impossible). Formalizing them would mean making these premises explicit as axioms and checking whether the conclusions follow. Key sources: ST I q.2 a.3, CCC §31-35, Edward Feser's *Aquinas* (2009) for modern reconstruction. Open question: do the Five Ways prove the existence of the SAME being (the God of classical theism), or could they each point to a different entity? - [ ] **[RESEARCH] Augustine: Original Sin Theory (De peccatorum meritis, Contra Julianum)** — Our OriginalSin.lean formalizes the CCC's version, but Augustine's theory is the historical source and is STRONGER than what the CCC states. Augustine argues that original sin is transmitted through biological generation (concupiscence in the act of procreation), that unbaptized infants who die are condemned (though to the mildest punishment), and that the entire human race is a "mass of damnation" (*massa damnata*). The CCC softens each of these: it does not specify the transmission mechanism (§404 says "by propagation, not by imitation" but avoids concupiscence-in-generation), it expresses hope for unbaptized infants (§1261), and it does not use *massa damnata* language. What we'd need: a formalization of Augustine's FULL position alongside the CCC's softened version, showing exactly which Augustinian claims the CCC retains, which it weakens, and which it drops. This would be a case study in development of doctrine. Key sources: De peccatorum meritis I.9-16, Contra Julianum, CCC §396-409. Open question: is the CCC's softening of Augustine a legitimate development or a departure?

- [ ] **[RESEARCH] Augustine: Grace and Free Will (De gratia et libero arbitrio, De correptione et gratia)** — The Pelagian controversy produced Augustine's most influential theology. Key claims: (1) after the Fall, humans CANNOT choose good without prevenient grace (contra Pelagius), (2) grace is irresistible in the elect (gratia efficax), (3) God predestines some to salvation and passes over others (double predestination in a weak sense). The CCC inherits (1) fully (§1996-2000), weakens (2) by insisting grace preserves freedom (T2, §1993), and avoids (3) almost entirely (§600 touches predestination but only of Christ). Our Freedom.lean and Soteriology.lean formalize the CCC's position. What we'd need: Augustine's stronger claims formalized as alternative axiom sets, showing where T2 (grace preserves freedom) conflicts with Augustine's gratia efficax, and where the CCC's silence on predestination is a deliberate theological choice. Key sources: De gratia et libero arbitrio, De correptione et gratia, Council of Orange (529), Council of Trent Session 6. Open question: is T2 compatible with Augustine, or does it represent a post-Augustinian correction? The Jansenist controversy (17th century) turned precisely on this question.

- [ ] **[RESEARCH] Augustine: City of God — Theology of History** — De Civitate Dei presents history as a cosmic drama between two "cities": the City of God (those who love God above self) and the City of Man (those who love self above God). The CCC echoes this in §769 (the Church is "the seed and beginning of the Kingdom") and §2796 (the Church as pilgrim). But the CCC does not reproduce Augustine's full framework. What we'd need: (1) a formalization of the two-city distinction and whether it maps onto existing structures (heaven/hell in DivineModes.lean? Church/world in ecclesiology?), (2) Augustine's theology of history as LINEAR (creation → fall → redemption → consummation) vs. cyclical — the CCC assumes this linearity (§280-281, §668-682) without defending it, (3) the relationship between the City of God and the visible Church — Augustine says they overlap but are NOT identical. Key sources: De Civitate Dei, Books XI-XXII, CCC §668-682, §769. Open question: can the two-city framework be formalized as a partition of moral agents, and does it collapse into the heaven/hell partition or remain genuinely distinct?

- [ ] **[RESEARCH] Augustine: Time and Eternity (Confessions XI)** — Augustine's analysis of time in Confessions XI is the most influential pre-modern treatment. Key insight: time is a "distension of the soul" (*distentio animi*, XI.26) — the present is experienced through attention, the past through memory, the future through expectation. God exists in an "eternal present" where all times are simultaneously present. The CCC assumes this framework without citing it: divine foreknowledge (DivineAttributes.lean) requires that God sees all times at once, and the liturgy's "making present" of past events (§1085) relies on God's eternal NOW. What we'd need: a formal model of the relationship between temporal experience (human) and eternal present (divine), showing how foreknowledge is compatible with temporal freedom. This connects directly to the foreknowledge-freedom gap in ArgumentGaps.lean and the mystery of providence. Key sources: Confessions XI, Boethius Consolation of Philosophy V (the mediating text), CCC §600, §1085. Open question: does Augustine's model of divine eternity actually SOLVE the foreknowledge-freedom problem, or does it merely restate it in different terms?

- [x] **Aquinas: The Real Distinction Between Essence and Existence (De Ente et Essentia)** — Formalized in `Creed/EssenceExistence.lean`. Ten axioms model Aquinas's most foundational metaphysical thesis (De Ente ch. 4-5; ST I q.3 a.4; CCC §213, §271, §290-301): in every creature, essence ≠ existence (the real distinction); in God alone, essence = existence (*ipsum esse subsistens*). (2) `god_unique_essence_existence_identity` — only divine beings have essence = existence; if any being's essence entails its existence, that being must be divine (not a creature). (3) `creatures_need_sustaining` — the real distinction explains WHY creatures need continuous divine sustaining: their essences don't include existence, so existence must be continuously given. (4) `divine_simplicity_from_essence_existence` — divine simplicity is a CONSEQUENCE of the essence/existence identity; if God's essence IS existence, there is no composition in God. (5) `total_creator_creature_distinction` — every being falls on exactly one side: creature (essence ≠ existence) or divine (essence = existence), with no overlap (`creature_and_divine_exclusive`). - [x] **Aquinas: Transubstantiation (ST III q.75-77)** — Formalized in `Sacraments/TransubstantiationDepth.lean`. ## How to contribute

### Formalization design guidance

These are distilled from Lean Zulip threads and related community guidance. The
main lesson is that good formalization quality is decided mostly upstream of the
proof script.

1. Prefer reusable objects plus API lemmas over giant existential theorem
   statements when the concept will be used downstream. If the real thing you
   want is a stable concept, define the concept first and prove properties
   separately.
2. Choose representations that match actual use. If we keep treating something
   as a predicate, model it as a predicate; if we keep using it as a structure,
   model it as a structure.
3. Do not keep duplicate formalizations just because they are rhetorically
   different. If two proof paths matter, they should usually differ by
   generalization, abstraction, or axiom set, not duplicated theorem clutter.
4. Decide deliberately when paired notions both deserve first-class API and when
   one should be canonical with bridge lemmas. Examples in Catlib include
   doctrine vs discipline, direct claim vs formal object, and hidden assumption
   vs modeling choice.
5. Abstract only when the repeated pattern is real and stable. Avoid "grand
   unified" abstractions that erase useful specifics or make files harder to
   use.
6. Put naming and modeling conventions into docs before doing sweeping cleanup
   passes. Drifted local style changes are worse than an imperfect but explicit
   standard.
7. Treat style, naming, visibility, and documentation as part of formalization
   quality, not as cosmetic polish after the fact.
8. Generalize when it reveals genuine reusable structure, not just to look
   sophisticated. But do not assume the narrower statement is "obviously
   enough" just because it matches the current article or use case.

See the private notes file [specs/lean-zulip-formalization-notes.md](specs/lean-zulip-formalization-notes.md)
for the fuller thread map and source links.

### Formalizations

1. Pick a CCC paragraph from CATECHISM_CLAIMS.md (or propose a new one)
2. Write your prediction BEFORE formalizing (what do you expect to find?)
3. Formalize in Lean 4 (no Mathlib dependency)
4. Write a companion `.md` file with the finding
5. Tag every axiom with Provenance and DenominationalTag
6. Run `./tools/theorem-tree --flow YourFile.lean` to verify the dependency structure
7. Note whether each finding is a "hidden assumption in the CCC" or a "modeling choice we made"

### Articles

1. Read `.claude/skills/write-catlib-article.md` for the template
2. Reference existing theorems (don't inline Lean code)
3. Tag everything denominationally
4. Show where traditions AGREE before showing disagreements
5. Be maximally loving and truthful

### Tone

This is a love letter. Not a takedown, not a debate tool. Every article should pass this test: would a practicing Catholic feel respected? Would a Protestant feel their position is fairly represented? If not, revise.
